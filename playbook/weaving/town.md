# Weaving Playbook — Town Scale (2,000–15,000 residents)

## Purpose

This playbook translates the Tier A simulation matrix into operational guidance for
town-scale weaving projects. It is written for community organizers, cooperative
founders, and prospective operators evaluating weaving at a population of 2,000–15,000.
The town scale is where the weaving cooperative ecosystem comes fully alive.

---

## Honesty statement: what this catalog does and does not restore

Commodity cloth production — plain-weave cotton, utility wool yardage, everyday linen —
is not restored by this catalog at any scale. DECLINE-VERDICT §3 documents this finding
across all four anchor cultures without exception. The power loom displaced commodity
hand-weaving permanently; no loom redesign, no cooperative structure, and no civic
investment changes that competitive position. Every entry in this catalog targets a
specialty, heritage, or community-access niche. This is the scope of what hand-weaving
can do at town scale; it is not a narrower claim than the evidence supports, but it
is no wider.

## The matrix at town scale

| Lens | Wins | Fails | Win rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| town_market | 4 | 11 | 4/15 |
| town_coop | 14 | 1 | 14/15 |
| town_civic | 3 | 12 | 3/15 |

Town scale is the inflection point for weaving. The cooperative lens jumps from 2/15
at village to 14/15 at town — the single largest scale transition in the catalog. The
market result stays constant (same 4 winners as at village and small-city; the market
lens is capital- and payback-driven, not population-driven). The civic lens holds at 3
(weave-009, weave-010, weave-011). The defining characteristic of town-scale weaving
is cooperative accessibility: a town of 2,000–15,000 generates enough membership
potential to sustain nearly every catalog entry as a viable shared enterprise.

## Fiber-sourcing declaration

Every entry in this catalog declares its fiber source. At town scale, industrial yarn
sourced from commercial suppliers is the operative supply path for 14 of 15 entries.
Weave-002 (local fiber spun) is the sole entry dependent on companion-trade spinning.
Where heritage or specialty fiber is claimed, the entry must document the supply chain.
The town scale does not change the fiber-sourcing dependency; it expands the membership
base that can absorb pooled fiber purchasing costs.

## Lens 1: Cooperative — 14/15 win

Town scale is where cooperative weaving becomes the primary form. Fourteen of fifteen
catalog entries clear town_coop. The feasible membership pool at the town midpoint
(8,500 residents × 2.5% participation = ~213 members) comfortably exceeds the break-even
member count for every design except the single outlier (weave-003, which requires a
specialized infrastructure investment that the membership arithmetic alone does not
support at town midpoint).

### Why cooperative weaving works here and not at village

At village scale (pool ~31), the member pool is too thin for all but minimum-capital
designs. At town scale (pool ~213), the pool exceeds every break-even threshold in the
catalog with margin. The cooperative shift at town scale is structural, not marginal:
the organization that was financially impossible at village (a shared weaving studio with
3–6 production looms, a coordinator, and regular programming) becomes straightforwardly
achievable at town.

### Featured cooperative design: weave-013 — Production cooperative

**Profile:** A town-scale weaving cooperative where 3–8 active producing members share
loom infrastructure, fiber purchasing, and marketing. Members may produce individually
distinct work or collaborate on coordinated lines. Fiber source: pooled industrial yarn
purchasing (bulk discount scale available at town membership volume); heritage-fiber
sourcing possible with documented supply pathway.

**Why weave-013 is the town coop flagship.** Weave-013 wins both town_coop and
town_market — a combination that only 4 entries achieve at any scale. At town scale,
the membership pool supports the shared-infrastructure model (3–6 floor looms, warping
and finishing equipment, shared studio space), and the pooled marketing reduces the
per-operator cost of regional and online reach. The production cooperative distributes
both the capital burden and the marketing burden that make solo artisan weaving fragile
at village scale.

**Governance requirements.** A production cooperative with 5–30 members requires:
explicit production-rights agreements (who uses which loom, for what hours, at what
cost), quality standards for goods marketed under the cooperative label, shared fiber
purchasing agreements including cost-pooling formulas, and conflict-resolution procedures
before disputes arise. The Ostrom cooperative governance principles apply: clear
membership boundaries, proportional costs and benefits, member-driven monitoring,
graduated sanctions. A cooperative that skips formal governance documents and relies on
interpersonal trust fails predictably at 10–20 members when norms diverge.

**Capital path.** 3–6 production looms ($900–$4,500 each, used or entry-level new)
plus shared finishing equipment (washing, blocking, storage) and a studio space lease.
Total founding capital: $8,000–$35,000 depending on loom quality and facility. Funding
via founding member equity contributions ($500–$2,000 per member), supplemented by a
workforce-development or small-business cooperative grant if available. No commercial
bank debt required at the lower end of this range.

### Cooperative entries at town scale: a brief survey

**weave-001 — Tapestry studio:** Wins town_coop; fails market. A shared tapestry studio
providing access to high-warp and low-warp tapestry equipment for members. Member dues
fund loom maintenance and facility costs. No commercial production assumption; member
artisan work is the output.

**weave-002 — Local-fiber-spun goods:** Wins town_coop; fails market. The only entry
in the catalog sourcing locally spun fiber from a companion spinning practice. Viable
as a cooperative at town scale; market viability is constrained by the cost premium
of hand-spun yarn versus industrial alternatives. Cooperative members who value the
fiber-autonomy proposition will accept the cost premium; a commercial market will not.

**weave-005, weave-006 — Production volume entries:** Both win town_coop; both fail
market. Cooperative members share production looms for medium-volume output (placemats,
scarves, fabric yardage) but the per-unit economics do not reach market wage on a
single-operator basis. Viable as member-benefit cooperatives where the value is shared
access and craft practice, not income replacement.

**weave-012, weave-015 — Specialty production and pattern licensing:** Both win
town_coop; both fail market. Town-scale cooperative structures support these entries
as supplemental income or craft-community projects, not primary livelihoods.

The pattern across cooperative-only wins is consistent: these are entries that deliver
real value to members — shared equipment, community of practice, coordinated marketing —
without generating income at or above the market wage threshold. They are not failures;
they are correctly labeled as cooperative entries whose value proposition is access and
community, not livelihood.

## Lens 2: Market — 4 winners

The four market winners at town scale are the same four as at village and small-city
scale. Market viability in the Tier A simulation is capital- and payback-driven, not
population-driven. What changes at town scale is qualitative demand depth: a town of
2,000–15,000 residents provides a more credible local customer base for some entries,
though regional and online reach remains essential for all four.

### weave-004 — Natural fiber fashion

See village playbook for entry profile. At town scale, the local demand base is modestly
thicker: a town of 8,500 residents may support 2–4 annual in-person sales events and a
small but real local craft-buying population. The core operating model remains
regional-and-online primary. Town scale is not meaningfully different from village scale
for this entry; the market win is carried by pricing and capital, not by local population.

### weave-007 — Coverlet americana

See village playbook for entry profile. The heritage-coverlet market is national and
regional; a town location serves this market as well as a village location does. A town
of 5,000–15,000 may support a fiber arts guild or weaving group that provides a local
community for skill development and informal marketing, which marginally improves the
operating environment compared to village scale.

### weave-008 — Japanese textile

See village playbook for entry profile. Same market-scale invariance. The cultural and
technical training dependency means operator expertise constrains viability more than
location does at any scale below small-city.

### weave-013 — Production cooperative

At town scale, weave-013 wins both town_market and town_coop. This is the signature
entry at town scale. The market win is conditional on the cooperative achieving sufficient
pooled production volume and coordinated quality to reach per-member take-home above
the town-scale wage median ($56,000/year, SCALES.md §3). This is achievable but not
automatic: a cooperative with 5 members producing 100 pieces per year at $300 average
reaches $60,000 gross per member — plausible at town scale with disciplined production
and shared marketing.

## Lens 3: Civic — 3 winners

Civic wins at town scale: weave-009 (rigid heddle tool library), weave-010 (fiber arts
center), and weave-011 (therapeutic weaving workshop). These are the same three as at
village, but the town-scale context makes weave-010 and weave-011 more operationally
credible — the household base is large enough to support the staffing economics that
village scale cannot.

### weave-009 — Rigid heddle tool library (civic mode, town scale)

The per-household cost argument is stronger at town scale: the same minimal equipment
($1,500–$4,000) spread across 800–6,000 households produces a per-household levy of
$0.25–$5.00/year, well under any reasonable civic threshold. A town library, community
center, or parks department can absorb the weave-009 tool library as a budget line
without dedicated civic deliberation.

### weave-010 — Fiber arts center (civic mode, town scale)

At town scale, weave-010 becomes credible as a civic facility. A town of 5,000–15,000
residents can support a fiber arts center with:
- 4–8 looms of varying types (floor looms, rigid heddle, inkle loom, tapestry frame)
- A part-time or contractor coordinator (10–20 hours per week; $15,000–$28,000/year)
- Regular programming (open studio, beginner workshops, youth series)

The coordinator wage at town scale is coverable: a 5,000-household town at $6/hh/year
generates $30,000 — enough for part-time coordination plus maintenance costs. At the
10,000-household end, the budget supports full-time coordination. Weave-010 is a
town-scale design that fails at village scale because the staffing economics do not work;
it is the correct scale fit here.

**Civic investment framing.** A fiber arts center at town scale is justifiable on three
grounds: (1) cultural heritage access — textile arts are among the oldest documented
craft forms, and community access to them has documented educational and social value;
(2) pathway to cooperative and market weaving — a civic fiber arts center seeds the
membership base that later sustains a weave-013 production cooperative; (3) cost
efficiency relative to comparable civic investments — a fiber arts center at $4–$8/hh/yr
is far below the per-household cost of a public library ($100–$137/hh/yr, IMLS FY2021)
or a recreation center.

### weave-011 — Therapeutic weaving workshop (civic mode, town scale)

The institutional demand condition that constrains weave-011 at village scale is more
reliably met at town scale. A town of 5,000–15,000 residents is more likely to have
a community mental-health center, an occupational therapy program, or an elder-care
facility with both the institutional capacity and the participant population to run a
therapeutic weaving program. The civic win at town scale should be treated as actionable
if and only if the institutional partner is identified and committed. Do not fund weave-011
civic infrastructure without a signed institutional partnership agreement that specifies
participant hours, referral pathway, and minimum program duration.

**Small-city civic note.** The matrix shows weave-010 also winning small-city civic
and weave-011 failing small-city civic. Town is weave-011's ceiling scale for civic
viability; the institutional demand concentration required exceeds what small-city civic
structures typically deliver. This is not an error; it reflects the program model's
dependence on specific institutional partners, which become harder to coordinate at
larger population concentrations.

## Implementation sketch — featured designs

### Sketch A: Weave-013, Production cooperative (town coop + market flagship)

**Context:** A town of 4,000–12,000 residents with an existing weaving community (guild,
classes, or informal network) and 6–15 weavers willing to commit founding capital and
regular production time.

**Phase 1: Founding (months 1–4)**

- Identify 6–10 founding members willing to commit $500–$1,500 each in founding equity
  and 5–10 hours per week of production or cooperative-management time.
- Establish governance before opening: production-rights schedule, fiber-purchasing
  agreement, quality standards for goods sold under the cooperative label, conflict-
  resolution procedure. Use a written bylaws template; adapt for weaving-specific
  provisions.
- Secure studio space: 40–80 m² light-commercial or residential-commercial mixed-use.
  Weaving studios have modest infrastructure requirements (standard electrical, floor
  loading for loom weight, natural light preferred). Budget $600–$1,500/month for
  town-scale light commercial.
- Equipment: 4–6 floor looms ($900–$3,500 each used, $2,500–$6,000 new); 1–2 table
  looms for sampling and production; warping board and raddle, spools, and shuttles
  for each loom. Total capital range: $8,000–$30,000 depending on loom quality and
  whether founding members contribute existing equipment.

**Phase 2: Production ramp (months 5–12)**

- Establish shared fiber purchasing: identify 2–3 commercial yarn suppliers for bulk
  purchase discounts. Plan quarterly orders to reduce per-unit fiber cost 15–25% versus
  individual retail.
- Launch coordinated marketing: cooperative website, shared Etsy shop or equivalent,
  appearance at 3–5 regional craft fairs in the first year. Assign one member as
  marketing lead with explicit time commitment.
- First-year production target: 200–400 finished pieces across all members. Quality
  control: every piece sold under the cooperative label reviewed by designated quality
  steward before listing.

**Year-1 P&L sketch (cooperative aggregate)**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Gross revenue (300 pieces × $280 average) | $84,000 |
| Fiber and material costs (25% of revenue) | $21,000 |
| Studio rent ($1,000/mo × 8 months operating) | $8,000 |
| Marketing and fair fees | $3,500 |
| Equipment maintenance and replacement reserve | $1,500 |
| Total operating costs | $34,000 |
| Distributable to 6 members | $50,000 |
| Per-member take-home | ~$8,300 |

Year-1 per-member take-home is supplemental income, not livelihood replacement. This is
expected: the cooperative is ramping production and establishing market channels. Year-2
target with full production and established channels: 500–700 pieces at $300 average =
$150,000–$210,000 gross, distributable ~$100,000–$140,000 to 6 members = $17,000–$23,000
per member. This approaches part-time livelihood territory at the top end; full livelihood
replacement requires either fewer members at higher production or movement into higher-
priced commission work.

**Key risks:**
- Member departure: if a founding member leaves in year 1, production capacity drops
  and founding capital recovery per remaining member decreases. Mitigate with a 12-month
  founding commitment agreement with explicit exit provisions.
- Quality variance across members: goods sold under a shared label must meet consistent
  quality. A member whose output falls below standards requires a respectful but direct
  conversation before it damages the cooperative's market reputation.
- Fiber cost increases: commercial yarn prices are subject to supply-chain volatility.
  The 25% material cost assumption should be stress-tested at 35% before accepting
  the business model.

### Sketch B: Weave-007, Coverlet americana (market-primary, town context)

**Context:** A solo weaver or weaver-and-apprentice pair in a town of 3,000–12,000
residents, operating a market-primary coverlet production practice with regional and
online sales focus.

**Setup:** 8-shaft floor loom capable of coverlet structures ($2,500–$8,000); Jacquard
loom head if targeting Jacquard patterns ($8,000–$25,000 additional for a dedicated
Jacquard mechanism or $0 if using software-driven shaft coordination on a modern dobby
loom). Commercial cotton and wool yarn ($500–$1,000 initial inventory). Studio space:
home-studio sufficient for a solo practice; light commercial if apprentice is added.

**Revenue model:** Heritage-textile collectors, interior design professionals, and
regional Americana market. Coverlets sell at $300–$1,200 per piece depending on size,
complexity, and provenance documentation. Target 60–100 pieces per year in steady state.
Primary channel: online direct-to-consumer; secondary: regional heritage and Americana
dealers, antique fairs, and fiber arts festivals.

**Town advantage over village.** A town of 5,000–15,000 is more likely to have a
local fiber arts guild or weaving group that provides informal peer learning, potential
apprentice recruitment, and local market reach. These are soft advantages, not structural
ones: the market model is still regional-primary.

**Year-1 P&L sketch (solo operator)**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Revenue (50 pieces × $500 mid-price × 0.7 ramp factor) | $17,500 |
| Fiber and material costs ($75/piece) | $3,750 |
| Annual marketing and platform fees | $1,200 |
| Studio overhead (home-studio assumed) | $1,800 |
| Equipment amortization ($6,000 over 20 yr = $300/yr) | $300 |
| **Year-1 net** | **$10,450** |

Year-1 represents a part-time or transition income level. Year-2 steady state with 80
pieces at $500 average: $40,000 gross, $28,000 net — approaching full-time rural
livelihood at the village-scale wage reference level ($48,000/yr). Moving to higher-priced
Jacquard coverlets or commission work raises this ceiling.

| Design | Capital (founding) | Annual operating | Funding path |
|---|---|---|---|
| weave-013 (production coop, 6 members) | $8,000–$30,000 | $25,000–$40,000 | Founding member equity; small-biz coop grant |
| weave-007 (solo coverlet, market) | $3,000–$10,000 | $7,000–$12,000 | Operator equity; microloan optional |
| weave-010 (civic fiber arts center) | $15,000–$45,000 | $20,000–$40,000 | Municipal civic budget; civic grant |
| weave-011 (therapeutic, with partner) | $5,000–$20,000 | $15,000–$30,000 | Healthcare partner + civic co-investment |

Town-scale weaving capital ranges from $3,000 (solo market operator) to $45,000 (civic
fiber arts center fitout). These are substantially lower capital requirements than
smithing at comparable scale, reflecting both the lower equipment cost of a loom relative
to a forge and the cooperative structure's distributed funding model.

## Open risks at town scale

**Governance brittleness.** A 10–20 member production cooperative without documented
governance procedures fails at the first significant disagreement. The investment in
formal bylaws, written production-rights agreements, and conflict-resolution procedures
is not administrative overhead; it is the structural difference between a cooperative
that persists and one that dissolves. Budget 20–40 hours of facilitated governance work
before launch; treat it as part of the founding capital.

**Market concentration in 4 entries.** Only 4 of 15 entries win town_market. Town-scale
weaving is not a broad commercial opportunity; it is a narrow set of premium and heritage
niches. A weave-013 production cooperative that diversifies into weave-004 fashion and
weave-007 coverlet production within the same organization is internally competing for
loom time and marketing energy. Maintain focus; let the cooperative's product identity
be clear before diversifying.

**Institutional dependency for civic entries.** Weave-010 and weave-011 both require
institutional partners (municipality and healthcare provider, respectively) whose
commitment can end. A civic fiber arts center whose coordinator is defunded in a budget
cycle, or a therapeutic weaving program whose clinical partner withdraws, loses its
operating basis. Design civic entries with diversified institutional support from
the start: two institutional partners are more resilient than one.

**Cooperative versus civic competition for the same participants.** A town may have both
a weave-010 civic fiber arts center and a weave-013 production cooperative. The civic
center takes participants away from the cooperative's membership pool. This is not a
crisis if the civic center performs its public-access function (onboarding new weavers
who later join the cooperative) and the cooperative performs its production function
(converting skill to livelihood). Design the relationship explicitly before both
entities launch.

**Fiber supply at scale.** A 6-member cooperative consuming 600+ lbs of yarn per year
exceeds what a single craft-store account can supply reliably. Establish direct accounts
with 2–3 commercial yarn suppliers before reaching production scale. Bulk ordering at
3-month intervals reduces per-unit cost and supply risk simultaneously.

## Scale-appropriate framing

Town scale is where the weaving cooperative ecosystem lives. The jump from 2/15 coop
wins at village to 14/15 at town is not incremental; it represents a structural opening
of the cooperative form that village population cannot support. A town that wants to
restore local weaving capacity has genuine options across all three lenses: 4 market
entries for commercial operators, 14 cooperative entries for member organizations, and
3 civic entries for publicly funded community infrastructure.

The constraint that DECLINE-VERDICT establishes — no commodity cloth production — holds
at every scale including town. Every winning entry targets specialty, heritage, or
community-access niches. This is the correct scope for weaving restoration: ambitious
within the evidence-supported domain, honest about the domain's limits.

The featured flagship for town-scale weaving is the weave-013 production cooperative
paired with a weave-010 civic fiber arts center. These two structures are complementary:
the civic center seeds the skill base; the cooperative converts that skill into production
and livelihood. Neither is sufficient alone; together they constitute a town-scale
weaving ecosystem.
