# Weaving Playbook — Village Scale (500–2,000 residents)

## Purpose

This playbook translates the Tier A simulation matrix into operational guidance for
village-scale weaving projects. Readers are community organizers, rural-development
workers, and prospective weavers evaluating whether a weaving practice is viable in a
settlement of 500–2,000 people. The playbook does not replace per-entry catalog files;
it synthesizes the matrix for someone who needs a decision.

---

## Honesty statement: what this catalog does and does not restore

Commodity cloth production — plain-weave cotton, utility wool yardage, everyday linen —
is not restored by this catalog at any scale. DECLINE-VERDICT §3 documents this finding
across all four anchor cultures (medieval northern Europe, Song China, Tokugawa Japan,
American frontier) without a single counter-case. The power loom and its industrial
successors displaced commodity hand-weaving permanently; no loom redesign changes that
competitive position. Every entry in this catalog targets a specialty, heritage, or
community-access niche where the evidence shows genuine restructuring or survival. This
is not a failure of the catalog; it is what the evidence says. A village planning committee
should read the market wins below as niche opportunities, not as evidence that broad cloth
production is recoverable.

## The matrix at village scale

| Lens | Wins | Fails | Win rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| village_market | 4 | 11 | 4/15 |
| village_coop | 2 | 13 | 2/15 |
| village_civic | 3 | 12 | 3/15 |

Weaving at village scale is the most constrained set in the catalog. Four market wins
reflect the narrowed landscape DECLINE-VERDICT establishes: only premium-artisan and
identity-specific niches clear the market threshold. The cooperative lens passes for only
two entries — both minimum-capital designs whose break-even member count falls within
the thin village membership pool. Village weaving is a premium artisan practice or a
minimum-capital community-access point; the broad middle of production-volume weaving
requires town scale to open.

## Fiber-sourcing declaration

Every entry in this catalog must declare its fiber source. This requirement flows
directly from DECLINE-VERDICT §3's fiber-sourcing falsifier: no historical weaving form
operated with autonomous fiber supply across four cultures and four centuries. The
practical implication at village scale is direct: most entries source industrial yarn
purchased from commercial suppliers (the operationally defensible choice). Only one
entry in the catalog (weave-002) sources local-fiber-spun yarn from a companion spinning
practice. Entries claiming heritage-fiber authenticity require a documented supply
pathway that a village-scale operator must verify before launch. Full fiber-to-cloth
autonomy requires not one trade but three or four coordinated trades.

## Market lens — 4 winning designs

The four market winners at village scale are identical to those at town and small-city
scale. The market lens in the Tier A simulation evaluates capital, payback, and operator
take-home against the village-scale wage median; it does not model demand depth directly.
Readers should apply the demand qualifications below before committing capital.

### weave-004 — Natural fiber fashion

**Profile:** Artisan fashion production using natural fibers (wool, linen, silk) for
direct-to-consumer sale. Operator: experienced weaver with design and marketing capacity.
Fiber source: commercial industrial yarn and natural-dye inputs; no local-fiber-spin
dependency at entry level.

**Why it wins at village scale.** The market lens passes because the unit price for
hand-woven artisan fashion ($180–$600+ per piece) is high enough to support operator
take-home against modest capital, even with thin local volume. The product is
non-fungible: a factory cannot replicate a named weaver's hand-woven piece with
documented fiber provenance and hand-crafted identity. DECLINE-VERDICT §3 identifies
this product-differentiation mechanism as the core survival pathway for luxury
hand-weaving.

**Village-specific qualification.** A village of 500–2,000 residents does not generate
enough local fashion demand to sustain a full-time artisan fashion weaver from walk-in
customers. Viability requires a hybrid channel model: direct-to-consumer online sales,
craft-fair appearances (regional, not local), and commissions from customers outside
the village population. A weave-004 operator in a village must plan for regional and
online reach from day one. Do not model revenue on village foot-traffic alone.

**Capital entry.** Four-shaft floor loom ($800–$3,000); warping board and accessories
($200–$400); initial yarn inventory ($300–$600). Total: $1,300–$4,000 depending on loom
choice. This is the lowest capital entry for a market-viable weaving design in the catalog.

### weave-007 — Coverlet americana

**Profile:** Reproduction and original-design coverlets in the Jacquard or overshot
tradition of American frontier weaving. Fiber source: commercial industrial yarn
(primarily cotton and wool); heritage-breed wool is possible with documented supply
pathway. Operator: weaver trained in multi-shaft or Jacquard-mechanism coverlet weaving.

**Why it wins at village scale.** Jacquard and overshot coverlets are the most thoroughly
documented restructured specialty in American frontier weaving history (DECLINE-VERDICT
§2.4). The niche survived mid-nineteenth century because factory production could not
replicate a named weaver's custom-pattern coverlet from a specific household's yarn. That
differentiation logic applies today. Coverlets command $300–$1,200 per piece in the
heritage-textile and collectors' market.

**Village-specific qualification.** As with weave-004, local demand alone cannot sustain
a coverlet specialist. The customer base is the heritage-textile and interior-decoration
market, reached primarily through online channels, craft fairs, and regional antique and
Americana dealers. A village is an acceptable operating location — the craft is portable
in its logistics — but the revenue comes from outside the village.

**Fiber-sourcing note.** Standard industrial cotton and wool yarn sourced from commercial
suppliers is the documented and viable fiber path. A weave-007 operator claiming heritage-
breed wool must document the supply chain explicitly before launch. The American frontier
weaving tradition used purchased factory-spun yarn by the 1830s–1840s (DECLINE-VERDICT
§2.4); historical accuracy and supply-chain practicality align here.

### weave-008 — Japanese textile

**Profile:** Production of textile goods in the Japanese hand-weaving tradition: kasuri
(ikat), hon-nishiki variants, and regionally specific fabric types. Fiber source:
commercial industrial silk, cotton, or linen yarn; heritage-grade raw silk requires a
documented sericulture or import supply pathway.

**Why it wins at village scale.** The Nishijin survival case in DECLINE-VERDICT §2.3
is the catalog's clearest example of luxury hand-weaving surviving through product
differentiation and technological adaptation. The product's value proposition is
inseparable from the craft identity: a named weaver's kasuri is not replicable by
industrial production. Unit prices for museum-quality or gallery-quality Japanese textile
work range from $400 to $4,000+, sustaining operator take-home against modest capital.

**Village-specific qualification.** Operator cultural and technical training is the
primary constraint, not capital. Authentic Japanese weaving traditions require years of
specialized skill development that is not easily acquired in a general weaving workshop.
A village-scale weave-008 operation is realistic only if the operator has pre-existing
deep training. Capital is low (floor loom or specialized Japanese loom, $1,000–$8,000
depending on tradition); the binding constraint is operator expertise, not equipment.

---

### weave-013 — Production cooperative

**Profile:** A small production cooperative (3–6 weavers) pooling loom capacity,
fiber purchasing, and marketing. Members may produce individually distinct work or
coordinate on shared pattern libraries. Fiber source: pooled industrial yarn purchasing
(bulk discount) with optional heritage-fiber sourcing if supply is documented.

**Why it wins at village scale — and why the matrix overstates it.** The market lens
passes for weave-013 on the assumption that pooled production and bulk fiber sourcing
reduce per-unit costs enough to reach viable operator take-home. This is architecturally
correct. However: a production cooperative at village scale faces the same demand thinness
as individual market entries — the local customer base cannot sustain 3–6 weavers
producing at volume. The market win is real but the operating model requires regional
reach and shared marketing investment. Treat the village market win for weave-013 as
conditional: viable if the cooperative actively pursues regional and online channels
from launch; fragile if it relies on village walk-in demand.

## Cooperative lens — 2 winning designs

Only two entries clear village_coop: **weave-009** (rigid heddle tool library) and
**weave-014** (backstrap loom community access). Both win for the same structural reason:
their break-even member count falls below the village feasible membership pool (500–2,000
residents × 2.5% participation = 13–50 members, midpoint ~31). Every other catalog entry
has a higher break-even than the village pool can supply.

### weave-009 — Rigid heddle tool library

**Profile:** A shared tool library of rigid heddle looms (entry-level, portable, $60–$200
each) made available to community members by booking. No dedicated facility required at
minimum configuration; looms check out like library books. Membership dues cover
replacement and maintenance.

**Why this works at village scale.** Break-even member count is low because capital is
minimal. A library of 6–10 rigid heddle looms ($600–$2,000 total) plus a small consumables
budget ($300–$500/year for yarn samples and maintenance parts) clears on a membership
of 15–20 dues-paying participants — well within the village pool. This is not a production
forge; it is a community access point that reduces the capital barrier to trying weaving.

**Civic compatibility.** Weave-009 also wins village_civic (see below). The tool-library
model is the strongest village-scale candidate for a combined cooperative-and-civic
structure: a community organization can hold the looms, dues-paying members fund
maintenance, and occasional civic-access events (school programs, open loom days) serve
non-member residents. The distinction between cooperative governance and civic governance
matters less at this scale than at town or small-city.

**Fiber-sourcing note.** Rigid heddle looms operate on commercial yarn available at any
craft store. No specialized fiber supply pathway is required; this is the simplest fiber
situation in the catalog.

---

### weave-014 — Backstrap loom community access

**Profile:** Shared backstrap looms (traditional, minimal-capital, portable) used in
a community education and cultural-access context. Fiber source: commercial yarn;
cultural-tradition fiber sourcing where applicable and documented.

**Why this works at village scale.** Backstrap looms cost $20–$150 each; a community
collection of 8–12 looms is acquired for $160–$1,800 total. Break-even is similarly low:
10–15 members can sustain the library. Backstrap weaving has the lowest capital barrier
of any weaving form in the catalog and the most portable pedagogical format.

**Cultural dimension.** Backstrap loom weaving carries documented heritage significance
in multiple cultural traditions (Mesoamerican, Andean, and Southeast Asian textile
traditions in particular). A community with cultural ties to those traditions may find
weave-014 carries civic value that the cooperative-lens formula does not capture. Where
that cultural context exists, the cooperative form and civic investment case are
mutually reinforcing.

## Civic lens — 3 winning designs

Three entries win village_civic: weave-009 (rigid heddle tool library), weave-010 (fiber
arts center), and weave-011 (therapeutic weaving workshop). The formula passes; the
qualitative analysis below is required before acting on these wins.

### weave-009 — Rigid heddle tool library (civic mode)

Already discussed under the cooperative lens. Wins civic at village because the
per-household cost is very low (minimal capital spread across even a small household
base) and the public-access function is explicit. This is the one village civic win
where the formula result is operationally robust: the scale of commitment matches the
scale of the resource.

### weave-010 — Fiber arts center

**Profile:** A civic fiber arts center providing looms, spinning equipment, and textile
tools for community use, with programming (classes, open studio, youth programs). Capital
$15,000–$45,000 for a modest fitout.

**Village-scale qualification.** The civic formula passes, but weave-010 at village
scale faces a staffing economics problem: a meaningful fiber arts center requires a
coordinator or instructor whose wage is not easily supported by the per-household tax
or dues levy that a village of 400–800 households can generate. The formula win is real;
the staffing model requires honest examination before committing. A village that wants
fiber arts center capability is better served by a hybrid: a weave-009 tool library
(low operating cost, no dedicated instructor) supplemented by periodic visiting-instructor
events rather than a full weave-010 civic center. Full weave-010 civic infrastructure
is more viable at town scale where the household base is larger.

### weave-011 — Therapeutic weaving workshop

**Profile:** Weaving as occupational therapy or mental-health support programming,
delivered in partnership with a healthcare or social-services institution. Looms are
specialized for accessibility (adjustable height, simplified shedding mechanisms). Fiber
source: commercial yarn, simplified and robust.

**Village-scale qualification.** Weave-011 wins village_civic on the formula, but the
institutional demand condition is strict: this entry's viability depends on a healthcare
partner (occupational therapy program, community mental-health center, elder-care
facility) committing participant hours and institutional support. DECLINE-VERDICT §5
states this explicitly: therapeutic weaving is a civic lens entry whose "viability
depends on institutional demand committing the workshop hours and participant population
— it is not an assumed demand condition." A village must identify the institutional
partner before treating the weave-011 civic win as actionable. Without a documented
healthcare or social-services partner, the civic win is a formula result, not an
operational plan.

## Implementation sketch — featured designs

### Design 1: Weave-004, Natural fiber fashion (solo artisan, market-primary)

**Context:** Experienced weaver relocating to or already based in a rural village of
800–1,500 residents, seeking to operate a small artisan fashion practice with a hybrid
local-and-online revenue model.

**Setup:**

- Equipment: 8-shaft floor loom ($1,800–$4,500), warping board, shuttles, and accessories
  ($300–$600), natural dye setup if using dye work ($200–$500). Total capital:
  $2,300–$5,600.
- Fiber sourcing: commercial industrial yarn from craft suppliers (Ashford, Valley Yarns,
  Webs, regional wool mills). No special supply chain required to launch. Heritage or
  natural-dye fiber can be added incrementally as sources are identified.
- Studio space: a dedicated room in an existing structure is sufficient for a solo loom.
  No separate commercial space required at launch; add it when revenue justifies.

**Revenue model:**

- Online direct-to-consumer (Etsy, personal website, Instagram-to-shop funnel): primary
  channel, targeting 60–70% of revenue.
- Regional craft fairs (4–6 per year, 2–4 hours drive radius): secondary channel.
- Custom commissions from regional interior designers, boutique clothing buyers,
  or fiber-art collectors: tertiary channel once reputation is established.

**Year-1 target:** 40–60 finished pieces sold at $200–$400 average = $8,000–$24,000
gross revenue. This is a part-time or supplemental-income model in year 1. Full-time
viability requires year-2 volume of 80–120 pieces or movement into higher-priced
custom commission work ($500–$1,200 per piece). Most sustainable village artisan fashion
weavers operate at the intersection of production and commission work.

**Key risks:**
- Online marketing skill gap: weaving quality and marketing quality are separate
  competencies; plan for 3–6 months of platform learning before expecting consistent
  online sales.
- Fiber cost management: natural fibers (wool, linen, silk) have higher raw material
  costs than synthetic alternatives; build 30% material cost into pricing from launch.
- Scale ceiling: a single weaver on a single floor loom has a hard production ceiling
  (~200 hours of finished product per year at part-time weaving). Pricing must reflect
  this scarcity.

---

### Design 2: Weave-009, Rigid heddle tool library (cooperative/civic, minimum-capital)

**Context:** Community organization, library, school, or rural community center in a
village of 500–1,200 residents seeking to provide accessible weaving infrastructure.
No commercial operator needed; the entry point is community access, not production revenue.

**Setup:**

- Equipment: 8–12 rigid heddle looms (Ashford Knitter's Loom, Schacht Cricket Loom, or
  equivalent; $65–$180 each). Total loom cost: $520–$2,160. Add 2–3 table looms for
  participants ready to progress ($200–$600 each). Full kit: $1,120–$3,960.
- Storage: a standard bookshelf or closet; looms are flat when disassembled.
- Management: a volunteer coordinator responsible for booking, checkout, and basic
  maintenance is sufficient. No paid instructor required at minimum.

**Governance options:**

- Cooperative model: 15–25 dues-paying members at $40–$80/year cover maintenance and
  replacement. Governance via simple membership agreement.
- Library or civic model: existing organization (library, community center, school)
  holds equipment and manages lending. Annual budget line of $600–$1,500 covers
  consumables and replacement.

**Programming that adds civic value without adding staffing cost:**
- Quarterly open loom days: one Saturday per season, looms set up for drop-in use.
  Volunteer experienced weaver hosts. Cost: $0 in labor; modest yarn refreshment cost.
- Youth workshops: 4–6 sessions per school year in partnership with local school.
  One experienced volunteer per session; minimal yarn cost.
- Lending to civic events: looms travel to festivals, senior centers, or farmers'
  markets as a demonstration and community-engagement tool.

**Year-1 target:** 15–25 dues-paying members, 60–100 loom-checkout events, 2–4
school/community workshops. No revenue target; this is a community-access investment.
Sustainability depends on member retention, not on commercial scale.

| Design | Capital | Annual operating | Funding path |

| weave-004 (solo artisan fashion) | $2,300–$5,600 | $3,000–$6,000 | Operator equity; microloan if needed |
| weave-009 (tool library) | $1,120–$3,960 | $600–$1,500 | Member dues; library budget line; small civic grant |

Village weaving at its two most defensible configurations requires $1,000–$6,000 in
capital and modest annual operating budgets. This is the lowest capital threshold in the
CERES catalog. The market-primary path (weave-004) recovers capital in year 1 or 2 with
moderate online sales volume; the community-access path (weave-009) is not designed for
capital recovery — it is a service.

## Open risks at village scale

**Demand thinness.** Four market winners exist on formula; all four depend on regional
or online reach beyond the village. A village of 500–2,000 residents cannot sustain any
of the four market-win designs on local demand alone. The market wins are genuine but
the operating model must account for this from day one.

**Fiber supply chain.** Industrial yarn is available via mail order with 3–7 day lead
time from major suppliers. Heritage or specialty fiber (heritage-breed wool, hand-reeled
silk) may require 2–4 week lead times and single-supplier dependencies. Plan inventory
accordingly; do not let a fiber stock-out interrupt a commission during a peak season.

**Skill reproduction.** Multi-shaft and Jacquard weaving require 3–5 years of consistent
practice to reach commercial quality. A village with one skilled weaver has no built-in
succession if that weaver leaves. The tool-library model (weave-009, weave-014) mitigates
this at the community-access level by keeping loom familiarity alive in a wider pool,
but it does not reproduce market-level production skill. Explicit skill-transmission
plans — mentorship, workshop series, regional weaving guild connections — are essential
for any village weaving initiative that wants generational continuity.

**The cooperative ceiling.** Only two entries clear village_coop and both are
minimum-capital. A village that wants a production-scale shared weaving studio
(3–6 looms, regular workshop hours, paid instructor) needs either town-scale population
to support the member pool, external funding to bridge the capital gap, or a civic
institutional partner to hold the infrastructure. Plan H entries designed for production-
scale cooperative weaving are not viable at village scale without one of these
supplements.

## Scale-appropriate framing

Village weaving is viable in two configurations: premium artisan practice (weave-004,
weave-007, weave-008) relying on regional and online markets, or minimum-capital community
access (weave-009, weave-014) operating as a civic amenity or small cooperative. These
two configurations do not compete; they serve different goals. Nothing in between — not
a production cooperative, not a fiber arts center with a paid instructor, not a therapeutic
program without institutional backing — clears village-scale thresholds.

The sharpest finding from DECLINE-VERDICT for village operators: commodity cloth
production is falsified. Every village weaving entry in this catalog targets a niche
that factory production cannot replicate. Premium artisan and heritage work; culturally
specific textiles; shared community access — these are the evidence-supported forms.
