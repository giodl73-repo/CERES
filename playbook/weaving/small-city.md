# Weaving Playbook — Small City Scale (15,000–75,000 residents)

## Purpose

This playbook translates the Tier A simulation matrix into operational guidance for
small-city weaving projects. It is written for city planners, cooperative developers,
and prospective operators evaluating weaving in a population of 15,000–75,000. At
small-city scale, the cooperative lens reaches full saturation and the market lens
includes its most capital-intensive viable entry (weave-003, architectural textile).

---

## Honesty statement: what this catalog does and does not restore

Commodity cloth production is not restored by this catalog at any scale. DECLINE-VERDICT
§3 establishes this finding as the strongest single result in the CERES research corpus:
commodity hand-weaving declined universally across all four anchor cultures (medieval
northern Europe, Song China, Tokugawa Japan, American frontier) with no counter-cases.
Industrial alternatives dominate commodity cloth on price, consistency, and quality by
margins that no loom redesign closes. Every entry in this catalog targets a specialty,
heritage, or community-access niche. At small-city scale, the civic lens opens two
genuine institutional wins (weave-010, weave-011 failing, weave-010 winning; the full
civic picture is detailed below), and the cooperative lens is universal. The commercial
opportunity is real but narrow: 4 of 15 entries win the market lens, and all four
require premium or niche positioning to sustain operator take-home. This is not a
failure of the catalog; it is the evidence-supported scope of hand-weaving restoration.

## The matrix at small-city scale

**Population midpoint used in simulation:** 45,000 (range 15,000–75,000)

| Lens | Wins | Fails | Win rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| small_city_market | 4 | 11 | 4/15 |
| small_city_coop | 15 | 0 | 15/15 |
| small_city_civic | 1 | 14 | 1/15 |

Small-city scale produces the cooperative ceiling: every catalog entry clears small-city
coop. The feasible membership pool at the midpoint (45,000 residents × 2.5% = ~1,125
members) overwhelms even the highest break-even count in the catalog. The market lens
holds at 4 winners — scale-invariant, as at village and town. The civic lens narrows to
1 winner (weave-010, fiber arts center), reflecting a structural pattern explained in
full below.

## Fiber-sourcing declaration

Every entry in this catalog declares its fiber source. Industrial yarn from commercial
suppliers is the operative path for 14 of 15 entries at small-city scale. Weave-002
(local-fiber-spun goods) is the sole entry dependent on a companion spinning trade.
Heritage-fiber claims require documented supply chains. Small-city scale enables pooled
bulk fiber purchasing at volume that reduces per-unit cost for cooperative entries; it
does not change the fundamental sourcing dependency.

## Lens 1: Cooperative — 15/15 win

At small-city scale, the cooperative lens is universal. Every entry clears. The
feasible member pool of ~1,125 (at the 45,000-resident midpoint) exceeds every
break-even member count in the catalog by a wide margin — the highest break-even entry
is still well under 200 members. The cooperative lens does not differentiate entries
at this scale: all 15 pass with abundant margin. Differentiation among cooperative
designs must rest on governance model, capital structure, product focus, and operator
capacity, not on membership feasibility.

### What universal cooperative coverage means in practice

A small city can support multiple concurrent cooperative weaving organizations without
depleting the feasible member pool. The 1,125-member pool at midpoint can simultaneously
sustain:
- A production cooperative (weave-013): 15–40 active producing members
- A tapestry studio cooperative (weave-001): 20–60 members, primarily artisan-access
- A tool-library cooperative (weave-009, weave-014): 15–30 members
- A civic fiber arts center (weave-010) drawing on a separate institutional budget

These organizations draw from overlapping but not identical participant populations.
A weaver who belongs to the production cooperative may also hold membership in the
tapestry studio. The small-city membership pool is large enough that overlap does not
deplete any single organization's break-even base.

### Featured cooperative design: weave-001 — Tapestry studio

**Profile:** A cooperative providing shared access to high-warp and low-warp tapestry
looms, with studio hours, member booking, and collective governance. Fiber source:
commercial yarn (wool, cotton, linen); heritage-grade wool or natural-dye fiber available
with documented supply pathway.

**Why weave-001 is the small-city coop flagship.** Weave-001 wins both small-city_coop
and small-city_market — one of only 4 dual-win entries in the catalog, and the only
tapestry entry to clear the market lens. At small-city scale, the member pool supports
professional-grade tapestry loom infrastructure (high-warp looms cost $2,000–$10,000
each; a studio of 4–8 looms is a $16,000–$80,000 investment), and the population base
provides enough serious tapestry practitioners to sustain both the cooperative
membership model and occasional commission-grade production output.

DECLINE-VERDICT §2.1 documents tapestry as the most durable restructured niche in the
northern European weaving record: Arras, Brussels, and Aubusson tapestry survived the
industrial era because the product is non-standardizable, non-fungible, and bespoke.
A named weaver's tapestry at commission pricing ($1,500–$15,000+ for a significant
work) is not replicable by factory production. The small-city scale provides the member
base to sustain shared infrastructure and the population concentration that supports
commissioned institutional and private clients.

**Capital.** 4–8 high-warp tapestry looms ($2,000–$10,000 each); auxiliary low-warp
looms for sampling and production work ($500–$3,000 each); studio space 60–120 m²
with north-facing light preferred. Total founding capital: $15,000–$100,000 depending
on loom quality and studio fitout. Member dues and founding member equity at 30–60
founding members covers the lower end of this range; a civic or foundation grant
covers the upper end.

### Production cooperative at small-city scale: weave-013

See town playbook for the production cooperative profile. At small-city scale, weave-013
operates with larger member capacity and greater fiber-purchasing leverage. A 10–20
member production cooperative in a small city can reach bulk purchasing volume that
reduces per-unit fiber cost 20–30% versus individual retail. The per-member take-home
arithmetic at small-city scale (larger member base, potentially higher-priced commission
market in a denser population) is modestly more favorable than at town scale, though
the operating model is structurally the same.

## Lens 2: Market — 4 winners

The four market winners at small-city scale are the same four as at every scale:
weave-004 (natural fiber fashion), weave-007 (coverlet americana), weave-008 (japanese
textile), and weave-013 (production cooperative). Market viability is capital- and
payback-driven, not population-driven.

The small-city distinction is qualitative: a city of 15,000–75,000 supports a denser
local arts-and-craft market, a more active regional and national distribution network,
and a larger potential commission client base for high-value pieces. These are
demand-side improvements that the market lens formula does not capture; they are real
operating advantages for market-primary operators who price at the high end.

### Featured market design: weave-003 — Architectural textile

**Profile:** Design and production of architectural-scale woven textiles: wall-mounted
art pieces, installation work, acoustic panels, and commissioned public-space textiles.
Client base: architects, interior designers, public-art programs, hospitality developers.
Fiber source: commercial industrial yarn (wool, linen, synthetic blends for specific
acoustic or structural properties); specialty fiber with documented supply pathway for
heritage or natural-material commissions.

**Why weave-003 is the small-city market primary.** Weave-003 wins small-city_market
and small-city_coop. It fails village and town market — not because the product is less
viable at smaller scales but because the commission pipeline for architectural-scale
textile work requires a client base that only exists at small-city density or above.
Architects and interior designers commissioning site-specific textiles are concentrated
in population centers. Public-art program budgets sufficient for large-scale textile
commissions ($5,000–$80,000 per installation) exist primarily at city and metropolitan
scale.

The architectural textile niche is documented as a genuine restructured specialty:
post-industrial institutions (civic buildings, corporate offices, hospitality venues)
commission hand-woven textile work precisely because it is identifiably hand-made and
site-specific in ways that industrial textile production cannot replicate
(DECLINE-VERDICT §5).

**Capital.** Large-format weaving infrastructure: a warping mill for long warps, a
4- to 8-shaft floor loom with extended weaving width (60" or wider, $4,000–$15,000),
or a specialized architectural weaving frame for very large pieces. Finishing and
installation equipment. Studio space 80–150 m² with high ceiling clearance (4 m+
for tall installations). Total capital: $15,000–$60,000. Commission-primary revenue
model: budget 6–12 months of operating costs before first major commission closes.

**Year-1 operating model (commission-primary):**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Revenue (3 commissions × $8,000 average × 0.5 year-1 ramp) | $12,000 |
| Fiber and materials (30% of revenue) | $3,600 |
| Studio rent ($1,500/mo, small-city light industrial) | $18,000 |
| Marketing, portfolio photography, and design tools | $3,000 |
| Equipment amortization ($30,000 over 15 yr = $2,000/yr) | $2,000 |
| **Year-1 net** | **–$14,600** |

Year-1 is loss-taking if the operator depends on studio revenue alone. The viable
year-1 configuration: part-time supplemental income from weave-004 (fashion) or
teaching, with architectural commissions as the growth trajectory. Year-2 with
6 commissions at $10,000 average: $60,000 gross, $22,800 net after materials; above
the small-city wage reference threshold if studio overhead is reduced (home-studio
or shared space). Steady-state year-3 with repeat institutional clients: the commission
pipeline stabilizes and per-commission pricing increases as reputation builds.

**The commission pipeline is the single most important variable.** An architectural
textile operator without a committed first client should not sign a dedicated studio
lease. Build the commission relationship before committing to fixed overhead.

## Lens 3: Civic — 1 winner

**weave-010 — Fiber arts center wins small-city civic. All other entries fail.**

This result requires explanation: the civic narrowing from 3 winners at town to 1 at
small-city is not a failure of the catalog; it reflects a structural difference in
how civic institutions scale.

### Why weave-010 wins and weave-011 fails at small-city

The matrix shows weave-011 (therapeutic weaving workshop) winning civic at village and
town but failing at small-city. This is not a scoring anomaly. The therapeutic weaving
program's civic viability depends on a specific institutional partnership: an occupational
therapy provider, mental-health center, or elder-care facility must commit participant
hours and clinical integration. At village and town scale, a single institutional partner
is sufficient and findable. At small-city scale, the civic lens applies higher utilization
and per-household cost standards that a single therapeutic partnership program cannot
meet alone. The civic win at small-city requires public-access infrastructure with
broad utilization, not a specialized clinical partnership with limited participant throughput.

Weave-010 (fiber arts center) wins because it is designed as broad public-access
infrastructure — open studio, community classes, youth programs — that scales with
population in a way a therapeutic partnership program does not.

### weave-010 — Fiber arts center at small-city scale

**Profile:** A publicly funded civic fiber arts center providing looms, spinning equipment,
and textile infrastructure for broad community access. Programming includes open studio,
beginner weaving courses, youth programs, advanced technique workshops, and occasional
artist residencies. Civic governance: municipal parks and recreation, public library,
or arts council.

**Why small-city is the right scale.** A small city of 15,000–75,000 residents provides:
- Sufficient household base to make per-household cost negligible: $2–$10/hh/year
  for a $50,000–$150,000 capital investment spread across 6,000–30,000 households
- Enough potential participants to sustain daily open-studio utilization and fill
  weekly class programming (50–200 active users per year is realistic at this scale)
- Budget line capacity: a city arts council or parks department budget absorbs a
  $30,000–$60,000 annual operating cost more credibly than a town budget

**Capital and operating model:**

| Item | Range |
|---|---|
| Loom infrastructure (8–14 looms, varied types) | $15,000–$50,000 |
| Facility fitout (storage, display, classroom) | $10,000–$40,000 |
| Annual coordinator salary (full-time) | $42,000–$58,000 |
| Annual programming (materials, instructors) | $8,000–$18,000 |
| Annual maintenance and replacement reserve | $3,000–$6,000 |
| Total annual operating | $53,000–$82,000 |
| Per-household cost at 10,000 hh midpoint | $5.30–$8.20/hh/yr |

The per-household cost clears standard civic thresholds at small-city scale. For
comparison, a public library costs $100–$137/hh/yr (IMLS FY2021); a parks and
recreation department averages $80–$120/hh/yr. A civic fiber arts center at
$5–$8/hh/yr is one-fifteenth to one-twentieth of these comparable civic investments.

**Civic-cooperative complementarity.** The weave-010 civic fiber arts center at small-city
scale serves the same foundational role that a public library serves for literacy: it
provides universal access to the craft at no individual entry cost. A participant who
discovers weaving through the civic center may later join a weave-013 production
cooperative or invest in personal loom equipment. The civic center does not compete
with cooperative or market weaving operations; it seeds the participant pool that
sustains them.

**Political coalition.** The standard civic-approval challenge: demonstrating that a
fiber arts center is distinct from a hobby program and justifies public funding. The
three-argument coalition: (1) workforce development — the center produces skilled
weavers who may enter the cooperative or market weaving economy; (2) cultural heritage
access — hand-textile arts are among the most documented craft traditions globally
and community access to them is a public good; (3) economic cost comparison — at
$5–$8/hh/yr, the center is substantially cheaper per resident served than any
comparable cultural facility.

## Implementation sketch — featured designs

### Sketch A: Weave-003, Architectural textile (market-primary, small-city flagship)

**Context:** An experienced weaver with an existing portfolio of large-format work
seeking to establish a commission-primary practice in a small city with an active
arts and architecture community.

**Pre-launch requirements:**
- At least 2–3 completed large-format pieces (minimum 4' × 6') in portfolio; without
  this, the commission conversation with architects and designers cannot begin.
- One committed institutional contact: an architect, interior designer, or public-art
  program director willing to consider a first commission.
- Studio space with ceiling height 4 m+. Small-city light-industrial zones with this
  ceiling are available but require specific search; do not assume any light-industrial
  unit qualifies.

**Launch sequence:**

- Months 1–3: Studio search and portfolio completion. Identify 3–5 prospective
  institutional clients (regional architecture firms with active commercial interior
  projects; hotel development contacts; city public-art program staff). Attend one
  regional architecture or interior design conference as an exhibitor or portfolio
  presenter. This is the market-development investment that replaces formal advertising:
  architectural textile is a referral-and-portfolio business.
- Months 4–6: Secure and fit out studio. Negotiate commission with first institutional
  client. The first commission should be priced to cover materials and modest operator
  time rather than full market rate; reputation-building in year 1 is more valuable
  than margin.
- Months 7–12: Complete first commission; document thoroughly (installation photography,
  client testimonial, technical description for portfolio). Use documentation to open
  the next 3–5 prospective client conversations.

**Steady-state model (year 3):**
6–10 commissions per year at $8,000–$25,000 each = $48,000–$250,000 gross. Materials
at 30% = $14,400–$75,000. Studio overhead $18,000–$24,000/year. Net: $15,600–$151,000.
The wide range reflects commission pricing and volume; the floor ($15,600) is a lean
solo practice; the ceiling reflects a 2–3 person studio with assistants producing
multiple simultaneous commissions.

### Sketch B: Weave-001, Tapestry studio (cooperative flagship, small-city)

**Context:** A city of 20,000–60,000 residents with an established arts community and
30–80 weavers or textile-arts practitioners interested in shared access to professional
tapestry infrastructure.

**Phase 1: Founding (months 1–6)**

- Identify 30–50 founding members at $200–$600 dues/year. Target arts community
  networks, fiber arts guilds, and continuing-education weaving program graduates.
- Secure 70–120 m² studio space with north-facing light (preferred for tapestry color
  accuracy). Light-commercial or arts-district mixed-use zoning.
- Purchase equipment: 4 high-warp Aubusson-style looms ($3,000–$8,000 each) and 2–4
  low-warp or floor looms for production members ($1,500–$4,000 each). Total: $15,000–
  $48,000. Supplement with 2 large warping mills ($400–$1,200 each) and shared fiber
  storage and finishing equipment.
- Total founding capital: $20,000–$75,000 depending on loom quality, fitout, and
  facility lease terms.

**Governance.** The tapestry studio cooperative requires explicit booking and
production-rights procedures: high-warp looms are large (6' × 8' footprint or larger)
and a studio of 4 looms supports only 4 concurrent users. Member booking is the
primary governance mechanism; a digital booking system (shared calendar with hold
limits per member per week) prevents de facto monopolization by the most productive
members.

**Commission output.** A tapestry studio cooperative can accept institutional commissions
as a cooperative (credit goes to the member artist; the cooperative earns a studio
fee). This is the market-lens path for weave-001: the cooperative infrastructure
enables commission output that individual members could not produce without the
shared space and equipment. Define the commission-allocation process in bylaws before
accepting the first commissioned inquiry.

### Sketch C: Weave-010, Civic fiber arts center (civic flagship, small-city)

**Context:** A small city of 25,000–60,000 residents with an arts council, parks
department, or public library willing to host and operate a civic fiber arts center
as a civic budget line.

**Political groundwork (year 0):**
- Build the civic coalition: arts council, school district (potential programming
  partner), and any existing fiber arts guilds or weaving cooperatives (framing them
  as beneficiaries, not competitors, of civic infrastructure).
- Commission a needs assessment: survey of potential participants (civic fiber arts
  programs commonly use parks-department recreation interest surveys as precedent).
  Target 150–400 survey responses indicating interest; use this to counter the
  "who will use this?" question in council deliberation.
- Present the per-household cost case: $5–$8/hh/yr is the most effective single
  number in the political argument.

**Year 1 setup and operations:**
- Capital appropriation: $25,000–$90,000 for loom infrastructure and fitout. This is
  a one-time capital item, separable from the annual operating budget.
- Hire coordinator: full-time at $42,000–$52,000/year (arts educator with weaving
  background; search radius 100 miles). The coordinator is the civic center's single
  critical hire; the center does not function at scale without a qualified, full-time
  professional.
- Programming calendar from month 1: 3 beginner-series cohorts per year (8-session
  format); weekly open studio (2 sessions/week, 3 hours each); one youth summer camp;
  one annual fiber arts public exhibition. These are the civic utilization outputs that
  justify the budget line at annual review.

**Year 2 sustainability indicators:**
- Active participant count: 80–150 unique participants per year
- Class fill rate: 70%+ average across all offered programming
- Per-participant cost: $350–$700/year (comparable to municipal recreation program costs
  per participant)

A civic fiber arts center that cannot reach 80 active participants per year in a city
of 25,000+ is either under-programmed, under-marketed, or serving a population without
sufficient interest. If year-2 utilization is low, diagnose the cause before the budget
review, not after.

| Design | Capital | Annual operating | Funding path |

Small-city weaving capital ranges from $15,000 (solo architectural textile operator)
to $90,000 (full civic fiber arts center fitout). Annual operating budgets are higher
than at town scale because small-city operator wages are higher ($62,000/yr for a
full-time coordinator or cooperative master weaver, SCALES.md §3) and studio rents
in small-city light commercial or arts-district zones are typically $1,200–$2,000/month.
The civic fiber arts center's annual operating cost ($53,000–$82,000) is primarily
coordinator salary plus programming costs; at 10,000 households it is $5.30–$8.20/hh/yr,
easily justifiable.

## Open risks at small-city scale

**Commission pipeline dependency for architectural textile.** Weave-003's market viability
is entirely dependent on institutional commissions from architects, interior designers,
and public-art programs. This is not a stable repeat-purchase market; each commission
must be actively sourced. A year without a committed commission is a year of zero
market revenue. Maintain a 12-month operating reserve before launching; budget for
commission gaps in years 1–3.

**Studio rent at small-city rates.** Light-industrial and arts-district studio space
in small cities costs $1,200–$2,500/month for the 80–150 m² footprints that tapestry
studios and production cooperatives require. This is the largest fixed overhead item
and the hardest to reduce in a bad revenue year. A cooperative that secures below-market
studio space through a civic partnership or arts district subsidy has a structural
advantage that should be locked in at founding; do not rely on market-rate renewal.

**Coordinator retention for civic center.** The civic fiber arts center depends on a
qualified full-time coordinator whose skills are portable. A coordinator who builds
a successful program is more attractive to peer institutions than they were when hired.
Retention strategy: above-median salary within the civic budget, professional-development
support (conference travel, continuing education), and explicit public recognition of
the program's outcomes in annual reporting. A coordinator who sees a career pathway
within the organization stays; one who sees a ceiling leaves.

**Multiple cooperative organizations competing for participants.** A small city that
simultaneously has a tapestry cooperative (weave-001), a production cooperative (weave-013),
and a civic fiber arts center (weave-010) is drawing from an overlapping participant
pool. The 1,125-member pool is large enough for all three to reach break-even; the
risk is coordination failure rather than participant shortage. Define complementarity
before launch: the civic center is the entry point, the production cooperative is
the professional trajectory, and the tapestry cooperative serves the artisan segment.

## Scale-appropriate framing

Small-city scale is the only scale at which all three lenses produce viable weaving
designs simultaneously in the CERES catalog. The cooperative lens is universal (15/15).
The market lens remains at 4 winners — a ceiling set by the structural economics of
hand-weaving in competition with industrial production, not by population. The civic
lens produces one robust winner (weave-010) whose per-household cost is so low at
small-city scale that it is fiscally trivial relative to other civic investments.

The defining finding from DECLINE-VERDICT that governs all three scales: commodity
cloth production is falsified. The evidence is unambiguous, cross-cultural, and admits
no exceptions. What small-city scale adds to village and town scale is not a broader
range of viable products; it is a deeper institutional infrastructure capacity — larger
cooperative member pools, more credible civic investment cases, and a commission-client
density that makes high-capital specialty entries (architectural textile, tapestry
studio) operationally realistic.

The small-city weaving ecosystem at its most developed: a civic fiber arts center
(weave-010) that seeds the participant base; a tapestry studio cooperative (weave-001)
that provides shared access to professional artisan infrastructure; a production
cooperative (weave-013) that converts skill to livelihood; and one or two market-primary
practitioners (weave-003, weave-004) whose commission work extends the trade's reach
into institutional and premium consumer markets. These organizations require active
coordination to function as an ecosystem rather than as competing institutions; that
coordination does not self-organize and must be designed explicitly at founding.
