# ── OPERATOR PROFILE ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

operator_skill_floor: master-weaver
# Per weaving SCHEMA §5 (master-weaver tier): required for large-scale
# architectural commission work. The master-weaver tier is specified for
# weave-003 in the SCHEMA §5 note. Architectural commission weaving requires:
# (a) custom pattern design integrated with architectural specification
# (no standard pattern; each commission requires a custom threading and
# tie-up design), (b) wide-format loom management (warp-tension control
# across 180 cm is a distinct skill from standard-width weaving; errors
# propagate across the full width and are visible at installation scale),
# (c) client-brief interpretation (translating an architect's specification
# into a weave structure, color palette, and material specification that
# meets visual, acoustic, and durability requirements), and (d) large-
# format quality assessment (distinguishing a visible weft-float error
# at 0.5 m viewing distance from an invisible one at 3 m installation
# height). A journeyman weaver cannot perform these functions independently
# without direct master supervision; this entry is not apprentice-only-viable.
# Per REQUIREMENTS: operator_skill_floor: master-weaver required for weave-003.

operators_concurrent: "1-3"
# Scale-dependent. Single-operator studio at 60 m² footprint and one loom:
# feasible for standard architectural commissions. Two-operator studio at
# 80–100 m² with two looms: increases throughput and allows commission
# overlap. Three-operator studio approaches the upper bound for a non-
# production architectural practice (three looms would require ~120 m²
# footprint; see footprint_m2 note). For sim_params, single-operator
# baseline is used.

apprentice_friendly: true

apprentice_path:
  training_stages: >
    Stage 1 (0–6 mo): loom mechanics and safety; warp calculation and winding
    for standard widths (up to 90 cm); plain-weave and twill cloth on a 4-shaft
    floor loom; color theory for textile work; fiber identification and
    properties. Role is studio assistant throughout: yarn preparation, bobbin
    winding, finishing tasks, studio maintenance.
    Competency gate: can independently wind and dress a 4-shaft floor loom to
    90 cm width, produce a consistent twill cloth sample meeting studio quality
    standards, and identify common fiber types by feel and burn test.
    Stage 2 (6–18 mo): 8-shaft floor loom setup and operation; standard pattern
    structures (overshot, M's & O's, networked twill) at standard width (90–120 cm);
    introduction to wide-format warping at 150 cm; design fundamentals for
    pattern repeat and block arrangement; client-communication observation
    (accompanies master on client review sessions without independent client
    contact). Acoustic and structural textile specifications introduced.
    Competency gate: can independently draft and execute a custom 8-shaft
    overshot pattern at 120 cm width meeting the master's quality standard for
    evenness and float consistency; can participate in warp calculation and
    color specification for a standard commission.
    Stage 3 (18–42 mo): wide-format weaving at full 180 cm+ warp width; complex
    pattern structures at architectural scale; commission management under
    master oversight (single commission from design brief to finished panel);
    quality assessment at installation viewing distances; installation preparation
    (finishing, blocking, mounting hardware specification).
    Competency gate: can independently design and execute a single architectural
    commission (acoustic baffle or lobby panel) at full studio quality standard,
    including client brief interpretation, warp calculation, threading design,
    and finished-piece quality sign-off. Journeyman-weaver designation.
    Stage 4 (42–72 mo): full commission cycle management including site visit,
    architectural specification review, material specification, complex pattern
    commissions with client-specific design requirements, installation coordination,
    and quality representation to client and installing contractor.
    Competency gate: can independently manage the full architectural commission
    cycle from initial client brief to installed acceptance; can design and execute
    commissions requiring custom pattern structures not previously woven in the
    studio; master-weaver designation per weaving SCHEMA §5.
  time_to_independent_operation: >
    ~5–6 years (60–72 months) to master-weaver independent operation on the
    full architectural commission cycle. Journeyman-weaver standard (independent
    single-commission production at quality standard, up to 150 cm width) is
    achievable in ~36–42 months. The extended timeline reflects the design-
    interpretation, client-management, and wide-format technical skill components
    of master-tier architectural weaving, not basic weaving proficiency alone.
    Per weaving SCHEMA §5 (master-weaver tier): full technical range across loom
    types; architectural-commission management; design of complex threading sequences.
  succession_note: >
    Apprentice co-presence during production integrates skill transfer into
    daily studio operations: yarn preparation, bobbin winding, and finishing tasks
    performed by the apprentice give continuous exposure to the full commission
    cycle. The long training arc (5–6 years) requires succession planning to begin
    when the master weaver is in mid-career. A studio with no apprentice at Stage 3
    or above has less than three years' succession runway. The master weaver must
    treat apprentice development as a production-priority function embedded in
    normal commission work, not a separate educational program.

## Summary

Weave-003 is a master-weaver studio producing large-scale handwoven textiles for architectural installation: lobby panels, acoustic baffles, curtain walls, and corporate and hospitality commission work. The entry occupies the architectural end of the weaving catalog — the niche where handwoven textile becomes building infrastructure, analogous to forge-010 (Architectural Ironwork Bespoke) in the smithing catalog. The studio operates at the B2B end of the market, selling primarily to general contractors, interior designers, and institutional clients (universities, hospitals, civic buildings) who specify bespoke textile installations in signature spaces. The output is not decorative fabric: it is performance textile — fire-rated, acoustically specified, custom-designed for a particular architectural context — that no commercial mill produces to specification. Unit value is high ($650/m² mid-price vs. $40/m² industrial baseline), volume is low (180 m²/yr from a single-operator studio), and the client relationship is B2B commission, not retail. Capital requirement ($30,000–$110,000) is primarily driven by wide-format floor looms and the humidity-control system required for multi-fiber specialty work. The entry is viable at small-city scale under the market lens; cooperative and civic lenses are poor and marginal respectively, consistent with the premium-B2B market logic.

## Design rationale

No other weaving entry in the catalog occupies the architectural commission cell of the design space. Weave-001 (Tapestry Studio) focuses on wall-hanging tapestry art at gallery scale; weave-002 targets a distinct production segment; weave-004 (Fashion Atelier) targets the garment-and-accessories market. Weave-003 is distinct in three dimensions: (1) its output category — architectural-scale textile panels (lobby panels 4 × 6 m, acoustic baffles in runs of 20–50 m²) designed to be permanently installed in commercial and institutional buildings — is absent from every other catalog entry; (2) its client channel is B2B commission (contractor and institutional client, not gallery buyer or retail customer), requiring a different sales cycle and a different quality-documentation standard (fire-rating certification, NRC acoustic performance data); and (3) its physical plant is defined by wide-format loom capacity (180 cm warp width) and humidity-controlled environment, not by pattern tradition or fiber heritage. The wide-format loom is the physical signature of this distinction: no other weaving entry requires 180 cm warp width, and no standard floor loom configuration supports the dimensional requirements of a full-lobby panel without seaming. This entry directly tests the DECLINE-VERDICT premium-B2B positioning claim for architectural textile: whether a handwoven panel at $650/m² can compete for the same commission against $40/m² commercial wallcovering when performance specifications (fire rating, acoustic NRC, dimensional stability) and design differentiation (custom woven pattern vs. printed pattern) are the decision criteria.

## Historical lineage

The Bauhaus weaving workshop (1919–1933) is the primary functional precedent for this entry, interpreted anti-romantically. The Bauhaus weavers — Anni Albers, Gunta Stölzl, Otti Berger — were not primarily producing decorative art; they were producing structural textile specifications for a new architectural vocabulary [Bauhaus Dessau Foundation documentation]. Their innovation was the integration of weave structure with spatial and performance requirements: developing fabrics with specified light-filtering, acoustic, and thermal properties; designing for the industrial production technologies of their era; and treating the loom as a design instrument for building materials, not a craft appliance for ornamental fabric. The functional inheritance this entry carries forward is that analytical approach: a woven panel for a corporate lobby is designed to specification (acoustic absorption at target NRC, fire-rating at Class A, color palette coordinated with the architect's materials schedule), not selected from a catalogue. The Bauhaus weavers operated within an institutional structure (the school, the architecture department, industrial partnerships with Gropius and Mies) that provided a captive commission pipeline and design research infrastructure. Those institutional conditions cannot be reproduced in a small-city studio operating commercially: the modern architectural textile practitioner must develop and maintain a contractor and designer client network through sustained B2B marketing, without the captive institutional context of the Bauhaus. The Nordic tapestry and Scandinavian flat-weave monumental-commission tradition provides a second functional precedent: the Scandinavian practice of large-format narrative tapestry for civic and institutional buildings (parliament halls, university aula, public libraries) demonstrates that handwoven textile at architectural scale has an established market in Western institutional culture [Larsen and Constantine 1981]. The functional inheritance from the Nordic tradition is the legitimacy framework: clients and architects who specify bespoke handwoven textile for civic and institutional buildings are drawing on a century-plus precedent of such commissions in northern European practice. What cannot be reproduced from either historical precedent: the Bauhaus institutional subsidy (school funding rather than market revenue); the Nordic cultural-consensus patronage model (national cultural investment in craft for civic buildings that has no direct US equivalent at the public-funding level); and the historical workshop's access to student labor at effectively zero market cost. The modern US architectural textile practitioner must build the same client relationships and technical capabilities at full market labor cost, without institutional subsidy, in a procurement environment that treats handwoven textile as a luxury specification item rather than a standard building-material category.

## Key assumptions

Capital cost ($30,000–$110,000, mid $65,000) is the entry's most uncertain field. Wide-format floor loom pricing varies significantly by manufacturer, configuration, and condition: a quality used Leclerc or Louet wide-format 8-shaft loom can be acquired for $8,000–$15,000; an equivalent new loom with computer-assisted dobby runs $18,000–$30,000. The mid estimate ($65,000) assumes a new primary loom, a quality used secondary loom, a new commercial HVAC system for humidity control, and a full studio fit-out; a resourceful operator can approach $30,000 by prioritizing secondhand equipment and phasing the HVAC investment. All capital cost figures are structural estimates from equipment-category pricing; [CITATION-NEEDED] flags are applied throughout. The asymmetry of the capital range (mid $65,000 is below the arithmetic mean of $70,000) reflects this secondhand-market potential.

Annual consumables ($22,000) are dominated by specialty yarn at an estimated $100/kg average. This is the most consequential unit assumption: if the commission mix shifts toward lower-cost acoustic synthetic fiber ($60–$80/kg) rather than premium fire-retardant wool ($100–$140/kg), consumables could fall to $16,000–$18,000/yr; if the mix shifts toward luxury natural-fiber commissions (premium silk or heritage wool at $180–$250/kg), consumables could reach $30,000–$35,000/yr. The $100/kg central estimate is calibrated to a mixed architectural-commission portfolio. Key sub-assumption: 162 kg/yr of yarn at 0.9 kg/m² average cloth weight for 180 m² of annual output. Cloth weight is the most uncertain physical parameter; heavier acoustic baffles (1.2–1.5 kg/m²) raise the yarn requirement materially.

Throughput (180 m²/yr) is derived from 0.5 m/day × 180 cm width × 230 operating days × (1 − 0.15 downtime). The 230 operating days assumption accounts for warping setup days (a new 10 m warp requires 2–3 setup days amortized across the production run), holidays, and commission-gap days. The 0.5 m/day central estimate is between the SCHEMA §1 lower bound (0.2 m/day for complex pattern work) and upper bound (1.0 m/day for simpler baffles); the mix of high-complexity lobby panels and simpler acoustic baffles supports the 0.5 m/day central estimate. Market price ($300/$650/$1,500 low/mid/high per m²) is the most contested assumption. The $40/m² industrial baseline is better anchored than the premium multiple; the 7.5–37.5× premium range is structurally argued but not empirically verified with a published price survey. The pricing_validation acknowledges this explicitly.

## Known risks / failure modes

**Regulatory.** Fire-code compliance (ASTM E84 / IBC §803 Class A flame-spread requirement) is the most significant regulatory risk for this entry. An architectural textile studio that cannot document Class A flame-spread compliance for its fiber specifications cannot sell to commercial occupancy markets; the building inspector will reject the installation at final inspection, creating client-delivery failure. Fire-rating compliance requires either specifying fiber types that inherently meet Class A requirements (certain wool, fiberglass, or inherently flame-retardant synthetic blends) or obtaining third-party testing for each new fiber specification. The cost of ASTM E84 testing ($2,000–$5,000 per sample) is significant at studio scale and must be budgeted into commission pricing for untested fiber combinations. Acoustic performance claims (NRC rating per ASTM C423) are not legally required but are expected by institutional specifiers; undocumented acoustic claims create contract disputes. The zoning requirement — commercial or studio-zoning with structural approval for loom floor loads — is an additional regulatory constraint; older commercial buildings may require structural reinforcement for wide-format loom anchor points, adding $5,000–$20,000 to installation cost.

**Labor pipeline.** Master-weaver succession is the primary labor-pipeline risk, mirrored from forge-010. The 5–6 year training arc to master level requires succession planning in mid-career. Architectural textile is a narrow specialization within weaving — the number of master weavers who combine wide-format loom competency, architectural client management, and fire-code specification knowledge is very small in any regional market. External hiring is not a reliable alternative to internal apprenticeship; there is no standard professional network or job board for architectural textile practitioners. The apprentice path described (5–6 years to master, with staged competency gates) is the primary succession mechanism; it requires the master weaver to commit to training as a business function.

**Supply chain.** Single-supplier dependency for specification yarns is the binding supply-chain risk. Architectural commission work often requires a specific fiber with documented performance characteristics (a particular fire-retardant wool blend with a specific NRC co-efficient tested and certified by the fiber manufacturer). If that fiber's supplier is a single industrial spinner, a supply disruption — production shutdown, stock-out, or distribution change — forces the weaver to re-specify the commission from scratch, potentially invalidating previously submitted architectural drawings and requiring new fire-rate testing at $2,000–$5,000/test. Lead time of 45 days (worst-case) for specialty yarn compounds this risk: a 6-month production schedule has two potential supply-disruption windows. Maintaining a 60-day yarn safety stock for active commissions reduces this risk but increases working-capital requirement ($8,000–$12,000 of yarn inventory on hand).

**Market and channel dependency.** The B2B commission channel (contractors and interior designers) creates client-concentration risk: losing two or three major contractor relationships can cut revenue by 50–60% in a studio serving 6–10 active clients. A studio with a single general contractor as the dominant client is in a quasi-captive relationship with that contractor's project pipeline; if the contractor exits the market, retires, or shifts to a different material supplier, the studio's revenue collapses. Geographic market saturation is also a risk: in a small city, the number of high-specification interior renovation projects in any year is limited; a studio may exhaust the local commission pipeline within 3–5 years and require regional or national marketing reach to sustain volume.
