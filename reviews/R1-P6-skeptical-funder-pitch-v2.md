# R1 — P-6 Skeptical Funder Review
## Artifact: CERES Three-Trade Pitch Narrative v2.0

---

### Overall

This is the strongest pitch document CERES has produced, and it earns that assessment by doing something most pitches avoid: naming its own strongest objections and attempting to answer them before the funder can ask. Section 6 is genuinely useful. But the pitch survives its own objections only partially — the responses to Objections A and C are strong; the response to Objection B is honest but incomplete; and the pitch carries a structural risk that Section 6 does not name at all, which is the operator-sourcing problem. I would not write a check on this draft. I would write a check on the revision that closes the three gaps I name below.

---

### What Works

**The pre-registered success criteria structure (§5, "What Success and Failure Look Like") is the best feature in this pitch.** A funder-pitch that defines project failure as inadequate data collection rather than negative economic outcomes — and explicitly calls out the distinction between a "scientific null" (acceptable finding) and "project failure" (process breakdown) — is doing the funder a real service. Most pitches define success as the positive outcome and quietly omit the failure mode. This pitch does the opposite, and the distinction between validated hypothesis, scientific null, and project failure in §5 is the kind of clarity that makes a grant agreement workable.

**The objection-first structure of Section 6 is well-executed.** Placing the strongest objections before the appendix rather than burying them or omitting them is the right move. Objection A (single-trade scope) is answered substantively — the three-trade completion directly addresses it. Objection C (high civic failure rate) is answered correctly — the civic lens correctly filters for civic design intent. The pitch earns credibility from naming these before I raise them.

**The budget tiers (§5) provide meaningful decision-making granularity.** A high version at $1.9M–$3M, mid at $1M–$1.44M, and low at $490k–$700k gives a funder a real menu. The low version is in the program-related investment range for a mid-size foundation; the high version requires a civic-economic-development office or a multi-funder coalition. The budget structure correctly separates equipment, operator wages, operating costs, evaluation, and working capital. The categories are right even if the numbers are estimates.

**The competitive-response analysis (§7) is appropriately calibrated.** The claim that industrial competitors are not the meaningful competitive threat — and that the real threat is labor-market attrition — is correct and specific. The acknowledgment that a cooperative forge in a town of 8,500 is a threat to the regional repair shop 60 miles away, not to a steel mill, is honest about the scope of the market. The labor-attrition risk identification is the most operationally realistic risk the pitch names.

**Finding 1's capital-structure framing is pitch-ready.** "Capital mobilization, not local population density, is the constraint on private-market viability" is a clean, actionable claim for a civic-economic-development funder. It reframes the intervention from "where to place a facility" to "how to structure the capital" — which is exactly where foundation program design can add value.

---

### What Doesn't

**Objection B is answered honestly but not adequately.** The pitch says economic numbers are "order-of-magnitude estimates" and that the pilot's purpose is to replace them with operating data. I accept this framing. But the pitch then uses these estimates as the basis for specific budget figures ($22k–$30k for forge-005, $28k–$45k for forge-003, $30k–$60k for bake-007) and specific performance thresholds ($56,000/year operator take-home at town scale). An order-of-magnitude estimate cannot generate this precision. The error bars on the budget figures should be stated explicitly — not as a range, but as a confidence level. A funder writing a $1M grant based on estimates with, say, ±50% uncertainty at the line-item level is making a very different risk bet than one writing a grant based on ±20% estimates. The pitch should name the uncertainty level of its own numbers rather than presenting ranges as if they were supplier quotes.

**The single most critical unaddressed risk is operator sourcing, and it does not appear in Section 6.** The pitch diagnoses skilled-trades pipeline erosion in §2 as a core problem. The pilot requires journeyman-level operators in smithing, baking, and weaving in 2–3 towns of 2,000–15,000. This is the same population where the pipeline is thin. The pilot selection criteria (§5) require a local economic-development partner and a market gap — but not a verified operator. A pilot that fails because no qualified forge operator could be sourced in the launch window does not produce a "scientific null"; it produces a "project failure" by the pitch's own definitions. Operator sourcing should be Objection E in Section 6, with a specific answer — not a gap the funder's program officer has to identify independently.

**The low-version pilot ($490k–$700k) is structurally under-resourced for its stated purpose.** The pitch says the pilot's value is the data it generates, and that "success is defined as either a validated economic hypothesis or a rigorously documented null." The low version allocates $100k–$130k to evaluation across three trades in one town over 24 months, which is approximately $33k–$43k per trade. This is an evaluation budget appropriate for basic outcome tracking, not for the kind of rigorous documentation that produces publishable findings or a fundable Phase 2. The pitch should either raise the evaluation budget floor in the low version or honestly downgrade the low version's claim from "rigorous documentation" to "preliminary evidence."

**Skin in the game is not addressed.** The pitch proposes that operators receive "stipends and wages" from the pilot budget for 24 months. If the market-primary designs are successful, the operators presumably continue operating commercially after the pilot. But what happens at month 25 if the design is not yet commercially self-sustaining? Does the funder's obligation end? Does the operator absorb the loss? Does the local partner step in? The transition structure from pilot funding to self-sustained operation (or documented closure) is not described. A funder without a clear exit pathway either faces pressure to continue funding indefinitely or faces the reputational cost of pulling support from a partially-viable community facility. The pitch should describe the off-ramp.

**The 20-km market-gap criterion (§5) is defensible but limits pilot geography in ways the pitch does not acknowledge.** The requirement that candidate towns lack operating commercial facilities within 20 km for the target trade(s) is correct for the market-lens hypothesis. But most towns of 2,000–15,000 in the United States and Western Europe have access to artisan-adjacent services within 20 km — a regional artisan baker, a welding and repair shop, a fiber arts studio. This criterion may be testable only in genuinely rural or post-industrial contexts. If the realistic pilot universe is limited to, say, 200–400 qualifying towns nationally, the pitch should say so, because it affects the scalability claim.

### The Question I'd Push On

**"Where are the operators coming from, and what is the pilot contingency plan if operator sourcing fails for one of the three trades before the launch window?"**

This is my strongest objection and the one the pitch does not address. I read every proposal looking for the risk nobody wants to think about. In this pitch, it is operator sourcing. The pitch correctly identifies that the skilled-trades pipeline is thin in the target town-scale communities. It then proposes a 24-month pilot that requires qualified operators in three trades simultaneously. It does not describe a sourcing strategy, a verification step in the pilot selection process, a training-up lead time, or a contingency if sourcing fails. A pilot that launches two trades but cannot source a weaver is not a three-trade pilot; it is a two-trade pilot that spent one-third of its design budget on the third slot. I need to see either a sourcing strategy with named labor markets and realistic lead times, or an explicit acknowledgment that single-trade pilot deployments are a planned contingency — with a budget reallocation showing what the constrained version looks like.
