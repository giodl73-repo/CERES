# R1 — P-1 Market Economist Review
## Artifact: CERES Three-Trade Pitch Narrative v2.0

---

### Overall

The pitch earns real credit for a capital-structure finding I can work with — the claim that viability tracks capital and margin, not population density, is the kind of clean industrial-district result that Alfred Marshall would have appreciated. But the narrative keeps reaching past what the numbers actually prove, and the market-lens stress test I apply to every proposal reveals gaps that a funder's due-diligence team will find before I do.

---

### What Works

**The scale-invariance finding (Section 4, Finding 1) is structurally sound.** If payback period and operator take-home are functions of capital cost, revenue per unit, and throughput — and if those variables don't change with settlement population — then of course viability is scale-invariant within a trade. That's not a modeling artifact, it's basic unit economics, and the pitch is right to call it out. The tables at §4.1 are the cleanest data display in the document.

**The failure-mode honesty is unusually good for a pitch narrative.** forge-001 and forge-014 failing the wage threshold because throughput can't support living-wage take-home is exactly the kind of analysis I want to see. The pitch is explicit that low capital does not equal viable: "the capital is low enough to clear payback, but the throughput is insufficient to pay a living wage." That's the right analysis and it's stated plainly.

**The pilot structure in Section 5 correctly prioritizes market-lens designs.** The instruction that a constrained pilot "should prioritize the market lens to maximize the probability of producing a clean economic signal within 24 months" is sound. The market lens is the cleanest signal because it doesn't depend on cooperative governance performance or municipal political will.

**The cross-trade DECLINE-VERDICT alignment (Section 4, Finding 4)** is convincing as a methodology proof. Stronger demand-recovery signal maps to more market winners: smithing 7, baking 6–7, weaving 4. The gradient holds, and using the pre-registered DECLINE-VERDICT prediction as a validation test is legitimate.

**Objection B (data quality) is answered honestly.** The pitch says explicitly: the numbers are order-of-magnitude estimates, the pilot's job is to replace them with operating data, the funder is funding the generation of evidence. That's the right framing — don't overstate the claim of the estimate.

---

### What Doesn't

**The baking win-rate inconsistency is not explained.** At §4.1, baking shows 7 market winners at village scale, 6 at town scale, 5 at small-city scale. This breaks the scale-invariance pattern the pitch presents as a key finding. The pitch calls these "marginals reflecting tighter wage thresholds at higher scales" — but it does not explain why the wage threshold tightens with scale. If the market lens holds scale-invariant in smithing and weaving, why does baking diverge? A real funder's analyst will find this immediately. The pitch needs a clean explanation or the scale-invariance finding needs to be qualified more carefully for baking.

**The premium-pricing assumption does all the work, and the pitch does not defend it.** The seven smithing market winners are described as concentrated in "repair and specialty niches." The seven baking winners are in "high-margin categories: artisan bread with premium provenance positioning, specialty pastry, and farm-direct product lines." The four weaving winners are in "luxury, specialty, heritage, and custom-commission niches." In every case, market viability depends on customers paying premium prices over industrial alternatives. The pitch cites no market evidence for these premiums. It cites no customer willingness-to-pay data. It cites no existing comparable operations reporting these margins. The DECLINE-VERDICT documents make theoretical arguments; the pitch asserts the premiums will materialize. This is not market analysis; it is demand assumption.

**The pilot site criteria implicitly rest on a market-gap assumption that needs supporting.** The pitch specifies "candidate towns should not already have operating commercial facilities within 20 km for the target trade(s)" because "the market lens's wage threshold assumes a market gap." This is correct — but it means the pilot results will not generalize to towns that do have nearby competition. A substantial fraction of American towns of 2,000–15,000 residents are within 20 km of a competitor in at least one of the three trades. The usable pilot universe may be smaller than implied.

**The low-version budget ($490k–$700k) is inconsistent with "meaningful economic signal."** The pitch says the pilot's value is the data it generates. But the low-version budget assigns only $100k–$130k to evaluation — while also funding three operating facilities in one town with operator stipends. At $40–65k of evaluation spend per trade, this is not an evaluation budget capable of producing publishable-quality outcome data. The pitch should either defend the evaluation rigor at that budget level or acknowledge that the low version generates preliminary evidence, not a clean economic signal.

**The competitor counter-move analysis (Section 7) is too sanguine.** The labor-market competition risk is correctly identified — a trained smith or baker leaving for a higher-paying employer after 2–3 years is the real threat. But the pitch then says this is "addressed" by pilot outcome measures and succession provisions. Succession provisions in catalog entries don't address the labor market. The pilot measuring operator retention through 24 months doesn't prevent attrition; it only documents it. The economic risk of losing a trained operator to a higher employer — and the cost of replacing them — needs to appear in the budget model, not just the risk register.

---

### The Question I'd Push On

**"What is the actual customer willingness-to-pay evidence for the premium-pricing assumption that underlies all three trades' market winners?"**

The entire market viability finding rests on the assumption that customers in towns of 2,000–15,000 residents will pay premium prices for artisan products over industrial substitutes. The pitch invokes this assumption for smithing repair, artisan bread, and heritage weaving — but provides no market research, no comparable-operation revenue data, and no consumer survey evidence. The DECLINE-VERDICT documents identify demand-recovery signals theoretically; they do not establish that the customers with willingness-to-pay at premium margins actually exist in sufficient density at town scale. Before I write a check, I need something stronger than "industrial alternatives can't replicate this product profile." Who buys this, at what price, in what quantity, in a town of 8,500 people? Show me one comparable operation running the numbers.
