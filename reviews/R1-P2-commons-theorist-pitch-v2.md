# R1 — P-2 Commons Theorist Review
## Artifact: CERES Three-Trade Pitch Narrative v2.0

---

### Overall

The cooperative viability finding — that coop paths open wide at town scale and are closed at village scale — is structurally correct and reflects the kind of member-pool arithmetic Ostrom would have demanded before any commons design advances. My concern is that the pitch treats "cooperative break-even member count" as the only governance question, when it is only the first; a well-funded pilot that hits break-even membership but fails on governance design will produce misleading data about whether the cooperative path actually works.

---

### What Works

**Finding 2's treatment of the village ceiling is exactly right.** The pitch is clear: a feasible pool of 31 members at village scale (2.5% of 1,250) cannot sustain any design requiring more than approximately 15 break-even members. Two of 15 designs clear at village scale in each trade. This is honest. Many cooperative advocates would fight this finding; the pitch states it as a structural result and uses it to direct pilots toward town scale. That discipline reflects Ostrom's insistence on matching governance scope to realistic participation pools.

**The town-scale opening result (§4.2) is well-framed.** A feasible pool of 213 members at town scale unlocking 15 of 15 smithing designs, 11 of 15 baking designs, and 14 of 15 weaving designs is a genuine finding. The pitch correctly interprets this as shifting the question from "can cooperatives work?" to "which cooperative design matches the community's goals and organizational capacity?" — which is precisely where the interesting governance work begins.

**forge-003 is cited with specific governance language.** The pitch notes that forge-003 addresses "Ostrom principles 1–6" in its governance structure and includes a mandatory apprentice slot. Citing specific Ostrom principles by number is the right level of specificity for a pitch-document reference; it tells a governance-literate reader that the underlying catalog entry has engaged seriously with the design-principles literature.

**The pitch acknowledges that the cooperative and civic paths are structurally complementary.** The observation that "a civic makerspace or training facility trains operators who may go on to staff market or cooperative shops in the same community" is a legitimate commons-theory claim: civic infrastructure reduces the startup cost of subsequent cooperative formation by lowering the skill-acquisition barrier.

---

### What Doesn't

**The pitch counts member-pool math as governance, which it is not.** Break-even membership is a feasibility condition, not a governance design. A cooperative forge with 213 potential members at town scale still needs: rules for booking time under contention, a process for handling tool damage, a mechanism for dues adjustment when membership falls, graduated sanctions for violations, and a dispute-resolution pathway that doesn't require legal counsel. None of these appear in the pitch. The claim that cooperative viability "opens wide" at town scale is only true of the financial threshold; it says nothing about whether the governance design can sustain the cooperative once formed.

**The pitch recommends forge-003 as a pilot design without specifying which governance gaps remain.** The pitch says forge-003 addresses "Ostrom principles 1–6" — but Ostrom has eight principles, and the pitch does not say which two are missing or why. For a cooperative pitch to a foundation interested in funding cooperative infrastructure, the unaddressed principles matter. Principles 7 (state recognition of the right to organize) and 8 (nested enterprises for larger systems) are the two most commonly omitted in small-cooperative designs. If forge-003 is missing these, the pilot should note that monitoring the governance implications of those gaps is part of the evaluation design.

**The 2.5% member-pool formula is cited but not defended in the pitch.** The pitch references `corpus/program/SCALES.md §5` for the feasible-pool formula. But a funder reading the pitch will not have access to that file. The pitch should note the basis for the 2.5% figure: is it drawn from comparable cooperative formation data? Tool-library membership rates? Rural electric cooperative participation rates? Without a brief sourcing note, a skeptical reader can challenge the entire cooperative viability finding by challenging this one number.

**Cooperative break-even figures are cited only for smithing, not baking and weaving.** The pitch gives specific break-even member counts for smithing cooperative designs (forge-003 at 60-member break-even). The baking and weaving sections do not provide equivalent specificity — they name the winning entries but not the break-even thresholds that make them winners. For a pitch claiming a "robust, multi-trade finding for any foundation interested in funding cooperative economic infrastructure," the cross-trade governance details should match the smithing level of specificity.

**The pilot design table (§5) recommends cooperative-lens pilots but does not specify governance infrastructure investment.** The table lists forge-003 and weave-009 as cooperative-primary pilot designs. But the budget tables that follow do not include a governance-design or governance-support line item. Ostrom's commons can fail even with correct member-pool sizing if governance is underdesigned. The evaluation and coordination budget ($120k–$160k in the mid version) presumably covers data collection; it does not appear to cover governance design support for the cooperative pilots.

---

### The Question I'd Push On

**"What is the governance specification for each cooperative-primary pilot design — the actual rules for booking, damage, sanctions, and membership changes — and how will the pilot evaluate whether those governance arrangements are adequate?"**

The pitch proves that the cooperative path is financially open at town scale. It does not prove that the governance arrangements required to keep a cooperative operating are in place or testable. A pilot that hits break-even membership but collapses at month 18 due to governance failure — a disputed tool damage incident, a member-fee dispute, an active-minority capture of the booking system — will produce data saying "the cooperative path failed" when what actually failed was governance design. To generate clean evidence about whether the cooperative path works, the pilot must specify governance arrangements before launch, not discover them after the first conflict.
