# R1 — P-3 Civic Steward Review
## Artifact: CERES Three-Trade Pitch Narrative v2.0

---

### Overall

The civic argument in this pitch is the most surprising strong point — the per-household cost comparisons are the clearest quantitative case in the document, and the library benchmark ($100–137/hh/year vs. $1–15/hh/year for civic artisan facilities) is exactly the kind of council-meeting number that actually moves a vote. But the pitch leaves the political coalition unnamed and the staffing model un-costed, which are the two things that will kill a civic proposal at the council level, regardless of how strong the per-household arithmetic is.

---

### What Works

**The library benchmark comparison (§4.3) is the pitch's most fundable paragraph.** A civic forge at $4.23/hh/year and a civic bakery or fiber arts center below $15/hh/year against a library baseline of $100–137/hh/year is a specific, sourced, council-ready comparison. This is Jane Jacobs with a budget: the civic case is not "wouldn't it be nice" but "this is one-tenth the cost of a library and produces a different category of civic infrastructure." The IMLS FY 2021 source citation elevates this from assertion to evidence.

**The distinction between civic-designed and market-primary designs (§6, Objection C) is exactly right.** The pitch's explanation of why most designs fail the civic lens is correct and useful for a civic funder: designs were not built for civic use, and correctly fail a lens that requires public-access hours. A private bakery does not pass the civic lens because it sells bread. This prevents the category error that would make the civic finding look weaker than it is.

**The civic diagnostic narrative (§4.3)** — explaining that the Plan D all-fail result was a data-authoring gap in `annual_public_use_hours`, not genuine civic unviability — is the right disclosure for a funder's due-diligence reader. Transparent identification and correction of a data error builds more credibility than silence would.

**The civic-and-market complementarity claim (end of §4.3)** is a strong civic argument. A civic training forge or civic bakery that produces operators who then staff market or cooperative shops is precisely the kind of multiplier effect that justifies civic investment. The civic facility is infrastructure for private economic activity, which is the Jacobs argument in compressed form.

**The pilot selection criterion requiring "willingness to accept a null outcome" (§5)** is politically realistic. Any civic partner that will only accept positive outcomes should not receive pilot funding — this is the correct position and stating it as a filter, not a hedge, is direct.

---

### What Doesn't

**The political coalition for civic pilots is unnamed.** The pitch identifies "a municipal development office" or "civic-economic-development foundation" as the funder archetype but never says who inside the pilot town would champion a civic forge, civic bakery, or fiber arts center at the council level. Who is the local champion? What objection does the council opposition raise — taxpayer waste on artisan hobbies? Competition with private bakers? Liability at a shared forge? The pitch says "the fiscal case is strong; the constraint is political coalition and civic design intent, not budget arithmetic" — and then stops there. That is precisely where the political analysis should begin.

**The staffing model for civic facilities is absent from the pitch.** Civic facilities require staff. A civic forge with scheduled public-access hours needs someone to open, supervise, maintain, and close the facility. The per-household cost figures for forge-004 ($4.23/hh/year at town scale) and forge-011 ($6.44/hh/year) are simulation outputs — but do they include staff wages? A part-time civic facility manager at even 20 hours/week at $25/hour is roughly $26,000/year in labor cost, which at 8,500 households is approximately $3.06/hh/year on its own. If staff cost is included in the per-household figure, the pitch should say so. If it is not, the civic cost numbers are understated.

**The civic pilot designs in Table §5 have no municipal partner criteria.** The pilot selection criteria specify "a local economic-development partner capable of managing the evaluation process, interfacing with operators, and producing outcome data" — but this appears to be aimed at market-primary and cooperative-primary pilots. A civic-primary pilot (forge-004, bake-010) requires a different kind of institutional partner: a municipality or public institution capable of operating a civic facility, not just evaluating a market enterprise. The criteria should distinguish these.

**The civic externality argument is asserted but not quantified.** The pitch claims that civic artisan facilities generate externalities "the market does not price — apprentice training, local repair culture, emergency production capacity." These are real civic arguments. But the pitch does not attempt to quantify them or compare them to the cost. Emergency production capacity is the strongest of the three — the 2020–2024 supply-chain disruption reference in §2 is appropriate grounding — but it deserves a one-sentence quantification attempt: one supply-chain disruption requiring local production capacity costs a community how much in lost time, expediting costs, or alternative sourcing? Even an order-of-magnitude estimate would strengthen the civic investment case.

**The mid-version pilot budget allocates $120k–$160k to "evaluation and cross-town coordination" without specifying what fraction goes to civic-lens evaluation.** Civic utilization is measurable (person-hours per capita) but requires a different data infrastructure than market or cooperative data. If the evaluation budget is shared across three lenses without specifying how civic utilization is tracked, the pilot design may under-invest in the civic data stream.

---

### The Question I'd Push On

**"Who is the political champion for a civic forge or civic bakery in a specific pilot town of 2,000–15,000, what does the council opposition argue, and why does the proposal win the vote?"**

The per-household arithmetic is strong. The library benchmark comparison will survive a council briefing. But a civic proposal lives or dies on the local political coalition, and this pitch never names one. I need to understand: is there a type of municipality — one with an existing maker culture, a CTE-oriented school board, a rural economic development office that already funds community infrastructure — where this proposal has a realistic political pathway? Or is the plan to find a friendly municipality through the pilot selection process? If the latter, what characteristics make a municipality politically ready for this, and how does the pilot selection criteria operationalize that?
