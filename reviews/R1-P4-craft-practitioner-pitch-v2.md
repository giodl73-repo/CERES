# R1 — P-4 Craft Practitioner Review
## Artifact: CERES Three-Trade Pitch Narrative v2.0

---

### Overall

This pitch is written by someone who has thought seriously about the equipment and the economics, and the cross-trade comparison of market winners by niche type reflects genuine understanding of where each trade's real work lives. But it remains a pitch-level document — nowhere does it reckon with what actually happens on the floor when the operator starts and ends a day, or what breaks first, and I will push on those gaps because they're the ones that collapse a pilot mid-cycle.

---

### What Works

**The throughput-and-wage analysis for failing designs (§4.1) is the most operationally honest passage in the document.** forge-001 and forge-014 fail market not because payback is long but because throughput cannot sustain living-wage take-home. That is exactly the right failure diagnosis — the problem is not the capital cost; it is that a micro-forge cannot produce enough billable work in a working day at realistic customer acquisition rates to pay a skilled tradesperson what the trade is worth. Every small-shop operator I've talked to knows this intuitively; it is good to see it stated explicitly in a catalog methodology.

**The distinction between repair-dominant and commodity designs (§4.4, Finding 4)** is operationally correct. Repair work is judgment-intensive and location-bound in exactly the way the pitch describes. A broken plow cannot be shipped to a factory; neither can a custom gate hinge or a heritage bread recipe. The pitch understands why these niches survive industrial competition — not because of sentiment, but because the work itself resists centralization.

**The "mandatory apprentice slot" citation for forge-003 (§4.2)** shows awareness that single-operator designs without succession are a dead-end. An artisan facility that trains no successors is not infrastructure; it is a temporary service dependent on one person's health and willingness. The apprentice pipeline measurement in the pilot outcomes (§5, outcome 5) is the right metric.

**The 24-month pilot window produces useful operational evidence.** Survival at 24 months is not a trivial test. Equipment failure modes in forge, bakery, and loom environments typically manifest within the first 12–18 months of operation — thermal cycling cracking in induction coils, oven element degradation, loom reed wear. A pilot that tracks what fails and when would produce the first modern empirical record of these failure rates in small-scale community settings.

---

### What Doesn't

**No design in the pitch names what breaks first.** The SKILL.md prompt I work from asks a specific question: "What breaks first? What breaks second? What is the maintenance schedule, and who performs it?" For forge-003 (a coal forge): what is the firepot or air-gate service interval? For bake-010 (neighborhood bakery): what is the deck element or stone surface replacement cycle? For weave-009 (fiber arts tool library): what is the heddle wear rate at high-use throughput? The catalog entries presumably specify these; the pitch should at minimum note that maintenance schedules and consumables budgets are specified for each pilot design. As written, a pilot operator reading this pitch has no idea what they are committing to maintain.

**The coal-forge recommendation for a civic makerspace context (forge-003) is operationally problematic.** forge-003 is described as a "Shared Tool-Library Coal Forge" and is recommended as a cooperative-primary pilot design. Coal forges in shared multi-user environments require specific ventilation infrastructure, coal storage and delivery logistics, and operator skill floors that are higher than induction-electric alternatives. The "urban-compatible regulatory profile" that the pitch notes for forge-015 (induction) is meaningfully absent for forge-003. A town-scale cooperative forge is more likely to be sited in an existing building than a purpose-built facility; the coal ventilation and fuel-storage requirements can make a retrofit prohibitively expensive. The pitch should acknowledge this operational constraint in the pilot design recommendation.

**Operator skill floors are not addressed for any of the three trades.** The pilot assumes operators exist. The pitch notes that the CTE-pathway civic designs (forge-011, bake-011) produce trained operators — but the pilot designs intended for the first 24 months include market-primary and cooperative-primary designs that require operators already at journeyman level in their trade. Where does the pilot source a journeyman smith in a town of 8,500? A trained artisan baker willing to operate a cooperative? A skilled weaver? The pitch mentions "skilled-trades pipeline erosion" as a problem in §2 but does not address how the pilot solves the operator-sourcing problem it has itself diagnosed.

**The pilot budget tables include "operator stipends and wages" but not training costs.** If the pilot must develop operators from earlier stages (apprentice → journeyman), the training period reduces billable throughput and the cost of that reduced throughput should appear in the budget. If the pilot sources operators who are already at journeyman level, the sourcing strategy and the realistic labor market for that sourcing should be described. As written, the budget assumes operators arrive ready to run, which is the one assumption most likely to fail in a rural town of 8,500.

**The weaving market-winner section (§4.1) states "only 4 market winners, consistent with the trade's strongest demand-collapse finding" — but does not address the operational skill floor for those 4 winners.** weave-004, weave-007, weave-008, and weave-013 are described as luxury, specialty, heritage, and custom-commission niches. These are the hardest niches to enter. A heritage-breed wool weaver operating on a multi-shaft or Jacquard loom in a custom-commission context typically requires 5–10 years of skill development. The pitch should acknowledge that the weaving market winners are not accessible to a first-year operator — and that the pilot's 24-month window may not be sufficient to assess whether an operator could reach the skill level required to operate in the market-viable niche.

### The Question I'd Push On

**"How does the pilot source, verify, and retain operators at journeyman-or-above skill level in each of the three trades, in towns of 2,000–15,000 residents, in a region where the trades pipeline has been eroded for decades?"**

The pitch correctly diagnoses that skilled-trades pipeline erosion has left rural communities with a thin labor pool for metalwork, artisan baking, and textile arts. It then proposes a pilot that requires operators at journeyman level in all three trades simultaneously, in 2–3 pilot towns. These are the same towns with the thin pipelines. The pilot's success depends on solving an operator sourcing problem that the pitch's own background section identifies as structurally difficult. I want to know: has CERES identified where these operators currently work? What would it take to attract or relocate them? What is the realistic failure rate on operator sourcing, and what does the pilot look like if one of the three trades cannot recruit a qualified operator in time for the launch window?
