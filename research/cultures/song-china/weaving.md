# Weaving in Song-Era China (ca. 960–1279)

## 1. Period and Geography

The Song dynasty (960–1279) governed one of the most sophisticated textile economies
in the pre-industrial world, eventually divided between the Northern Song (960–1127,
centered on the Yellow River basin and the North China Plain) and the Southern Song
(1127–1279, centered on the Yangtze valley, Jiangnan region, and southern provinces)
after the Jurchen Jin dynasty seized the north. The center of gravity of silk production
shifted southward with the political center: the Jiangnan region — particularly the
prefectures of Suzhou, Hangzhou, and Huzhou — became the dominant silk-weaving zone
under the Southern Song and retained that position through the subsequent dynasties
[Kuhn 1988; CITATION-NEEDED: specific Jiangnan silk-weaving geography, Southern Song
period; Shiba 1970 may provide partial coverage].

The geographic conditions for silk production required both specific agricultural
ecology — mulberry cultivation for silkworm feeding, reliable water for retting and
washing — and sufficient population density to support the labor-intensive sericulture
and weaving operations. The Jiangnan region provided both. The Pearl River delta in
Guangdong had its own sericulture traditions by the Southern Song period, but the
Jiangnan cluster was the administrative and prestige center of luxury-silk weaving
throughout this era [CITATION-NEEDED: Guangdong silk production Song period; comparison
of Jiangnan vs. southern sericulture scale under Southern Song].

Beyond silk, the Song textile economy encompassed ramie (*zhuma*, Boehmeria nivea),
the dominant bast-fiber textile for everyday use; hemp (*dama*) for coarser goods and
rope; and the early expansion of cotton (*mianhua*) from its coastal and southern
footholds. Cotton cultivation in China was not yet widespread in the Song period; it
spread substantially under the Yuan (1271–1368) and became dominant only under the
Ming (1368–1644) [Bray 1997, ch. 6; CITATION-NEEDED: Song-period cotton cultivation
geography and scale; Chao 1977 provides context on cotton expansion trajectory].
The fiber hierarchy for Song-period weaving, in descending order of prestige and
economic value, was: silk, ramie, hemp, (early) cotton — essentially the inverse of
the Ming-era hierarchy once cotton displaced ramie for everyday wear. This ordering
matters for understanding what "a weaver" in Song China made and for whom.

The Song population has been estimated at 60–100 million by the mid-dynasty
[CITATION-NEEDED: Song population estimates, variance and basis; Gernet 1962 provides
general framework]. The upper bound is structurally significant: it implies a textile
demand of scale comparable to early modern Europe in aggregate, but concentrated
in a single imperial administrative system, with consequences for how that demand was
organized and satisfied. Per-capita textile consumption figures are not recoverable from
the sources consulted; order-of-magnitude reasoning only is appropriate for downstream
synthesis.

## 2. The Weaver's Place

The social position of the Song weaver was not uniform across the three structurally
distinct production forms that coexisted within the same empire: state imperial
workshops, rural household producers, and urban commercial workshops. Treating these
as variants of a single "weaver" role misreads the economic and social history.

**State imperial workshops** (*guansi*, 官司) produced silk textiles for court use,
official ceremonial dress, and diplomatic tribute. These workshops were concentrated
in the capital and in Hangzhou under the Southern Song. Workers in state workshops
were drawn from registered artisan households (*jianghu*, 匠户) — a hereditary
registration system that bound households to their craft assignment across generations.
The *jianghu* system meant that "weaver" status at the imperial workshop level was not
a voluntary occupational identity but an inherited administrative category, with
obligations to supply labor to the state at regulated intervals [CITATION-NEEDED:
*jianghu* weaving-household obligations, Song dynasty; the system is better documented
for Yuan and Ming; Shiba 1970 or Golas 1999 may provide Song-period coverage].
Social prestige within this system was ambiguous: proximity to the court conferred
a kind of visibility, but freedom of movement and occupational choice were constrained.

**Rural household weavers** produced ramie and hemp cloth primarily as tax-in-kind
obligations. The silk-cloth tax (*juanfu* or silk-tribute component of the *liangshui*
two-tax system) required farming households in silk-producing regions to deliver a
defined quantity of silk or other cloth to the state as part of their tax liability
[CITATION-NEEDED: Song *liangshui* (two-tax) system and its textile-tribute component;
von Glahn 2016 ch. 5 for general fiscal framework; specific administrative structure
of textile tribute needed]. This was not voluntary production for market; it was
compelled production meeting a state claim. Households that could not produce enough
cloth to satisfy the tax obligation faced fines or forced sales of assets — a
structural coercion that the "cottage silk industry" framing frequently obscures
[CITATION-NEEDED: documented cases of peasant household textile-tax distress in Song;
general framework in Gernet 1962].

Gender was a defining feature of rural household weaving. Women operated the loom as
a matter of social expectation and practical organization; the classical Chinese dyad
of *nangeng nüzhi* (男耕女织, "men plow, women weave") described the normative
division of agricultural household labor. In the weaving household, the adult women
managed sericulture (silkworm cultivation, mulberry leaf collection), silk reeling,
and loom operation. Children — including young children — assisted with silkworm
handling; children's small hands were preferred for picking silkworm cocoons and
managing pupae without damaging silk filaments [CITATION-NEEDED: child labor in Song
sericulture; Kuhn 1988 on sericulture labor organization]. This child-labor
component was structural, not incidental.

**Urban commercial workshops** employed hired weavers producing silk for the merchant
market. By the Song period, merchant-financed silk production was well-established in
the Jiangnan cities; silk merchants (*sibo*, 丝帛 merchants) advanced thread and sometimes
looms, collected finished cloth, and distributed through urban markets and eventually
export networks [CITATION-NEEDED: Song silk merchant organization; Shiba 1970 is the
key source on Song commerce; specific silk merchant putting-out system details needed].
Urban hired weavers were nominally free wage workers, but merchant capital mediated
their access to raw material and market — a putting-out structure that limited their
practical economic autonomy even when their legal status was formally free.

**Draw-loom operation** for complex figured silk required a two-person team: the weaver
at the treadles and a second person — typically called the *huazi* or "draw boy" —
who operated the pattern-heddle-lift mechanism above the loom. This was skilled,
coordinated, physically demanding work. The "draw boy" role was often performed by
children or adolescents, given the physical demands (the draw-boy typically sat or
stood above the loom, reaching down to pull pattern cords in sequence); the term
implies a young assistant, though adult draw-workers also existed in large workshops
[Kuhn 1988; CITATION-NEEDED: draw-boy age and conditions, Song or later Ming sources
as proxy; Kuhn 1988 vol. V pt. 9 is the authoritative reference].

The *refined silk culture* image — elegant workshops, skilled artisans expressing
aesthetic sensibility — erases the material conditions of production: tax coercion of
rural households, hereditary artisan registration restricting mobility, child labor
embedded in sericulture and draw-loom operation, and merchant capital structurally
limiting wage-worker autonomy in commercial workshops.

## 3. The Loom

Three loom types coexisted in Song China, occupying different production niches with
distinct mechanical profiles.

**The horizontal treadle loom** (*jizhang*, 机张) was the standard household loom for
plain cloth — ramie, hemp, and plain-weave silk. It operated with multiple treadle-
operated heddles for simple tabby and twill weaves. The treadle loom used foot-operated
pedals connected to heddle frames that raised and lowered warp threads to create the
shed for the weft shuttle. A single weaver could operate it without assistance for
plain weaves. Floor area required: approximately 2–3 m in length by 1–1.5 m in width;
height varied by loom design and shed-opening mechanism [CITATION-NEEDED: Song-period
horizontal treadle loom dimensions; Kuhn 1988 or Needham vol. V pt. 9 for technical
specifications]. Warp preparation — warping, sizing, threading the heddles — was a
separate time-consuming process preceding weaving; on plain cloth looms, sizing the
warp with rice-flour paste stiffened the threads against breakage under treadle-shed
tension [CITATION-NEEDED: Song warp-sizing practice; starch-sizing of silk and ramie
warp, Kuhn 1988].

**The draw loom** (*hua ji*, 花机 or *ti hua ji*, 提花机) was the specialized
instrument for complex figured silk — brocades, damasks, gauzes, and compound weaves
requiring independent control of individual or small groups of warp threads beyond what
shaft heddles could provide. The draw loom used a pattern-heddle system mounted above
the main loom body; cords from individual warp threads or groups passed up through a
template (the *jia*) which, when individual pattern cords were pulled in sequence,
lifted the precise warp configuration required for each row of the pattern. The draw-
boy sitting above the loom pulled the appropriate cords in sequence as directed by the
weaver below; together, one pass of the shuttle per cord pull advanced the pattern
[Kuhn 1988, vol. V pt. 9; CITATION-NEEDED: detailed draw-loom mechanism description
for Song period specifically; Needham 1965 vol. V pt. 1 covers textile technology
broadly]. A single complex figured-silk design required the pattern to be encoded in
the cord arrangement before weaving began — a setup process requiring skilled pattern
reading and cord-threading that was itself a significant labor investment before a meter
of cloth was produced [CITATION-NEEDED: draw-loom setup time and skill requirements;
Kuhn 1988].

Draw-loom dimensions were larger than household treadle looms: the overhead pattern-
heddle rack added 1–2 m of height above the weaving surface, and the machine length
was commonly 3–4 m or more [CITATION-NEEDED: draw-loom physical dimensions, Song or
Ming sources; Kuhn 1988 or technical archaeology source]. This scale meant draw looms
were not household instruments in the same sense as plain treadle looms; they were
installed in dedicated workshop spaces.

**The backstrap loom** was used in rural and peripheral contexts for narrow cloth,
primarily hemp and rough ramie for local use. Its capital requirement was minimal — a
back strap, stick heddles, and a shed stick — but its productive capacity was
correspondingly low. Cloth width was limited to arm-span distance (typically 40–60 cm),
and patterning possibilities were elementary. The backstrap loom was the lowest-capital-
entry form and is not the instrument associated with Song luxury textile production
[CITATION-NEEDED: backstrap loom use in Song-period China; geographic distribution in
southern and peripheral regions].

**Humidity and temperature control** were material constraints for silk weaving that
are absent from bast-fiber weaving. Silk filament is hygroscopic; in low-humidity
conditions it becomes brittle and breaks during warping and weaving. High humidity
conversely causes thread to swell and bind. The Jiangnan climate — humid subtropical,
with high summer humidity — was not accidental to the region's silk dominance; the
ambient humidity was functionally helpful for maintaining silk-thread workability.
Workshop management of thread humidity was a tacit operational knowledge embedded in
siting and seasonal scheduling, not a documented technical parameter in surviving
sources [CITATION-NEEDED: humidity management in Song silk workshops; inference from
Kuhn 1988 and Jiangnan climate data].

## 4. Products

Song China's weaving output divided across several structurally distinct product
categories, each with its own market structure, quality level, and downstream use.

**Plain-weave silk** (*juan*, 绢; *luo*, 罗 for loosely woven open-weave) was the
volume commodity of silk production — simple tabby and twill weaves, used for clothing,
lining, padding, and as currency-substitute in tax payment and wage disbursement.
Plain silk was produced in quantity on household treadle looms; quality varied from
coarse tax-grade cloth to fine merchant-grade material. Its use as a medium of exchange
— silk bolts circulated alongside copper cash as de facto currency — reflects both
its standardized quality and the chronic copper shortage in Song monetary circulation
[CITATION-NEEDED: silk as currency substitute in Song China; von Glahn 2016 on Song
monetary history; Hartwell 1967 on commodity money broadly].

**Figured silks** — brocades (*jin*, 锦), damasks (*ling*, 绫), and gauzes (*sha*, 纱)
with woven patterns — were luxury goods requiring draw-loom production and specialized
weaver skill. These were the prestige export commodities and the court dress materials.
Brocade involved supplementary weft threads in additional colors woven to create the
pattern; the Suzhou and Hangzhou workshops producing brocade for the court and luxury
market occupied the apex of the product hierarchy [CITATION-NEEDED: Suzhou brocade
production, Song imperial procurement; Kuhn 1988 for technical differentiation of
brocade, damask, and gauze weave structures].

**Kesi** (缂丝, tapestry-weave silk) was the most time-intensive and highest-prestige
product of Song weaving. Kesi used discontinuous weft threads of different colors,
building up the design zone by zone using a comb beater rather than a full-width
shuttle. The weft threads did not traverse the full warp width; they reversed at the
color boundary, creating small slits (*ke*, 缂, hence the name, sometimes rendered
"cut silk" but more precisely "woven-through silk"). The technique allowed pictorial
representation of great detail — birds, flowers, calligraphic text — indistinguishable
in effect from painting when executed at the highest skill level. Kesi production was
not scalable: a square foot of fine kesi might represent days of a skilled weaver's
work [CITATION-NEEDED: kesi production rate, Song period; Watt and Wardwell 1997 for
technical description and examples]. Its market was entirely prestige and court;
it was not a volume commodity and should not be treated as representative of Song
weaving economics generally.

**Ramie cloth** (*zhuma bu*, 苧麻布) was the dominant everyday textile for non-elite
households throughout the Song period. Ramie — a bast fiber from *Boehmeria nivea* —
was processed by retting (fiber separation from the stalk), beating, spinning, and
weaving on household treadle looms. The resulting cloth was cooler and less skin-
irritating than hemp, making it preferred for summer wear across the social spectrum
below elite-silk consumption [Bray 1997; CITATION-NEEDED: ramie production and
consumption as everyday textile in Song; geographic distribution of ramie cultivation].

**Hemp cloth** (*hemp bu*, 麻布) was coarser and cheaper than ramie, used for
workwear, sacking, and rope. Hemp cultivation was geographically widespread, and hemp
cloth was a standard component of the tax-in-kind textile obligations from households
in regions unsuited for silk production [CITATION-NEEDED: hemp-cloth tax obligations
in Song fiscal system; von Glahn 2016 ch. 5 for fiscal framework].

**Early cotton cloth** (*mianbu*, 棉布) appeared at the margins of Song textile
production, particularly in coastal Fujian and Guangdong and among minority
populations in the south. It was not yet a mainstream commodity in the Song heartland
[Bray 1997, ch. 6; CITATION-NEEDED: Song-period cotton cloth production and
geographic distribution, scale relative to silk and ramie].

## 5. Labor Regime

The labor regime of Song weaving must be stated explicitly because its structure
bears directly on CERES's falsifier assessment (spec §2: "labor-regime dependency").
The regime differed by production scale and form and cannot be collapsed to a single
description.

**Household tax-labor** was the foundational condition for most ramie and hemp
weaving. Rural households were required by the Song two-tax (*liangshui*) system to
deliver cloth as part of their tax obligation, in addition to or in lieu of cash
payments. The quantity and fiber type were specified by administrative quota, varying
by region and household assessment. This was not market-responsive production; it was
compelled production against a state claim. Households that failed to deliver faced
penalties. The labor that produced this cloth — overwhelmingly women's labor — was
economically invisible in the sense that it was neither waged nor costed in surviving
accounts; historical calculations of cloth production costs that exclude this labor
are structurally misleading [CITATION-NEEDED: Song *liangshui* textile component
specifics; administrative sources or secondary account of textile-tribute structure;
von Glahn 2016; Gernet 1962 ch. on daily life and taxation].

**Artisan household registration** (*jianghu*) bound weaving households in imperial
workshop districts to hereditary craft obligations. These households supplied labor
to state workshops on a rotational basis — typically a defined number of months per
year — and were not free to change occupation or residence without administrative
permission. The quality of artisan registration varied: households producing prestige
court silk operated under tighter oversight and more regularized expectations than
general artisan households [CITATION-NEEDED: *jianghu* weaving-household obligations
specific to silk production, Song period; system description in Elvin 1973 ch. on
artisan organization; full treatment needed].

**Hired labor in commercial workshops** was the form most proximate to modern wage
employment. In the Jiangnan commercial silk workshops — the large-scale merchant-
financed operations producing for domestic luxury markets and export — weavers were
hired at day or piece rates. The merchant capitalist (often also a silk dealer) owned
or advanced the raw material and might own the loom; the weaver supplied skilled
labor. This was a classic proto-industrial putting-out arrangement in its credit
structure even when physically concentrated in a single workshop space
[CITATION-NEEDED: Jiangnan commercial weaving workshop labor organization, Song
period; Shiba 1970 on Hangzhou merchant organization; whether piece-rate or day-rate
was standard is unresolved — source needed].

**Child labor** was structural at two points in the silk production chain: in
sericulture (children's small hands handled cocoons and pupae without damaging
the silk filament) and in draw-loom operation (the "draw boy" above the loom pulled
pattern cords in sequence). These were not peripheral or exceptional uses of child
labor; they were embedded in the technical organization of production itself. The
draw-loom pattern mechanism was partially designed around the expectation of a small-
bodied operator working above the loom frame [Kuhn 1988; CITATION-NEEDED: age and
conditions of draw-loom assistants; inference from mechanical description and Chinese
agricultural practice; Kuhn 1988 as primary technical source].

**Physical conditions** were not mitigated by the elegance of the product. Sericulture
required sustained attention during silkworm hatching and growth — essentially
continuous monitoring for several weeks, around the clock during critical periods.
Loom operation involved sustained seated posture, repetitive treadle motion, and
— for draw-loom operation — coordination under time pressure between two workers.
Silk thread was fine enough that breakage, knot repair, and rethreading were constant
interruptions; shed-error correction in figured silk required backing out and
re-establishing the pattern sequence, which could destroy hours of work if mistakes
were not caught immediately [CITATION-NEEDED: weaving error rates and correction
practice, general loom-operation literature; Kuhn 1988 for technical basis].

## 6. Supply Chain

Song silk weaving was not supply-chain autonomous. The upstream dependency chain
was long and could not be separated from the weaving process itself.

**Sericulture as an inseparable upstream dependency.** Silk thread required
silkworm cultivation as its absolute upstream precondition; unlike bast fibers (ramie,
hemp) or wool, raw silk could not be obtained from plant or animal husbandry that
was separable from intensive labor management. The silkworm lifecycle — from egg
hatching through four larval instars to cocoon spinning — took approximately 30–35
days and required controlled temperature (typically 22–28°C for optimal cocoon
yield), regular mulberry-leaf feeding, and monitoring for disease [CITATION-NEEDED:
Song sericulture technical specifications; temperature ranges and feeding cycles in
Kuhn 1988 or Bray 1997 agricultural chapters; *Nongshu* of Chen Fu (1149) as Song
primary source on sericulture]. The mulberry cultivation preceding silkworm rearing
required its own land, water, and labor investment. A silk-weaving operation that
did not also manage or contract sericulture had to purchase raw cocoons or reeled
silk thread at market prices, exposing it to supply volatility. In the household
production model, sericulture and weaving were performed by the same household;
in commercial workshop production, silk thread was purchased from specialized
silk-reelers or merchant intermediaries [CITATION-NEEDED: Song silk-thread market
structure; relationship between sericulture households and commercial weavers].

**Silk reeling** (*zhisi*, 治丝 or *saosi*, 缫丝) converted cocoons into continuous
silk filament. This required immersing cocoons in hot water (approximately 60–80°C)
to dissolve the sericin binding the filament, locating the filament end, and drawing
the filament from multiple cocoons simultaneously onto a reel while maintaining even
tension. Reeling required skilled hand-feel to produce even thread; uneven reeling
produced slubby thread that wove poorly. Reeling was typically done by women in
the same household that managed sericulture, or by specialist reelers in commercial
thread markets [CITATION-NEEDED: silk reeling technology and labor, Song period; Kuhn
1988 on reeling machinery development; whether mechanical reeling frames (compared to
hand reeling) were widespread in Song is an open technical question].

**Degumming** (*jingsuo*, refinement) was a finishing process that removed residual
sericin from woven silk cloth, producing the characteristic lustrous drape of finished
silk. It involved immersion in alkaline solution (typically ash-water or later soap-
water) and gentle kneading. Degummed silk lost 20–30% of its original weight —
a commercially significant loss that was built into pricing structures [CITATION-NEEDED:
degumming practice and weight loss in Song silk finishing; Kuhn 1988 for technical
description].

**Dyestuffs** were an essential supply-chain component for commercial and luxury
silk. The major colorants in Song China included: safflower (*hong hua*, 红花) for
red and pink; indigo (*lan*, 蓝, from *Indigofera tinctoria* and related species)
for blue; yellow from gardenia, turmeric, or weld species; black from iron-tannate
mordanting with gall-nut sources; and lac insect (*zizhi*, 紫胶) for purple and deep
red [CITATION-NEEDED: Song dyestuff sources and trade; Kuhn 1988 for dye chemistry
context; specific Song dyestuff market geography needed]. Mordants — alum, iron salts —
were also required for fixing many dyes on silk. The dyestuff supply chain was
geographically extended: some dyes were imported from Central and Southeast Asia
through the maritime trade routes and the Silk Road [CITATION-NEEDED: imported
dyestuffs in Song China, maritime trade sources; Wang Zhenping or maritime trade
history sources].

**Merchant distribution** of luxury silk organized export along the Silk Road
overland routes and the maritime Silk Road to Southeast Asia, the Persian Gulf, and
East Africa. Song maritime trade (*haishang sichou zhi lu*, 海上丝绸之路) was
state-licensed through *shibo si* (market-ship offices, 市舶司) controlling import
and export tariffs [CITATION-NEEDED: Song *shibo si* (Superintendency of Merchant
Shipping) organization and silk-export regulation; Shiba 1970 is the primary source;
Clark 1991 on Song maritime trade for verification]. Silk was the dominant export
commodity; demand for it structured the supply chain backward into the Jiangnan
workshops. The state's fiscal interest in maintaining silk export quality and
quantity created regulatory pressure on production, not just on trade [CITATION-NEEDED:
Song state regulation of silk export quality standards].

## 7. Decline or Restructuring

The trajectory of Song-era silk weaving after the dynastic transition requires careful
framing; the mechanisms of change operated on different timescales and affected
different production forms differently.

**Mongol conquest and the Yuan reorganization** (1271–1368) did not destroy
Chinese silk weaving — the Mongols were active consumers and exporters of luxury silk —
but substantially reorganized it. The Yuan court expanded the *jianghu* artisan
registration system, applying it more systematically to craft households and
concentrating skilled weavers in state workshops servicing Mongol court demand. Some
Jiangnan commercial weaving households were absorbed into this state-controlled form;
others continued under Mongol licensing [CITATION-NEEDED: Yuan *jianghu* weaving-
household system; comparison with Song-period organization; Farquhar 1990 on Yuan
government institutions; specific weaving-workshop treatment needed]. The Yuan period
also saw the large-scale northward deportation of skilled Southern Song artisans to
supply craft labor at the Yuan capital — a coercive reorganization of the craft
labor force [CITATION-NEEDED: Yuan deportation of Song artisans to northern workshops;
primary or secondary account of scale and conditions].

**Cotton's displacement of ramie** under the Yuan and Ming was the major structural
shift in the lower tier of Chinese textile production. The Yuan-period agronomist
Wang Zhen (active ca. 1295–1315) promoted cotton cultivation and the cotton gin (*mian
jiaoche*, 棉搅车) for seed removal; by the mid-Ming, cotton cloth had substantially
displaced ramie and hemp as the everyday textile for non-elite households across most
of China [Bray 1997, ch. 6; CITATION-NEEDED: specific timescale of cotton displacing
ramie in Jiangnan and central China; demographic and fiscal evidence for the
transition]. This was a demand-collapse event for the ramie and hemp weaving
household economy that had been the volume base of Song textile production. Households
that had produced ramie as tax cloth found that cotton produced more cloth per unit
labor input once the cotton gin reduced the bottleneck of seed removal. The Song-era
ramie household weaving economy did not restructure into cotton; it was displaced by
a structurally different fiber system.

**Luxury silk weaving survived and intensified** under the Ming and Qing as a
prestige and export category. The Suzhou and Hangzhou imperial workshops (*zhizao ju*,
织造局) became formalized institutions under Ming and Qing administration, continuing
and in some respects expanding the imperial-workshop silk tradition [CITATION-NEEDED:
Ming-Qing *zhizao ju* continuity with Song imperial silk workshops; Kuhn 1988 or
Nishijima 1984 for Ming textile industry]. The draw-loom technology for figured silk
remained the prestige production instrument for centuries; no mechanically superior
alternative appeared until the Jacquard loom (patented 1804 in France), which
addressed the same mechanical problem — independent warp-thread control for pattern
weaving — that the draw loom had solved a thousand years earlier in China
[CITATION-NEEDED: Jacquard loom as functional parallel to draw loom; whether Jacquard
had any documented knowledge of Chinese draw-loom technology is disputed and should
not be asserted without source].

**Western machine-textiles entered Chinese markets** from the 1840s onward, following
the Opium Wars and the treaty-port system. British and later Japanese machine-woven
cotton cloth progressively undercut hand-woven cotton production in Chinese domestic
markets across the second half of the nineteenth century, producing a demand-collapse
event for hand-woven everyday cloth comparable to the contemporaneous collapse of
hand-weaving in Western Europe [CITATION-NEEDED: machine-textile import competition
effects on Chinese hand-weaving, 1840s–1890s; Feuerwerker 1970 on modern Chinese
economic history; Hamilton and Chang on Chinese cloth markets].

**Luxury hand-woven silk survived** as a distinct prestige category that machine
production could not replicate at equivalent quality for court, ceremonial, and
export markets. Suzhou embroidery and Hangzhou figured silk continued as recognized
artistic-craft forms into the twentieth century and persist today. This survival
reflects a pattern common across luxury textile forms globally: machine production
competed on cost for volume goods but could not displace craft production in
identifiably hand-made prestige categories where the production process itself was
part of the value proposition.

**Verdict**: `mixed`, combining `demand-collapse` (everyday ramie/hemp household
weaving displaced first by cotton, later by machine cloth), `regulatory-dissolution`
(the *jianghu* and imperial workshop system dissolved under the Republic and its
successors), and `restructuring` (luxury figured silk and kesi survived as prestige
niches). The mechanisms operated on different product segments and different timescales.
Task 7 (DECLINE-VERDICT.md) must not conflate these: the household ramie economy,
the imperial workshop silk regime, and the luxury commercial figured-silk sector
had different decline trajectories.

## 8. Functional Requirements Learned

For Plan C (REQUIREMENTS.md), abstracted from this culture:

**Loom type is product-determined.** Plain cloth (tabby, twill) on a treadle loom
can be operated by a single weaver; complex figured silk on a draw loom requires
two operators in coordinated roles. Modern equivalents: shaft-loom weaving vs.
Jacquard or computer-controlled dobby. A CERES catalog entry for a "loom" must
specify product scope; a single loom design cannot span plain household cloth and
figured silk without specifying which mechanical form is being evaluated.

**Fiber properties create distinct workshop constraints.** Silk weaving and bast-
fiber weaving are not variants of the same process; they require different thread
preparation, different ambient conditions, and different finishing processes.
The key silk-specific constraints:
- *Humidity*: silk thread requires ambient humidity in an operational range to
  avoid brittleness (low humidity) or swelling (high humidity). Specific range
  not quantifiable from sources consulted; Jiangnan climate conditions (subtropical
  humid) were functionally enabling [CITATION-NEEDED: operational humidity range
  for silk-thread weaving; experimental textile literature needed].
- *Temperature for sericulture*: silkworm optimal temperature approximately 22–28°C
  [CITATION-NEEDED: silkworm temperature requirements; *Nongshu* of Chen Fu 1149
  or Kuhn 1988 on sericulture]; below ~20°C larval development slows significantly.
- *Degumming weight loss*: 20–30% of silk cloth weight lost in finishing; must be
  priced into production economics [CITATION-NEEDED: degumming weight-loss figure;
  Kuhn 1988 technical basis].

**Sericulture is a non-separable upstream dependency for silk weaving.** A
modern analog of Song household silk production must incorporate or contract the
sericulture operation; it cannot be treated as a commodity input equivalent to
purchased cotton or wool. This dependency is a design constraint, not a cultural
artifact. A modern silk weaving operation that sources reeled thread from a market
supplier has transferred this dependency upstream but not eliminated it.

**Draw-loom pattern setup is a major fixed cost.** Pattern heddle threading for a
new design on a draw loom required skilled labor (pattern reading and cord threading)
before a single weft pass was made. This setup cost was amortized over the production
run; short runs of complex patterns are economically hostile. For a modern catalog
entry, this translates to: the economics of figured-silk weaving are sensitive to
run length. Jacquard-loom setup has an analogous structure (programming the card
or digital pattern is the modern equivalent of draw-loom cord threading).

**Throughput is loom-type and product-dependent, not estimable from general
"weaving" assumptions.** The following estimates are order-of-magnitude reasoning
from production-process analysis; no direct Song-period throughput records are present
in the sources consulted:
- Plain-weave silk on a household treadle loom: approximately 1–3 meters per
  active weaving day, depending on warp width and thread count [CITATION-NEEDED:
  hand-loom plain-silk throughput; this is a soft estimate from general loom-speed
  literature; must be sourced or flagged as estimate before Requirements.md cites it].
- Figured silk on a draw loom: substantially slower; complex brocade at perhaps
  0.2–0.5 meters per day or less depending on pattern density [CITATION-NEEDED:
  draw-loom figured-silk throughput; Kuhn 1988 may provide basis for estimation].
- Kesi: non-comparable; production rate was measured in days per design unit, not
  meters per day.
- These figures are structurally incomparable and must not be averaged into a
  "weaving throughput" without specifying product type.

**Maintenance failure modes.** First-order maintenance failure modes for the draw
loom: heddle-cord wear and breakage (frequent; pattern cords under repeated tension
stress); shuttle wear (low frequency); reed tooth damage (moderate frequency); loom-
frame joint loosening from seasonal humidity cycling (annual). For the treadle loom:
treadle-pivot wear; heddle cord failure; shed-stick warping. Silk-specific: warp
thread breakage from dryness is more frequent than for bast fibers; repair technique
(finding the broken end, tying a weaver's knot, and re-tensioning without distorting
adjacent warps) is a distinct skill component [CITATION-NEEDED: loom maintenance
failure modes; general loom operation literature; Kuhn 1988 for draw-loom specifics].

**Labor-regime falsifier.** The historical economics of Song silk weaving incorporated:
household tax-labor (unpaid women's and children's labor meeting state obligations),
hereditary artisan registration restricting occupational choice, child labor embedded
in sericulture and draw-loom operation, and merchant-capital dependency in commercial
workshops. No modern operation can replicate these conditions ethically or legally.
The historical cost structure does not transfer. Modern equivalent operations under
contemporary labor law must price skilled weaver labor, child-labor-free sericulture
(requiring adult workers for all cocoon-handling stages), and draw-loom assistant
labor at full market rates. The falsifier constrains cost structure, not functional
achievability: draw loom operation is mechanically reproducible; its historical
economics are not.
