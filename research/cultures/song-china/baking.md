# Grain-Food Production in Song-Era China (ca. 960–1279)

## 1. Period and Geography

The Song dynasty (960–1279) governed one of the most densely urbanized civilizations of
the premodern world. At its demographic peak the Northern Song empire supported an
estimated 60–100 million people [CITATION-NEEDED: Song population estimates — Elvin 1973
provides an upper-end framework; cf. Bray 1994 on regional agrarian demography; the
variance in this range is large enough that per-capita grain-consumption calculations
must treat it as order-of-magnitude only], with cities of a scale unmatched anywhere
in the contemporary world. Kaifeng, the Northern Song capital, may have held over one
million inhabitants by the eleventh century [CITATION-NEEDED: Kaifeng population
estimate; McKnight 1992 on Song urban society and Kracke on Song urban structure are
likely sources; confirm before citing in downstream synthesis]. Hangzhou, capital of the
Southern Song after 1127, was described by Marco Polo (writing retrospectively) as the
greatest city in the world [CITATION-NEEDED: Marco Polo on Hangzhou; *The Travels of
Marco Polo*, Book II — confirm relevant passage and whether it refers to Hangzhou
specifically or Lin'an by alternate name].

The empire was divided by both political change and agro-ecological geography. The
Northern Song (960–1127) controlled the Yellow River basin and the North China Plain —
a dry-farming wheat and millet zone, where steamed and flatbreaded grain foods had been
the primary carbohydrate for millennia. The Southern Song (1127–1279) retreated to the
Yangtze valley and south China following the Jurchen Jin conquest of the north — a
paddy-rice zone where rice (eaten as boiled grain or porridge) was the primary staple and
wheat-based preparations were a supplementary or elite food [Bray 1994, chs. 1–2].

This north-south divide is not incidental to the study of grain-food production. "Baking"
in the European oven-bread sense was concentrated in the north; it was a wheat-based,
heat-intensive practice that had no equivalent in rice-dominated southern China. Most
wheat-based preparations across both regions were steamed, not oven-baked — a distinction
that defines the physical equipment, the product range, and the commercial structure.
The chapter addresses both traditions while maintaining this distinction throughout.
Where this chapter uses "baking" it refers specifically to the direct-heat grain-food
trades; steaming is documented as the dominant preparation form but not conflated with
oven-baking.

## 2. The Vendor's Place

The grain-food trade in Song China was not organized around a household baker or a
guild-regulated craft workshop. It was organized around commercial street vendors and
urban food markets. This is a structural difference from the medieval European pattern
and must be stated at the outset, because projecting the European baker's trade onto
the Song context produces systematic misreadings.

Song cities supported extremely dense commercial food economies. Meng Yuanlao's
*Dongjing Menghua Lu* ("Dreams of Splendor of the Eastern Capital," ca. 1147), a memoir
of Kaifeng's commercial life before the 1127 fall, describes food vendors operating
through the night, open markets for steamed breads, flatbreads, noodles, and pastries
available at all hours [Meng Yuanlao, *Dongjing Menghua Lu* — CITATION-NEEDED: verify
English-language translation; West and Idema provide partial translations in
*The Story of the Western Wing*; a dedicated translation of the *Dongjing Menghua Lu*
is needed — document the existence and page range of the grain-food vendor passages
specifically]. Similar accounts appear in *Wulin Jiushi* (Records of Wulin, describing
Hangzhou) [CITATION-NEEDED: *Wulin Jiushi* — verify authorship (Zhou Mi, 1232–1298),
date of composition, and availability of partial English translation covering food-market
passages; E.N. Anderson 1988 may summarize this source].

This commercial density was driven by the urban population structure. Song city-dwellers
were not subsistence cultivators; they were artisans, merchants, soldiers, officials, and
service workers who purchased their food rather than growing it. The grain-food vendor
was not a neighborhood amenity; the vendor was a node in a dense supply network feeding
a non-self-provisioning urban population [Anderson 1988, ch. 3; CITATION-NEEDED:
Anderson's specific chapter covering Song urban food markets — confirm chapters and
pages in the 1988 edition of *The Food of China*].

The social position of grain-food vendors differed fundamentally from European
baker-guild members. Song commercial food preparation was conducted by licensed
merchants (*shangren*) or by informal street vendors operating at the margin of
licensing systems. There was no equivalent to the bakers' guilds of London or Paris —
no corporate body with formal apprenticeship stages, exclusive market rights enforced
by charter, or quality tribunals. The *jianghu* artisan-registration system applied
to a range of craft trades, but commercial food vending operated under merchant
licensing — essentially a tax and regulatory framework rather than an occupational
corporation [CITATION-NEEDED: Song merchant licensing and food-vendor regulation;
McKnight 1992 or von Glahn 2016 on Song commercial law and licensed merchant categories;
specific treatment of food-vendor licensing needed]. The economic distance between
guild-controlled craft production and Song licensed food vending is large; eliding
it distorts both.

Wealthy households maintained their own cooks — staff who prepared grain foods and
dishes as part of household service rather than as commercial vendors. Women of working
households prepared grain foods at home using the same steaming and griddle equipment
available to commercial vendors. These two non-commercial forms coexisted with the
market-vendor economy but served different population segments and operated under
different economics.

## 3. Equipment

### The Dominance of Steam

The single most important material fact about Song grain-food production is that steaming,
not oven-baking, was the dominant cooking method for wheat-based foods. The primary
equipment was the *zheng* — a tiered bamboo or clay steaming basket set over a wok
or pot of boiling water [CITATION-NEEDED: physical description of Song-era steaming
equipment — Huang 2000, *Science and Civilisation in China*, vol. VI, pt. V,
Fermentations and Food Science, on steaming equipment for grain foods; confirm page
range]. Multiple tiers stacked vertically allowed simultaneous cooking of large batches.
The *zheng* was modular, low-cost, and suited to high-throughput production without
requiring substantial fuel or capital investment beyond a water-heating vessel and an
open fire.

This equipment profile is radically simpler and cheaper than a masonry oven. It required
no substantial construction, no extended preheating cycle, and no proprietary fuel
management skill. The implication for CERES is direct: the capital barrier to entry for
steamed grain-food production was low, which contributed to the dense competitive market
structure rather than suppressing it.

The fuel was wood or charcoal, managed in a clay stove or open hearth beneath the
steaming vessel. Coal was available in northern cities, particularly in Kaifeng and other
Yellow River basin towns, where coal from Shanxi had been entering commercial fuel
markets since at least the eleventh century [CITATION-NEEDED: coal as urban fuel in
Northern Song Kaifeng — Hartwell 1962 documents coal in ironworks but urban domestic
fuel use requires a separate source; Elvin 1973 may address this; specific evidence of
coal in Song grain-food preparation needed]. The southern commercial food trade would
have relied on wood and charcoal.

### Clay Ovens and Shaobing

True oven-baking did exist, concentrated in the north and in the shaobing tradition.
Shaobing (烧饼) — a sesame-coated wheat flatbread — was baked in a barrel-shaped clay
oven (a *tian lu* or similar form; CITATION-NEEDED: specific term and physical description
of the clay barrel oven used for shaobing in Song period — Huang 2000 is likely the
most direct source; Anderson 1988 may provide context; the specific oven morphology
must be documented from a primary-sourced secondary account, not assumed) in which the
dough was slapped directly onto the hot interior walls and baked by radiant heat, in a
manner structurally analogous to the tandoor of Central and South Asia [CITATION-NEEDED:
relationship between Chinese shaobing oven and Central Asian tandoor traditions —
food history or culinary archaeology source; the Silk Road transmission pathway is
plausible but must be sourced, not assumed]. The fuel was charcoal or coal; the oven
required preheating; the operation was physically demanding in a confined hot space.

Shaobing vendors were documented in both the Kaifeng and Hangzhou market accounts.
They were commercial street operations, not household production. A single vendor or
small team managed an oven, produced batches of flatbreads, and sold them to passing
trade — not producing on the order of the bulk-grain throughput of a European town
mill-and-bakery system, but matching demand to a mobile urban customer base
[CITATION-NEEDED: shaobing vendor operations in Song urban context — *Dongjing
Menghua Lu* and *Wulin Jiushi* are likely primary-evidence vehicles; Anderson 1988
for secondary synthesis].

### Griddles and Open-Fire Cooking

Alongside steaming and clay ovens, griddle-based preparation — applying dough to a
heated flat iron surface — produced a range of pancake-type and flatbread products that
do not require classification as either steaming or oven-baking. The griddle (*bing
dang* or similar) was a further equipment form in the northern flatbread tradition
[CITATION-NEEDED: griddle types in Song northern flatbread production; Huang 2000 is
the preferred source; specific equipment descriptions needed rather than product-category
inference].

### Rice Processing in the South

In the southern Yangtze-valley economy, the grain-food production system centered on
paddy rice. The equipment base was dominated by mills for husking and polishing rice,
and by steaming and boiling equipment for rice-porridge and rice-based preparations.
Rice-based cakes and glutinous preparations (nian gao, yuan xiao) were made by
grinding soaked rice to a paste and then steaming or frying — a process requiring
stone mills, straining equipment, and heat application, but not an oven in any sense
[CITATION-NEEDED: rice-food preparation equipment in Song southern China — Bray 1994
on rice economy and rice-food forms; Huang 2000 on fermentation and grain-food
processing in the southern tradition]. These preparations were seasonal and commercial;
they appeared in market contexts as well as household production.

## 4. Products

The product range of Song grain-food vendors was wide, regionally differentiated, and
commercially evolved. The following categories are documented in contemporary sources and
standard secondary accounts:

**Northern wheat-based steamed foods:**
- *Mantou* (馒头): Leavened steamed wheat buns, made with fermented dough. The
  leavening agent was a wild-yeast starter culture (*jiaomu* or similar), not the
  brewer's yeast used in European bread baking; the fermentation process was managed
  empirically [Huang 2000, pt. V — CITATION-NEEDED: Huang's specific treatment of
  mantou fermentation and starter cultures; confirm volume, part, and page range].
  Mantou could be plain or filled with vegetables, meat, or sweet pastes (*baozi*
  when filled). They were produced in large batches in tiered steamers and sold by
  weight or count.
- *Bing* (饼, general flatbread category): A broad category of wheat preparations
  cooked by a variety of methods — griddle, oven, and sometimes pan-frying. The
  northern bing tradition predated the Song by centuries [CITATION-NEEDED: bing
  as pre-Song northern Chinese grain-food tradition — Huang 2000 or Anderson 1988
  on historical depth of the flatbread tradition; Bray 1994 on northern vs. southern
  wheat use].

**Oven-baked northern flatbreads:**
- *Shaobing* (烧饼): Sesame wheat flatbreads baked in clay barrel oven, as described
  in §3. Leavened and unleavened variants existed; some carried sesame paste fillings.
  This was the closest Chinese analog to the European bread trade in terms of
  equipment and process, but it was a street-vended product, not a household staple
  produced at domestic scale [CITATION-NEEDED: shaobing product variants in Song
  period; Huang 2000 on shaobing fermentation and product range].

**Noodles:**
- *Mian* (面): Wheat noodles, boiled or occasionally fried. The Song urban food markets
  sold noodles extensively [CITATION-NEEDED: noodle vendors in Song markets —
  *Dongjing Menghua Lu* passages; Anderson 1988]. Noodle production was a
  grain-processing and cooking operation distinct from either steaming or baking;
  it required kneading, rolling or drawing, and water-cooking infrastructure. Noodle
  shops were a distinct category of food vendor from steamed-bun vendors or flatbread
  vendors.

**Southern rice-based preparations:**
- *Nian gao* (年糕, glutinous rice cake), *yuan xiao* (元宵, glutinous rice balls),
  and various rice-paste preparations baked or fried for seasonal festivals and
  daily market sale. These were produced from ground glutinous rice paste by steaming
  and, in some variants, pan-frying in oil [CITATION-NEEDED: southern rice-cake
  production in Song period — Huang 2000; Bray 1994 on rice variety differentiation
  between standard and glutinous rice].
- Rice congee (*zhou*) and various porridge preparations were produced commercially
  and domestically. These are boiling preparations and not discussed in the equipment
  section but are a documented part of the commercial food economy.

**Pastries and sweetened products:**
- Song urban markets sold a documented range of sweetened pastry products —
  preparations using wheat flour, oil, and sugar or honey, baked or fried, often
  seasonal [CITATION-NEEDED: sweet pastry products in Song markets — *Dongjing
  Menghua Lu* or *Wulin Jiushi* passages; Anderson 1988 on Chinese pastry traditions].
  These were higher-value products than basic mantou or shaobing and were sold by
  specialist vendors.

The product range implies a highly differentiated commercial food economy with specialists
in distinct product lines. Mantou vendors, shaobing vendors, noodle shops, and pastry
vendors were distinct operations with distinct equipment and supply-chain requirements.
No single "baker" produced all of these, and the concept of a generalist grain-food
craftsman on the European village-baker model does not map onto the Song urban context.

## 5. Labor Regime

### Commercial Vendors

The typical grain-food vendor in a Song city was either a small proprietor running a
family operation with household labor, or a larger commercial operation employing hired
workers. The family operation relied on husband, wife, and older children performing
differentiated but interchangeable tasks: fuel management, dough preparation, steaming
or baking, and customer-facing sale. None of this labor was separately costed or
contracted; per-unit economics that exclude unpaid household labor systematically
understate the production cost.

Larger operations — vendors with multiple stalls or wholesale supply to other sellers —
employed hired workers paid a daily or seasonal wage. Song labor markets in the
major cities were active; landless migrants from rural areas and lower-ranked household
workers competed for urban wage labor including food-trade work [CITATION-NEEDED:
Song urban wage labor in food trades — von Glahn 2016 on Song labor markets; specific
evidence of food-vendor hired labor]. Labor was not formally stratified by a guild
apprenticeship hierarchy; a worker with basic dough-handling skills could be employed
without a formal indenture. The absence of guild entry barriers meant lower earnings
protection for workers: there was no institution preventing a new vendor from undercutting
established sellers on price.

### Household Women

In non-commercial contexts, grain-food preparation was primarily the work of women
in the household. The production of mantou, congee, and rice preparations for household
consumption was domestic female labor, not traded work. This household-production tier
was invisible to market records and to contemporary food-market memoirs. Its existence
matters for CERES's scale analysis: the commercial vendor trade served the urban
non-household population (sojourners, officials, soldiers, merchants away from their
own households), while households with women present produced their own grain foods
[CITATION-NEEDED: household vs. commercial grain-food production division in Song China —
Bray 1994 on women's roles in grain processing; Anderson 1988 on household vs. market
food economy].

### Wealthy Household Cooks

Elite households — official, merchant, and gentry families — employed specialized cooks
(*chu*) who prepared grain foods and dishes for the household. These cooks were
domestic servants, not commercial vendors. Their labor was a consumption expenditure
for the household, not a productive trade activity in the commercial economy. The
literary record of elite household food is more extensive than the record of street-vendor
food, which creates a selection bias in the surviving texts; the food preparation practices
of commercial vendors must be inferred from market memoirs and administrative records
rather than from the culinary literature that primarily documents elite cuisine
[CITATION-NEEDED: Song culinary literature and its elite bias — Anderson 1988 addresses
this methodological issue for Chinese food history generally; Huang 2000 is more
technically focused].

### Absence of Guild Structure

The *jianghu* artisan-household registration system — which applied to craft trades
including silk weaving, metalwork, and construction — imposed hereditary occupational
status on registered artisan households and made them available for state labor demands
[see song-china/smithing.md §Labor Regime for the *jianghu* framework]. It is not
established in the secondary literature consulted here that food vendors were brought
under *jianghu* registration in the same way as craft artisans [CITATION-NEEDED:
*jianghu* registration and commercial food vendors — whether food vending was a
*jianghu*-registered craft or operated under purely commercial merchant licensing;
von Glahn 2016 or a Song administrative history specialist needed to resolve this].
The practical difference matters: *jianghu* registration imposed hereditary labor
obligations and restricted occupational mobility; commercial merchant licensing imposed
tax and compliance obligations but did not typically constrain exit. The food vendor
was most likely operating under the latter framework.

## 6. Supply Chain

### Grain Supply and State Granaries

The grain supply for Song commercial food production was mediated by a combination of
market exchange and state institutional management. The Song state maintained an
extensive granary system — *changpingcang* (ever-normal granaries) — that bought grain
when prices were low and released stocks when prices rose, functioning as a price
stabilization mechanism [CITATION-NEEDED: Song ever-normal granary system —
Elvin 1973 ch. 5; von Glahn 2016 on Song fiscal and granary policy; specific treatment
of *changpingcang* operations and their market effects needed]. For commercial food
vendors, this meant that the grain input price was partly buffered by state intervention —
a different market structure from the European grain market, where famines could push
flour prices beyond the ability of town bakers to maintain output.

State grain also moved through the tax system: the fundamental Song land tax was paid
in grain, which flowed into state storehouses and then into state consumption (army,
officials, capital provisioning) and market release. This meant the state was a major
actor in the grain supply chain even in periods of commercial market expansion
[CITATION-NEEDED: Song grain tax system and state grain in commercial supply chain —
von Glahn 2016 is the comprehensive reference; Elvin 1973 provides the historical
framework].

### Commercial Milling

Between the grain market and the grain-food vendor sat the commercial miller. Wheat
had to be milled to flour before it could be made into mantou, shaobing, or noodles.
Song cities had commercial mills — water-powered mills along streams near cities, and
animal-powered mills within urban areas — run by specialist millers who charged a
milling fee or kept a portion of the flour [CITATION-NEEDED: commercial milling in
Song China — Bray 1994 on grain-processing technology; Needham on water mills in
China; specific evidence of urban milling operations and fee structure needed].
Rice milling similarly required commercial hulling and polishing equipment; rice
vendors did not process paddy into edible rice in the same facility. The commercial
food vendor was not a vertically integrated producer: grain purchase, milling, and
food preparation were separate economic activities.

This supply-chain structure has a direct falsifier implication for CERES: the Song
commercial food vendor was not self-sufficient from grain to finished product. The
minimum viable commercial grain-food operation required access to milled flour (or
rice) — a commercially produced input — and could not economically integrate milling
with preparation at small scale. The implication for modern catalog design is that
a grain-food production operation at commercial scale requires a parallel milling
supply chain or commercially available flour supply; assuming vertical integration
from grain is historically unsupported.

### Sesame, Oil, and Other Inputs

Shaobing required sesame — a seed oil crop grown primarily in northern China and the
Yellow River basin [CITATION-NEEDED: sesame cultivation geography in Song China —
Bray 1994 on oil crops and their production geography]. Sesame supply to northern
cities ran through merchant networks. Oil — for frying and pastry-making — was
similarly a traded commodity input, not locally produced by food vendors. The
commercial food vendor's supply chain therefore included grain, milling, sesame, and
oil as distinct upstream dependencies, each subject to seasonal price variation
and supply disruption.

Fermentation starters for leavened steamed breads and some baked products were
maintained by individual vendors as a managed microbial culture — a low-cost but
technically demanding input that vendors controlled directly. The failure of a starter
culture was an operational disruption; maintaining starter quality was part of the
technical knowledge specific to leavened grain-food production [Huang 2000 —
CITATION-NEEDED: Huang's treatment of Chinese grain fermentation starters (*jiaomu*
or equivalent) — confirm volume, part, and specific section].

## 7. Continuity and Non-Decline

### The Wrong Question

The section heading "Decline or Restructuring" that structures the smithing chapters
applies poorly to Song Chinese grain-food production, because the grain-food vendor
trade has not declined, restructured in response to industrialization, or undergone
an identifiable discontinuity in the Song-to-present trajectory comparable to what
European bakers' guilds experienced after the introduction of industrial roller-milling
and factory bread. The commercial vendor economy for steamed and flatbread grain
foods in Chinese cities has been continuous for over a millennium. This continuity
is itself a finding worth stating explicitly.

### Yuan and Ming Continuity

The Mongol conquest (1234 northern China; 1279 southern China) disrupted the Song
political economy but did not destroy the commercial food trade. Yuan dynasty Dadu
(Beijing) and the major commercial cities maintained dense food markets substantially
continuous with Song urban commercial culture [CITATION-NEEDED: Yuan dynasty food
market continuity with Song — Anderson 1988 on Yuan urban food; von Glahn 2016 on
Yuan commercial economy]. The Ming dynasty (1368–1644) inherited and continued
the commercial food-vendor economy in its cities. The *Bencao Gangmu* (Compendium of
Materia Medica, 1596) by Li Shizhen documents grain-food preparations in ways
consistent with the Song descriptions, confirming the long continuity of the product
forms [CITATION-NEEDED: Li Shizhen, *Bencao Gangmu* (1596) — grain-food entries;
confirm English edition or partial translation; Huang 2000 likely cites this source].

### Industrial Milling as the Structural Break

The structural break in Chinese grain-food production comparable to European industrial
baking came not from within the traditional vendor trade but from the introduction of
industrial roller-milling and factory-produced flour in the late nineteenth and early
twentieth centuries — principally through Shanghai and the treaty ports, where foreign
flour mills undercut traditional stone-milled flour on price. This price reduction did
not destroy the steamed-bread and flatbread vendor trade; it lowered their input cost
and potentially expanded the market [CITATION-NEEDED: industrial flour milling in late
Qing/Republican China — specific economic history source; the flour-milling
industrialization of Shanghai is documented but needs a precise citation, e.g., Rawski
on modern Chinese economic history or Bell on Republican-era Shanghai industry].

The commercial vendor trade for mantou, shaobing, noodles, and related preparations
survived this transition because the labor-and-skill content of the preparation process
was not displaced by industrial input supply — unlike the European case where factory
bread displaced the baker's entire operation. The product required hand-preparation and
rapid hot consumption regardless of flour source.

### Verdict

`continuous` — the commercial grain-food vendor trade operating in the Song period has
not undergone a structural collapse or guild-dissolution event comparable to the
European case. The applicable finding for CERES is that market continuity across
more than a millennium confirms the baseline commercial viability of the trade form.
The structural break (industrial flour milling) occurred much later and affected
upstream supply rather than the vendor operation itself. The CERES catalog should
not apply a "decline" or "guild dissolution" framing to this form; if the DECLINE-VERDICT.md
synthesis requires a canonical term, `continuous` with `upstream-supply-restructuring`
in the industrial period best represents the historical record.

## 8. Functional Requirements Learned

### Heat and Equipment

The primary heat requirement for Song grain-food production was water-boiling temperature
for steaming — sustained at 100°C through fuel management beneath a steaming vessel.
This is a substantially lower heat requirement than European bread baking (oven
temperatures of 200–250°C) and is achievable with any combustible fuel over any
open-fire or enclosed-firebox configuration. The capital and fuel requirements for
steaming are an order of magnitude below those for masonry oven baking; any catalog
entry that models grain-food production on the oven-baking form imposes a cost and
capital barrier that has no historical precedent in the dominant East Asian grain-food
tradition.

The shaobing clay barrel oven is a distinct equipment form requiring higher heat and
preheating management. Temperatures at the oven wall surface during baking likely
reached 200–280°C [CITATION-NEEDED: clay barrel oven interior temperatures for
shaobing baking — experimental-food-history or clay-oven archaeology source; this
figure is inferred from baking requirements and clay thermal properties and must be
sourced or flagged as an author estimate for Plan C]. This is comparable to European
flatbread baking temperatures but on a smaller thermal mass than a masonry batch oven.
The clay barrel oven preheats in approximately 30–45 minutes with appropriate fuel
loading [CITATION-NEEDED: preheating time for shaobing-type clay barrel oven —
no direct historical source identified; this figure must be sourced from an
experimental or contemporary-practice account before Plan C references it; blocking
citation required].

### Fermentation

Leavened grain-food production required management of a live starter culture. The
functional requirement is empirical monitoring of starter activity — recognizing
adequate fermentation, managing temperature to control rise rate, and refreshing
the culture regularly. This knowledge was held by individual vendors and households
as craft practice; no formal institution preserved or transmitted it [Huang 2000 —
CITATION-NEEDED: Huang on fermentation knowledge transmission in Chinese grain-food
tradition; confirm specific section]. The CERES catalog entry for leavened steamed
or baked grain foods must include fermentation-management as a core skill requirement,
not a peripheral detail.

### Labor and Scale

The minimum viable commercial operation for steamed grain foods (mantou, baozi) was
small: a household unit with one or two adults, one large wok-and-steamer setup, and
access to commercial flour. This is the historically documented scale for Song street
vendors. Scale-up was additive: more steaming tiers, more workers, more fuel. There
was no minimum-scale threshold enforced by capital equipment cost comparable to the
masonry oven.

For shaobing production, the minimum viable unit was one clay barrel oven, one or
two operators to manage the oven and form the dough, and access to commercial flour
and sesame. The oven's thermal mass required continuous operation to be economical
— an intermittent-use shaobing oven lost heat during downtime and required full
preheating at restart, creating a labor-to-output ratio disadvantageous for small
batches. This is a throughput-floor constraint, not a capital-cost barrier.

### Supply-Chain Dependency

Neither the steamed-food vendor nor the flatbread vendor was grain-supply autonomous.
Both depended on commercial milled flour as the primary input. The minimum viable
supply-chain for a Song-style grain-food operation therefore includes:

1. Grain market access (or, equivalently, commercial flour supply)
2. Milling (commercial miller or purchased flour)
3. Fuel supply (wood, charcoal, or coal for sustained combustion)
4. Fermentation starter (managed internally, low-cost but critical)
5. For shaobing: sesame supply and oil supply

The absence of any one of these does not restructure the operation — it stops it.
A modern catalog entry for steamed grain-food production must model these dependencies
explicitly rather than assuming local grain-to-product vertical integration.

### What This Form Cannot Tell Us

The Song commercial grain-food vendor operated under a market structure with no price
floor, no guild-enforced territorial monopoly, and no institutional barrier to entry.
The result was a dense, competitive, commercially-evolved supply of grain foods at
prices accessible to non-elite urban consumers. This market structure was made possible
by a supply-side density (many vendors, low capital barriers) and a demand-side density
(millions of urban consumers without household food production) that together created
the conditions. Neither condition transfers automatically to a CERES-context settlement
of 500–15,000 people. At small settlement scale, the Song density-dependent model
is not a direct precedent; the relevant precedent for small-settlement grain-food
production is probably the household or semi-commercial form, not the urban-vendor form.
