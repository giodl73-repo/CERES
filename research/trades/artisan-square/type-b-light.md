# Artisan Square — Type B Light-Trades Facility

## 1. Facility Context

Type B is the light-trades wing of the Artisan Square two-wing design. Where the
Type A wing houses combustion-intensive and heavy-load trades (forge, kiln, welding),
Type B serves trades whose infrastructure requirements stop well short of 3-phase
power, industrial exhaust risers, and 150 lb/sq ft floor ratings. The defining
difference is occupancy compatibility: Type B studios can often be permitted under
Group B (business) or F-2 (light manufacturing) in a mixed-use or retail-commercial
zone, whereas Type A requires dedicated industrial zoning and fire-separation
treatment. This regulatory asymmetry is strategically important — mixed-use and
retail-commercial zones are precisely where the artisan's customer base shops and
lives. A Type B facility positioned on a main-street commercial corridor has built-in
foot traffic advantages that a pure industrial-zone building cannot replicate.

The second defining difference is the commissary umbrella: Type B hosts food-producing
studios under a single operator-held commercial kitchen license, structuring tenant
food operations as individual registrants under a multi-operator commissary. This
is a regulatory unlock that Type A cannot provide. The following tenant types occupy
Type B studios in the Artisan Square design:

| Tenant type | Studio size | Primary infrastructure need |
|---|---|---|
| Cottage baker / artisan bread startup | 150–250 sq ft | 240V sub-panel, floor drain, NSF hot water, food-grade exhaust |
| Specialty food / confection | 150–250 sq ft | Same as baking; may require refrigeration circuit |
| Handweaver / fiber arts | 150–250 sq ft | Humidity-controlled HVAC; 240V lighting; optional dye drain |
| Leather / bookbinding | 100–200 sq ft | Standard 120V; optional ventilation for adhesives/dyes |
| Ceramics (light — no kiln) | 150–200 sq ft | Floor drain; dust management; no combustion exhaust |
| Soap / candle making | 100–200 sq ft | Standard 120V; ventilation for fragrance and hot wax |

The 2-hour fire-rated wall separating Type A and Type B is mandatory under IBC Section
508 (Mixed Use Protected) and is the physical mechanism that allows the two wings to
hold separate occupancy classifications on a single parcel.

## 2. The Baking Studio

### Physical Requirements

A Type B baking studio is a dedicated production space sized for a sole-proprietor
or small-team artisan baker. The spec-standard unit is 150–250 sq ft, but the
infrastructure density is higher than the square footage suggests:

- **Electrical:** 240V 60A single-phase sub-panel with individual metering. The 60A
  rating covers one commercial convection oven (4.5–7.5 kW draw), a stand mixer
  (2–3 kW), refrigeration, and general lighting simultaneously. This is not a
  shared panel — each baking studio receives its own metered sub-panel so tenants
  pay only their own power consumption.

- **Floor drain:** Required by food safety code in any commercial food-production
  space where water is used for cleaning. NSF International standards (NSF/ANSI 61
  for water system components; NSF/ANSI 2 for food equipment surfaces) govern the
  drain, floor finish, and hot-water line. The floor drain connects to the building's
  grease interceptor system — a dedicated interceptor at the base of the food-grade
  exhaust riser handles fat, oil, and grease (FOG) from the studio's cooking and
  cleaning operations.

- **Hot-water line:** NSF-rated hot water at minimum 120°F at the tap for equipment
  and surface sanitation per state health code. This is a dedicated line from the
  building's hot-water system, not shared with the restroom circuit.

- **Food-grade exhaust riser:** Each baking studio connects to a dedicated food-grade
  exhaust stack — entirely separate from the Type A industrial combustion exhaust.
  The food-grade stack handles grease-laden vapor from ovens and cooking surfaces.
  A grease interceptor (mechanical grease trap) is required at the base of this
  riser before connection to the municipal drain. The Type B exhaust stack operates
  at lower CFM than the Type A industrial riser (typically 300–600 CFM per food
  studio vs. 2,000+ CFM per forge station) but must be dedicated to food-producing
  studios; cross-connection to the general HVAC exhaust is not permitted under
  commercial kitchen ventilation standards.

- **NSF-rated flooring:** Quarry tile, sealed concrete, or other NSF/ANSI-2-compliant
  flooring with a minimum 2% slope to the floor drain. No carpet or porous surface.

### Commissary Umbrella Licensing Model

The Artisan Square operator holds a single Type A commercial kitchen license (the
highest-grade food facility license) that covers the entire Type B food-producing
wing as a multi-operator commissary. This is the model pioneered and refined by
food business incubators such as La Cocina SF.

La Cocina (San Francisco) is the benchmark food incubator for commissary-umbrella
licensing structure. Founded in 2005, it operates a licensed commercial kitchen
shared by multiple food entrepreneurs, primarily immigrant women building food
businesses. Under La Cocina's model, the facility holds the primary health department
license; individual operators register with the health department under the
commissary umbrella rather than obtaining standalone commercial kitchen licenses.
[CITATION-NEEDED: La Cocina SF commissary licensing structure — specific health
department registration mechanism; La Cocina published program guides are the
expected primary source.] La Cocina reports shared kitchen access at approximately
$20–40/hour for shared-use sessions [CITATION-NEEDED: La Cocina SF current rate
schedule — public program documentation; rates cited are the commonly reported
2020s-era range and require confirmation against current published rates.] Dedicated
studio rental at comparable food incubator facilities runs $800–1,500/month for
a 150–250 sq ft dedicated production space. [CITATION-NEEDED: comparable food
incubator dedicated-studio rental rates — La Cocina SF or comparable programs
(Pilsen Community Kitchen Chicago; La Cocina de la Esperanza Sacramento;
CommonWealth Kitchen Boston) published rate schedules; cited range is aggregated
from public program documentation ca. 2022–2024.]

The Artisan Square implementation of the commissary umbrella differs from La Cocina
in one critical respect: studios are exclusively dedicated to individual tenants, not
shared on an hourly basis. The commissary license structure is the same; the access
model is dedicated-studio rather than shared-rotation. This gives the tenant the
privacy and operational continuity of their own production space while allowing the
operator to hold a single commissary license covering all food tenants.

### What the Commissary Umbrella Saves the Sole-Proprietor Baker

The commissary umbrella is a meaningful financial benefit for the individual tenant,
not merely an administrative convenience:

- **Individual commercial kitchen license:** In most U.S. states, a sole-proprietor
  baker operating from a commercially licensed kitchen must either (a) obtain their
  own commercial kitchen license, (b) rent from a licensed commissary, or (c) operate
  under a cottage food law (which typically prohibits direct-to-consumer sales of
  certain products and caps annual revenue). Obtaining a standalone commercial kitchen
  permit requires a full health department buildout inspection and an annual license
  fee; initial permitting costs range from $1,500–$6,000 in most jurisdictions, with
  annual renewal at $300–$1,500. [CITATION-NEEDED: commercial kitchen permit costs by
  jurisdiction — FDA food facility registration costs are fixed federal fees; state
  health department commercial kitchen license fees vary; cited ranges are aggregated
  from publicly available state health department fee schedules; a per-state table
  would be required for precision.]

- **Annual savings under commissary umbrella:** Operating under an Artisan Square
  commissary umbrella eliminates the individual commercial kitchen license and its
  associated annual fees. Over a 3-year startup period, this typically saves the
  baker $5,000–$10,000 in licensing, inspection, and permitting costs, plus the
  administrative burden of managing health department compliance independently. The
  operator absorbs this compliance cost as part of the all-inclusive rent structure;
  the tenant benefit is built into the studio rent rather than priced separately.

- **Cottage food law ceiling:** Many states cap cottage food operation revenue at
  $25,000–$75,000/year. A baker scaling past that ceiling must obtain commercial
  kitchen access in any case. The commissary umbrella removes that ceiling from day
  one, without forcing the baker to navigate individual licensing at the point when
  their business is most cash-constrained.

## 3. The Fiber Arts / Weaving Studio

### Physical Requirements

The fiber arts studio is the second major Type B studio category by tenant count.
Its infrastructure requirements differ substantially from the baking studio:

- **Electrical:** 240V lighting-only circuit is sufficient for hand looms (rigid
  heddle, backstrap, standard four-shaft floor loom). Electric floor looms with
  powered dobby or Jacquard mechanisms draw 30–40A and require a 240V sub-panel,
  but this is lower electrical density than a baking studio. A 30A 240V circuit
  is the spec standard for fiber arts studios to accommodate both hand-loom
  operation and potential powered-loom installation.

- **Humidity control:** This is the defining infrastructure requirement for wool
  and silk weaving and is the item most commonly omitted from generic light-
  industrial studio designs. Per the weaving REQUIREMENTS.md findings, wool
  requires 65–75% RH and silk requires 65–70% RH for thread workability; below
  approximately 45% RH, static charge and warp breakage rates increase materially.
  Standard commercial HVAC systems are designed to maintain 40–60% RH for human
  comfort and do not hold the 65–75% RH envelope required for wool weaving in dry
  climates or dry winters without supplemental humidification. The Artisan Square
  Type B spec includes a dedicated humidification system for fiber arts studios —
  either a building-level humidifier on the HVAC trunk serving those studios, or
  studio-level ultrasonic humidifiers with RH sensors and automatic control. [CITATION-
  NEEDED: RH requirements for wool and cotton weaving — textile engineering or
  conservation science source confirming the 65–75% range; weaving REQUIREMENTS.md
  §2 flags this as requiring a citable primary source.]

- **Cotton and linen studios:** Less humidity-sensitive (50–65% RH adequate) and
  can operate on standard HVAC in most climates.

### Dye Studio Variant

The dye studio is an optional but economically valuable variant within the fiber
arts Type B footprint. Natural and fiber-reactive dyeing requires:

- Floor drain (mandatory; dye effluent cannot enter general drainage without
  neutralization in most jurisdictions)
- Hot water (110–140°F for fiber-reactive and natural dye processes)
- Chemical storage cabinet (OSHA-compliant for mordants including alum, iron sulfate,
  and acid dyes where used)
- Ventilation: direct exhaust for any heated dye baths; acid dyes and fiber-reactive
  dyes produce vapor that should not accumulate in an enclosed studio

The critical constraint is spatial separation from food studios: dye chemicals,
mordants, and synthetic dye compounds are categorically incompatible with a food
production environment. Any dye studio must be physically separated from the
food-producing wing of Type B — not merely by a partition, but by a full demising
wall — and must not share HVAC exhaust with food studios. In practice, dye studios
should be positioned at the far end of the Type B corridor from baking studios, with
separate exhaust to a non-food-grade stack.

The fiber arts studio has no commissary involvement. No food is produced; no health
department registration is required of the tenant beyond standard business licensing.
This is an important simplicity advantage for the operator: fiber arts studio tenants
do not appear on the commissary compliance calendar.

## 4. Type B Shared Infrastructure

### The Commissary Umbrella: Operator Structure and Risk

The Artisan Square operator (the building entity) is the licensee on the commissary
commercial kitchen license. This is the fundamental legal and operational structure:
the operator holds the license; tenants are registrants under it.

**Health department inspection calendar for a multi-tenant commissary.** A multi-
tenant commissary is typically inspected on the same schedule as any Type A
commercial kitchen — annually in most jurisdictions, with follow-up inspections if
deficiencies are found. The inspection covers the entire commissary umbrella, not
individual tenants. A health department inspector will evaluate:
- The physical facility (drains, surfaces, hot-water supply, exhaust, pest exclusion)
- The operator's commissary agreement documentation (signed tenant commissary
  agreements are required in most jurisdictions)
- Evidence that tenants are individually registered with the health department as
  commissary users
- Sanitation log records

**The umbrella risk — one tenant's failure affects the whole.** This is the most
significant structural risk of the commissary umbrella model. If a single tenant
fails an inspection condition — for example, uses prohibited equipment (a domestic
microwave rather than a commercial unit), fails to maintain required sanitation
records, or stores non-food items in the food production area — the health department
can place conditions on the entire commissary license, not merely on the offending
tenant. In a severe case, the license can be suspended while deficiencies are
corrected, affecting every food tenant in the building. [CITATION-NEEDED: legal
treatment of multi-tenant commissary license suspension when individual tenant
violates conditions — state health department administrative law; varies by
jurisdiction; California, New York, and Texas provide the most documented case
patterns.]

This risk is the reason that commissary compliance clauses in tenant leases must
be written with specificity and teeth. The Artisan Square lease template addresses
this by:

1. Requiring each food tenant to sign a commissary user agreement as a condition
   of tenancy
2. Making violation of commissary health code conditions a material lease default
   curable within 72 hours
3. Requiring tenants to maintain their own sanitation logs and make them available
   to the operator on request
4. Giving the operator right of inspection of the tenant's studio at any time for
   commissary compliance purposes (with 24-hour notice except in health emergencies)
5. Holding the tenant liable for operator costs arising from health department
   actions attributable to that tenant's non-compliance

The goal is to transfer as much of the compliance risk as practicable to the tenant
who caused it, while recognizing that the operator cannot fully escape licensee
liability.

### Food-Grade Exhaust Stack

The Type B food-grade exhaust stack is a separate, dedicated ventilation system
from the Type A industrial exhaust. It is designed to:

- Handle grease-laden vapor from commercial ovens and cooking surfaces in food
  studios
- Connect to a grease interceptor (mechanical or hydromechanical grease trap)
  at the base of the riser before connection to the municipal drain
- Operate at lower CFM than the Type A stack — typically 300–600 CFM per food
  studio, aggregating to 2,000–4,000 CFM for a wing of 6–8 food studios
- Maintain UL 710 (commercial kitchen ventilation) compliance for all food studio
  connection points

The grease interceptor requires periodic pumping — typically every 30–90 days
depending on tenant cooking activity. This is an operator maintenance obligation;
it is not tenant-managed. Failure to maintain the interceptor is a commissary
compliance deficiency.

### IBC Occupancy for Type B

The IBC occupancy question for a Type B facility is whether baking studios qualify
as Group F-2 (light manufacturing, specifically food processing) or Group M
(mercantile, or in some interpretations, retail food operations). The practical
answer varies by jurisdiction and by how the local building official reads the
use description. The distinction matters because:

- **F-2 (light manufacturing)** is broadly compatible with light-industrial and
  some mixed-use zones; it covers "Food and beverage products manufacturing" in
  IBC Table 307.1(2) and is the straightforward reading for a studio where
  production, not retail, is the primary use
- **Group B (business)** applies to the weaving, leather, and non-food studios and
  to the management office; it is the most permissive of the non-retail classifications
  for mixed-use zoning compatibility
- **Group M (mercantile)** may apply if street-facing studios conduct over-the-counter
  retail sales as their primary activity; this creates a higher parking and egress
  requirement in some jurisdictions

The CERES spec treats Type B as F-2 for all food studios as the conservative
assumption. This is intentionally conservative: if the local building official
reads the baking studios as F-2, the Artisan Square design already meets that
standard. If the official reads them as Group B, the design is over-specified,
which is the preferred direction.

### Mixed-Use and Retail-Commercial Zoning Compatibility

The Type B wing's street-facing studios are designed with retail windows and direct
street access. This enables tenants to sell directly to walk-in customers — a
significant revenue supplement for bakers (direct bread sales) and fiber arts
tenants (yarn, finished goods). To structure this correctly under mixed-use zoning:

- The street-facing studios must be on the building's ground floor with direct
  exterior access
- Signage rights must be included in the tenant lease or secured separately from
  the landlord
- The studio's use classification must permit both production and incidental retail
  sales (F-2 with accessory retail is typically permissible; the retail fraction
  must remain secondary to production by floor area)
- The building's certificate of occupancy must cover both the production use and
  the accessory retail use for the affected studios

The retail corridor is a Type B feature only; Type A studios have no retail window
component (the fire-separation wall and loading-dock orientation make this
impractical).

## 5. Tenant Economics from the Operator's Perspective

Type B rents are lower than Type A but the addressable tenant pool is substantially
larger. The economics work because Type B infrastructure cost per studio is lower
(no 3-phase sub-panel, no forge-grade exhaust, no 150 lb/sq ft floor rating) while
the target tenant population — startup food businesses, home-based fiber artists
scaling up, small-batch personal care product makers — is larger in most markets
than the journeyman smith or production ceramicist pool.

**Comparable market data:**

- Shared commissary kitchen access (hourly, shared-rotation model): $20–40/hour.
  At 20 hours/week of baking production, a tenant would pay $1,600–3,200/month
  for shared kitchen time alone — more than an Artisan Square dedicated studio.
  The dedicated studio at $1,100–1,750/month is therefore price-competitive with
  shared kitchen access once a baker exceeds approximately 10–15 hours/week of
  production. [CITATION-NEEDED: current commissary kitchen hourly rates — La Cocina
  SF, CommonWealth Kitchen Boston, or equivalent; cited range is aggregated from
  public program documentation ca. 2022–2024.]

- WeWork and creative coworking space (comparable square footage, no food
  infrastructure): $500–1,200/month for 150–200 sq ft in suburban/urban markets.
  [CITATION-NEEDED: WeWork or comparable shared office/creative space monthly rates
  for 150–200 sq ft private studios — WeWork published rates or commercial real
  estate broker survey data; cited range is broadly representative of 2022–2024
  U.S. suburban markets but requires current verification.]

- The Artisan Square Type B baking studio at $1,100–1,750/month (per the design spec
  §5.3) commands a premium over generic creative space because it includes the
  commissary umbrella, food-grade exhaust, NSF plumbing, and metered electrical.
  The tenant is paying for the regulatory access, not just the square footage.

**The commissary umbrella as a marketing asset.** A facility with a commissary license
actively listed on the health department's commissary registry generates inbound
tenant inquiries from bakers who have already identified the need for commissary
access. The operator does not need to explain what a commissary is; the tenant pool
is self-selecting. This reduces the operator's tenant acquisition cost substantially
compared to marketing general studio space to general small businesses.

**The fiber arts tenant pool.** Fiber arts studio tenants are a smaller pool than
food tenants but meaningfully larger than heavy metalwork tenants. The national
handweaving and fiber arts community is [CITATION-NEEDED: national count of active
handweavers or fiber arts practitioners in the U.S. — Handweavers Guild of America
membership or survey data; National Needlearts Association; relevant market-size
source] a measurable and organized community with active guild networks (Handweavers
Guild of America chapters, regional spinning and weaving guilds), which provides
a marketing channel the operator can address directly without cold outreach.

## 6. Operator Risks Specific to Type B

### Health Department Commissary Compliance Risk

The operator's most concentrated Type B risk is the commissary license. Unlike
building code compliance — which is a one-time permitting event with periodic
inspections — health department commissary compliance is a continuous operational
obligation. The operator cannot delegate it to tenants and then step back; the
licensee is responsible.

**Concrete exposure scenarios:**

- A tenant begins producing a product category not covered by their registered
  commissary use (e.g., a baker registered as a bread producer begins making pickled
  vegetables, which may require a separate acidified food permit)
- A tenant fails to maintain required temperature logs for refrigeration equipment
- A tenant introduces a consumer-grade (non-commercial) piece of equipment that the
  health department considers non-compliant with commercial kitchen standards

Any of these can trigger a conditions notice against the commissary license. The
operator's mitigation is the lease compliance clause (see Section 4) plus active
monitoring — quarterly walk-throughs of all food studios by the location manager,
with a checklist that mirrors the health department's inspection criteria.

**Structuring the compliance risk transfer.** The tenant compliance obligations in
the lease should be drafted to: (a) incorporate the health department's commissary
user requirements by reference, so that they update automatically as regulations
change; (b) make the tenant financially responsible for any re-inspection fees,
corrective action costs, and lost-business costs attributable to their non-compliance;
and (c) provide for accelerated cure periods (72 hours, not 30 days) for health code
violations given the license-level exposure.

### Humidity System Maintenance Risk

HVAC systems with supplemental humidification require more maintenance attention
than standard climate systems. The failure modes specific to the fiber arts studio
humidity envelope are:

- **Under-humidification:** Relative humidity drops below 45% RH in dry-winter
  conditions; wool and silk warp breakage rates increase; tenant production is
  disrupted; tenant satisfaction suffers
- **Over-humidification:** Relative humidity exceeds 80%; mold risk on fabric,
  fiber, and stored materials; potential building envelope damage; worst-case is
  a health code issue in studios where fiber storage is dense
- **HVAC failure in humid conditions:** A failed humidification system in summer
  can allow RH to spike above 80% in studios with stored fiber; mold can develop
  within 48–72 hours on natural fiber material at elevated temperature and humidity

The mitigation is a monitoring and rapid-response protocol: RH sensors in all
fiber arts studios reporting to a building management system; automated alerts to
the location manager when RH exceeds 80% or falls below 45%; a 4-hour response
SLA for HVAC service calls affecting fiber arts studios. Tenant leases for fiber
arts studios should include a clause requiring the tenant to report HVAC anomalies
within 24 hours.
