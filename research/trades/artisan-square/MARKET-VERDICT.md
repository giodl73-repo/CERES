# Artisan Square — Market Viability Verdict

## 1. The Question

Does operating an Artisan Square facility generate a viable private return? And if the
market path alone is marginal, can cooperative ownership or civic co-investment convert
marginal into viable?

The question is structured this way deliberately. CERES does not ask "is Artisan Square
good?" — it asks whether a private operator, a tenant cooperative, or a town's economic
development office can independently justify the investment. The answers differ by lens
and by location sequence. What fails at Location 1 may succeed at Location 5. What
fails on a private pro forma may succeed when a town subsidizes below-market land in
exchange for workforce development outputs.

This document produces a verdict per lens — market, cooperative, civic — and then a
combined verdict that guides Plan K catalog authoring. The verdict is not an investment
recommendation; it is an honest assessment of which configurations pencil out under
which conditions, using the design spec §6 unit economics as the baseline and the
five real-world benchmarks documented in FACILITY-TYPES.md §6 as the comparator set.

## 2. Evidence: Market Comparables

### Sola Salons

Sola Salons is the most direct structural analog to Artisan Square. Founded 2004, the
chain operates individual salon suites rented to independent beauty professionals —
the same "individually rented studio within a shared-infrastructure building" model
Artisan Square applies to craft trades. By 2023, Sola operated approximately 600+
locations in North America with approximately 20,000 salon professionals as tenants.
[CITATION-NEEDED: Sola Salons current location count and annual revenue per
mid-size location — Sola Salons published investor relations materials or franchise
disclosure document (FDD), Item 19; FDD filings are publicly accessible in states
requiring franchise registration.]

Industry reporting from the salon suite segment places annual revenue per mid-size
location at approximately $1.5–2.5M, with payback periods of 8–12 years for
well-operated locations in established markets — and substantially shorter payback
(5–7 years) for chain locations where the brand drives near-instant occupancy on
opening. [CITATION-NEEDED: Salon suite industry revenue and payback benchmarks —
IBISWorld salon industry report; Salon Today industry survey; or Sola FDD Item 19
for unit economics disclosure.] Profitability at chain scale is not in dispute; Sola
Salons is a documented commercial success. The relevant question for Artisan Square
is whether the longer payback inherent in lower-turnover craft tenants (vs. monthly
churn in salon suites) and higher build-out costs (vs. light salon suite infrastructure)
changes the fundamental viability of the model.

The Sola Salons unit economics are broadly favorable but operate at thinner
infrastructure: salon suites require 120V circuits, standard HVAC, and no industrial
exhaust — far below the capital intensity of Artisan Square's Type A wing or
commissary-licensed Type B wing. Artisan Square's higher build-out cost ($760k–1.45M
vs. a Sola Salons build at $300–700k for a comparable studio count) is the primary
reason the payback period extends relative to the salon model. [CITATION-NEEDED:
Sola Salons or comparable salon-suite franchise build-out cost — FDD Item 7 or
comparable industry cost survey.]

### La Cocina SF

La Cocina (San Francisco, founded 2005) operates as a nonprofit food business
incubator under a commissary umbrella license. It serves primarily immigrant women
building food businesses from shared commercial kitchen space accessed on an hourly
rotation basis — approximately $20–40/hour for shared-rotation studio access.
[CITATION-NEEDED: La Cocina SF current rate schedule — La Cocina published program
guides; rates cited ($20–40/hour) are the commonly reported 2020s-era range and
require confirmation against current published rates.] La Cocina's model is nonprofit,
subsidized by grants and below-market facility rents from the city of San Francisco.
It is not a private market-return vehicle; it is a mission-driven facility whose
economics work because the subsidy structure converts otherwise-marginal financials
into operational viability.

La Cocina is the gold standard for the commissary umbrella licensing structure that
Artisan Square's Type B wing adopts directly. But the economic model does not
translate: La Cocina's success depends on grant income and below-market facilities
that a private Artisan Square operator cannot assume. The market-comparable lesson
from La Cocina is that the commissary-only food incubator model requires subsidy to
achieve operational viability — the private-return question demands either higher rents
than La Cocina charges or a materially different occupancy structure (dedicated studios
at $1,100–1,750/month vs. hourly rotation at $20–40/hr).

Artisan Square's Type B model produces the higher-rent structure by design: dedicated
studios at market rates rather than shared hourly access. At $1,100–1,750/month for
a 200 sq ft baking studio, a tenant working 20+ hours per week of production pays
less than shared-rotation commissary rates for the equivalent time. The dedicated-studio
structure is both more revenue-dense for the operator and economically superior for
the tenant operating at production scale.

### Artisans Asylum

Artisans Asylum (Somerville, MA) operates approximately 52,000 sq ft with approximately
160 dedicated studios at varying scales, plus large shared-equipment bays. It is
structured as a nonprofit. [CITATION-NEEDED: Artisans Asylum current studio count,
building square footage, and membership structure — artisansasylum.com or direct
contact with Artisans Asylum.] Studio rents range from approximately $100–200/month
for the smaller studio tiers, aggregating to roughly $200–300k gross annual from
studio rents alone before equipment membership income. Operating costs at Artisans
Asylum's scale run higher than this gross, making grant income and membership
programming revenue essential to the nonprofit's viability.

The Artisans Asylum model confirms that heavy-trade multi-studio facilities are
operationally achievable, but its economics work only as a nonprofit: the studio rents
do not cover building costs, and the model depends on institutional support and
equipment-bay membership income that Artisan Square does not replicate. Artisan Square
operates with substantially higher per-studio rents ($1,300–2,400/month Type A;
$875–1,750/month Type B) and relies on occupancy for financial viability rather than
grant income. The nonprofit/for-profit structural difference is the key variable:
Artisans Asylum is not evidence that private market-return Artisan Square is viable,
but it is evidence that the operational model — dedicated studios, heavy infrastructure,
multi-trade tenancy — functions at scale.

### East End Maker Hub

East End Maker Hub (Houston, TX) is a commercial, for-profit heavy-fabrication
facility with large-format industrial studios rented to small manufacturing businesses.
[CITATION-NEEDED: East End Maker Hub current studio rates and infrastructure
specifications — eastendmakerhub.com or direct contact; rates cited ($18–28/sq ft/year
for raw industrial space) are from type-a-heavy.md §5 and require current verification.]
Its economic model is a private market-return play, but it operates at a fundamentally
different scale: studios are designed for small manufacturing businesses, not sole-
proprietor artisan practitioners, and the infrastructure is raw industrial space without
the commissary, compliance overhead, or metered 3-phase that Artisan Square Type A
studios provide. East End Maker Hub demonstrates that for-profit heavy-industrial
shared studio space can achieve commercial viability, but at a different capital
structure, rent level, and tenant profile than Artisan Square.

### Positioning Artisan Square

Artisan Square sits in a gap between these four benchmarks:

- Higher infrastructure cost per studio than Sola Salons (heavier trades, commissary
  compliance) but the same individual-studio-rental model at comparable rent ranges
- Higher rent and private-return focus than La Cocina (dedicated studios, not shared
  hourly rotation) but the same commissary umbrella mechanism
- Higher per-studio rent than Artisans Asylum (market-rate vs. subsidized nonprofit
  levels) but the same multi-trade dedicated-studio operational model
- Artisan-scale and commissary-integrated where East End Maker Hub is manufacturing-scale
  and food-excluded

None of the four fully occupies the Artisan Square position: a for-profit, multi-trade,
commissary-licensed, dedicated-studio building at artisan practitioner scale. The
absence of a direct comparable is both an evidence gap and a strategic opening.

## 3. Market-Path Verdict Per Facility Type

Using design spec §6.3 as the baseline and FACILITY-TYPES.md §3 as the supporting
economics table:

### Type B Only (Easiest to Build)

Build-out cost: $200k–450k (land excluded). Annual gross rent potential (14–20 studios
at 85% occupancy): $156k–265k/year. Annual operating costs (estimated): $120k–210k.
NOI at target occupancy: marginal to moderate positive.

**Payback at 85% occupancy:** 10–16 years (estimated, from FACILITY-TYPES.md §3 payback
range). [CITATION-NEEDED: Type-B-only payback not separately modeled in design spec §6;
figure is estimated by applying spec §6.3 methodology to Type-B-only build-out and
operating cost ranges from FACILITY-TYPES.md §3.] The tenant pool for Type B studios
(bakers, fiber artists, small-batch food producers) is substantially larger than Type A,
and Type B permits a retail-commercial zoning footprint that reduces land cost and
increases foot traffic.

**Verdict, Location 1 standalone:** MARGINAL. Payback exceeds 10 years under base-case
assumptions; requires sustained 85%+ occupancy through the payback period, with no
chain brand to drive occupancy. The commissary umbrella creates a self-selecting tenant
marketing channel that partially offsets this risk — bakers who need commissary access
find the facility. But the single-location standalone is thin.

**Verdict, chain scale (Locations 3–5):** GOOD. Brand drives near-full occupancy on
opening. The permitting playbook compresses the engineering cost from $80–150k at
Location 1 to $8–15k at Locations 3–5, materially improving the build-out economics.
Type B-only has the smallest competitive moat of any Artisan Square format (a food
incubator with dedicated studios is a simpler project than any format including Type A),
but at chain scale, brand and operational excellence produce the competitive protection.

### Type A Only (Heavy Infrastructure, Thin Tenant Pool)

Build-out cost: $350k–700k (land excluded). Annual gross rent potential (6–8 studios
at 85% occupancy): $84k–163k/year. Annual operating costs (estimated): $140k–230k.
NOI at target occupancy: breakeven to slightly negative.

**Payback at 85% occupancy:** 14–20 years (estimated). [CITATION-NEEDED: Type-A-only
payback not separately modeled in design spec §6; estimated from FACILITY-TYPES.md §3
payback range.] The Type A tenant pool — journeyman smiths, bladesmiths, metalwork
artists, production ceramicists operating as independent businesses — is thin in most
suburban markets. The graduation path requires 3–4 years to produce the next cohort
from an anchor training studio. Without an active pipeline, occupancy is the binding
constraint.

**Verdict, Location 1 standalone:** FAIL. The combination of high build-out cost
($350–700k), thin tenant pool (6–8 studios maximum at market size), and long payback
(14–20 years) does not produce a viable private-return investment at a single location.
The Artisans Asylum comparison is instructive: even a well-resourced nonprofit at
52,000 sq ft requires grant income to operate heavy-trade shared facilities.

**Verdict, chain scale with strong Type A brand:** MARGINAL. At Locations 3–5 where
the brand and the graduation path have established a regional practitioner network,
occupancy improves and the playbook compresses engineering costs. But Type A-only
never escapes the revenue ceiling of 6–8 studios per location. The CERES-optimal path
is not Type A-only standalone; it is Type A as a wing within a combined-campus building
where Type B studios cross-subsidize occupancy risk.

### Combined Campus (Highest Capital, Highest Potential)

Build-out cost: $760k–1.45M (adaptive reuse; land included per design spec §6.1).
Annual gross rent potential (24 studios at 85% occupancy): $306k–425k. Annual operating
costs: $245k–405k (design spec §6.2). NOI at 85% occupancy: $0–100k (design spec §6.3).

**Payback at 85% occupancy:** 12–15 years (design spec §6.3). At 95% occupancy: 8–10
years (design spec §6.3 "strong" scenario). The combined campus's highest NOI potential
stems from the highest gross rent base: 24 studios at blended market rates produce
$300k–500k annual at full occupancy. The two-wing destination effect anchors occupancy
above 85% more reliably than either single-wing format alone.

**Verdict, Location 1 adaptive reuse:** MARGINAL. The 12–15 year payback at 85%
occupancy is long but not unfeasible for a patient infrastructure investor. The primary
risk is Location 1's dual-occupancy permitting (10–18 months from permit submission;
first-ever in many jurisdictions) and the operational complexity of managing both
commissary compliance and Type A safety oversight simultaneously before operational
procedures are documented.

**Verdict, chain scale (Locations 2–5, purpose-built):** GOOD. Purpose-built combined
campus at Locations 2+ applies the Location 1 playbook: stamped engineering drawings,
established AHJ relationships, operational procedures from Location 1 experience.
Payback compresses to 10–14 years (FACILITY-TYPES.md §3), and brand-driven occupancy
at near-100% levels on opening (waitlists form by Locations 3–5 per design spec §6.4)
pushes toward the 8–10 year payback range.

**Verdict, with civic co-investment:** GOOD. Below-market land, an anchor training
studio subsidy, or an economic development grant of $100k–200k at Location 1 converts
the capital structure materially. At 10,000 households, civic investment of <$15/
household/year = $150k/year of civic subsidy, which at even a one-time grant level
reduces the Location 1 build-out cost into a payback range that works on private
financing (design spec §3).

### Small-Town Lite (Reduced Capital, Thinner Market)

Build-out cost: $500k–900k (design spec §8). Annual gross rent potential (12–18 studios
at 85% occupancy, blended Type A/B rates): est. $126k–220k/year. Annual operating costs
(estimated): $160k–280k (FACILITY-TYPES.md §3). NOI at target occupancy: breakeven to
thin positive.

**Payback at 85% occupancy:** 14–20 years without civic subsidy; 10–14 years with civic
anchor subsidy (design spec §3; FACILITY-TYPES.md §3 payback range). [CITATION-NEEDED:
small-town-lite unit economics are estimated from design spec §8 rollout table and §3
civic text; no standalone small-town-lite financial model exists; see FACILITY-TYPES.md
§3 CITATION-NEEDED note.]

The small-town-lite format's fundamental economics challenge is the market-depth
constraint: a town of 5,000–15,000 population supports a practitioner pool thin enough
that 85% occupancy is achievable only with strong civic co-investment bringing an anchor
training studio with guaranteed civic tenancy. Without the civic anchor, occupancy risk
in a thin market makes the 14–20 year payback difficult to sustain.

**Verdict, market path standalone:** MARGINAL. The market base is too thin to sustain
full occupancy through a 14–20 year payback without the brand maturity that only comes
at Locations 6+. This is not a failure mode — it is a finding that the small-town-lite
format is not the right entry point for a private operator without civic support.

**Verdict, with civic co-investment:** GOOD. CERES data shows towns of 5,000+ can
justify civic investment at <$15/household/year (design spec §3). A training studio
subsidy of $3,000–7,000/month that civic partners underwrite converts the anchor slot
from a below-market drag on operator economics to a fully-compensated pipeline asset.
The small-town format is the strongest civic-lens case of any Artisan Square type; see
Section 5 below.

## 4. Cooperative-Path Verdict

Can a tenant cooperative own the Artisan Square building rather than renting studios
from a private operator?

The cooperative ownership model reframes the economics: tenants buy into the facility
as members rather than paying monthly rent to an external operator. The revenue model
becomes member equity contributions + ongoing occupancy fees, and the governance model
becomes democratic member control rather than single-operator management.

**Revenue structure check (combined campus, 24 studios):** At $1,500/month average
studio occupancy fee, 24 studios × $1,500 = $36,000/month gross = $432,000/year. Annual
operating costs at $325,000 (design spec §6.3 midpoint). Surplus before debt service:
approximately $107,000/year. On a $1.1M property (midpoint of $760k–1.45M build-out),
debt service on a 20-year commercial loan at 6.5% on $800k (assuming $300k member equity
contributions) = approximately $72,000/year. NOI after debt service: approximately
$35,000/year — thin but positive.

The critical variable is the initial equity requirement. A worker-owned LLC or cooperative
structure needs member equity contributions to reduce the debt load to a serviceable
level. For a ~$1.1M property, $200k–400k in member equity buy-in at $8,000–17,000 per
member across 24 studio members is within the range of feasibility for practitioners
who have been in business for 2+ years — but represents a significant barrier for
pre-commercial or early-stage artisans. The Artisan Square graduation path produces
exactly the right cohort (2–4 years of business-building before moving to standalone
space or coop ownership), but the timing requires that the cooperative structure be
contemplated from the beginning and that founding members have reached the financial
maturity to contribute equity.

Real-world cooperative real estate models provide structural analogs. Urban Village
Cooperative (Minneapolis) and community land trust models demonstrate that cooperative
real estate ownership by small-business operators is structurally achievable — the
legal structures exist, CDFI and credit union lending has developed specifically for
this purpose, and the operational governance models are documented. [CITATION-NEEDED:
Urban Village Cooperative, Minneapolis — program structure, equity buy-in requirements,
CDFI lender relationship; confirm from Urban Village Cooperative public documentation
or equivalent cooperative real estate model; land trust model documentation from
national Community Land Trust Network.] The gap is not legal or structural; it is
financial: cooperative ownership requires either gap financing from a CDFI or significant
member equity at formation.

**Verdict, single building, no CDFI financing:** MARGINAL. The equity requirement
($200k–400k member contribution) is achievable but constitutes a meaningful barrier
for the practitioner profile Artisan Square serves. A 24-member cooperative contributing
$8,000–17,000 per member to buy into a building they have not yet occupied requires
members to have either accumulated capital (inconsistent with the artisan startup
profile) or institutional backing. Without gap financing, the coop path at a single
location is marginal.

**Verdict, with CDFI or cooperative lender gap financing:** GOOD. CDFIs and cooperative
lending programs exist specifically to address this gap, providing below-market-rate
gap financing that reduces the member equity threshold to $3,000–8,000 per member —
a range achievable for practitioners 2+ years into their business. The civic version
of this model, where a community land trust holds the land and a tenant cooperative
holds the improvements, reduces the total capitalization further. This path is good
where the operator builds CDFI relationships as part of the Location 1 development
process; it is not a default assumption for private market-path operators.

## 5. Civic-Path Verdict

The civic case for Artisan Square co-investment rests on three outputs that align
directly with municipal economic-development mandates:

**Workforce development:** The anchor training studio produces journeyman-level
practitioners on 6-month residency cycles. A combined-campus building with one training
studio per wing produces 3–4 trained practitioners per year. Over a 5-year period, this
represents 15–20 trained artisan practitioners who either stay in the local economy as
Artisan Square tenants or graduate to standalone commercial space — a workforce
development outcome that regional vocational programs and community colleges value
highly.

**Local economic multiplier:** Artisan Square tenants purchase materials and supplies
locally (steel, flour, fiber), sell products locally, and employ local part-time
assistants. Each tenant operating as a sole proprietor or small LLC generates $40–120k
in annual revenue with a local economic multiplier estimated at 1.2–1.8× (dollars
cycling through the local economy before leaking out). [CITATION-NEEDED: local economic
multiplier for sole-proprietor artisan businesses in small to mid-size US cities —
economic development literature; IMPLAN model or equivalent input-output analysis;
cited range is broadly consistent with small-business multiplier research but requires
a specific primary source for precision.]

**Food security:** Baking studios in the Type B wing produce locally-made bread and
specialty food products available to the surrounding community. In small towns with
limited grocery access, an Artisan Square with active baking tenants contributes to
food security in a way that the civic lens recognizes explicitly.

**The cost per household calculation:** At 10,000 households, $150k/year in civic
subsidy = $15/household/year — consistent with the CERES design spec §3 benchmark
that towns can justify civic investment at this level. A one-time civic grant of $150k
(rather than annual subsidy) contributes to Location 1 capitalization, compresses the
payback period, and converts a marginal private-return investment to a commercially
viable one. [CITATION-NEEDED: CERES data on civic investment threshold at <$15/
household/year — the figure is derived from internal CERES Tier A simulation results
and design spec §3; the specific simulation cells that confirm this threshold are in
simulations/tier-a-comparator/results/SUMMARY.md.]

**Civic investment forms observed in practice:**

- Below-market land contribution: municipalities with industrial surplus land can
  provide site acquisition at significant discounts to market value, reducing the
  $400k–800k building acquisition cost
- Training studio subsidy: a civic economic-development grant covering 24–36 months of
  anchor studio rent at below-market rates ($3,000–6,000/month) converts the training
  pipeline from a below-market drag on operator revenue to a fully-underwritten asset
- Economic-development grant for build-out: one-time capital contributions from
  municipal economic-development funds, USDA rural development grants, or state
  manufacturing assistance programs offset build-out cost

**Verdict, towns of 5,000+ with economic-development mandate:** GOOD. The civic case
is straightforward: workforce development, local economic multiplier, food security.
The cost per household is well within documented economic-development budget ranges.
Civic co-investment is not required for private market-path viability at chain scale,
but it converts Location 1 from MARGINAL to GOOD and converts small-town-lite from
MARGINAL to GOOD as a standalone investment. Civic involvement emerges organically from
the Artisan Square model — the design spec §3 notes it is not structured in, but
anticipated to appear — because the operator's outputs align with what towns are already
trying to fund.

**Verdict, towns under 5,000:** CONDITIONAL. Towns under 5,000 typically lack the
economic-development budget and practitioner pool base to sustain even a small-town-lite
format. Civic investment from a town of 3,000 does not reach $150k/year at $15/
household without representing a disproportionate commitment of the municipal economic-
development budget. The small-town-lite format at this population scale is viable only
as a regional anchor serving multiple surrounding small communities — a more complex
civic governance structure than the single-municipality model.

## 6. The Operator-Pipeline Binding Constraint

The binding constraint on Artisan Square viability is not equipment economics, building
code complexity, or financing — it is operator sourcing. Where do the journeyman smiths,
bakers, and weavers come from?

The ceres-panel R1 review of the multi-trade CERES pitch (Plan E) identified this as the
primary non-answered question in the CERES argument: the simulation confirms that artisan
production is economically viable in repair/specialty/premium niches, but confirming
viability does not produce practitioners. A building full of vacant studios — even one
with metered 3-phase and a commissary license — does not generate the practitioner
pipeline independently.

The Artisan Square response is the anchor training studio (design spec §7.3): 6-month
subsidized residencies for pre-commercial artisans, partnerships with vocational schools
and culinary programs, and a right-of-first-offer clause that converts residents to
full-price tenants when vacancies open. This is the mechanism, not a guarantee. The
mechanism functions only if:

1. The anchor training studio is staffed or partnered with a program that actively
   places residents — not an empty subsidy waiting for applicants
2. The residency cycle produces practitioners capable of operating production businesses
   at market occupancy within 6–12 months of graduation
3. The practitioner pipeline is producing tenants 2–3 years before the building has
   vacancies — meaning the training relationship must begin before the building opens

Without an active practitioner pipeline functioning by Year 2 post-opening, the
occupancy stabilization trajectory extends materially. The design spec §6.3 "target"
scenario assumes 85% occupancy — approximately 20 of 24 studios occupied. If the
training pipeline delivers only 2–3 ready practitioners per year rather than the 4–6
needed to offset natural churn (graduation, non-renewal, business failure), occupancy
stabilizes at 70–75% rather than 85%, which is approximately the breakeven scenario in
the spec §6.3 table: no positive NOI, no payback progress.

**Implication for the market verdict:** The MARGINAL ratings for Location 1 across all
facility types are contingent on the practitioner pipeline functioning. An Artisan Square
Location 1 with an active training partnership (a vocational school, culinary program,
or community college placing 4–6 residents per year into the anchor studio) is a
better-than-MARGINAL investment. Without a functioning pipeline, the payback period
extends past 20 years across all facility types, and the market verdict shifts from
MARGINAL to FAIL.

This is the single most important practical finding of this research cycle: the
Artisan Square operator must secure practitioner pipeline partnerships before breaking
ground, not after the building opens.

## 7. Overall Verdict

**Verdict: mixed. Confidence: moderate.**

The Artisan Square building-operator model is commercially viable under the right
conditions and commercially marginal under others. The distinction is not primarily
about facility type or financial structure — it is about the configuration of three
variables:

1. **Location sequence** — Location 1 is marginal by design; Locations 3–5 are good
   by design. The chain model is not an optimization; it is a structural requirement
   for strong private-market viability. An operator who commits to only one location
   is making a 12–20 year payback bet with significant execution risk. An operator
   who commits to 3–5 locations is making a substantially better-structured investment.

2. **Practitioner pipeline** — Active training partnerships producing 4–6 tenants per
   year per location are a prerequisite for 85%+ occupancy, which is a prerequisite
   for positive NOI. Without the pipeline, occupancy falls to the breakeven range and
   the investment does not recover.

3. **Civic co-investment** — Not required for chain viability, but it converts Location 1
   from marginal to good and converts small-town-lite from marginal to good as a
   standalone investment. The civic path is good wherever it is available, and it is
   frequently available to operators who document the workforce development, multiplier,
   and food security outputs that civic partners are already trying to fund.

**Market path:** MARGINAL at Location 1 (any facility type); GOOD at chain scale
(Locations 3–5, combined campus preferred); GOOD at Location 1 with civic co-investment.

**Cooperative path:** MARGINAL for single building (member equity requirement is a
barrier); GOOD where CDFI or cooperative lender provides gap financing and the
practitioner cohort has reached the financial maturity to participate in equity ownership.

**Civic path:** GOOD for towns of 5,000+ with economic-development mandate; CONDITIONAL
for towns under 5,000.

**The private chain model — a single operator building 3–5 locations using the Location 1
permitting playbook as the IP foundation — is the correct execution path.** Civic
involvement emerges without being structurally required. Cooperative ownership is a
viable downstream transition for tenants who have graduated to financial maturity and
want to own their space — it is a tenant-advancement mechanism, not a first-build model.

**Implications for Plan K catalog:** Catalog entries should span a range of scenarios
from marginal (Location-1 standalone, Type A only) to good (chain prototype
combined-campus, civic co-investment). Entries should be honest about which
configurations require the chain economics or civic co-investment to pencil out. The
Tier A simulation will identify which specific configurations clear the market and coop
thresholds at each context type (village, town, small-city) — the MARKET-VERDICT
provides the framing; the simulation provides the per-entry answer.
