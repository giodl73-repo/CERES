# Artisan Square: Combined Campus Model

## 1. The Combined Campus Concept

A combined campus places Type A (heavy trades: forge, metalwork, kiln) and Type B
(light trades: baking, weaving, leather, ceramics) studios in a single building
envelope under a single operator. The defining feature is not the building — it is
the adjacency.

A baker's customer, walking out after picking up a sourdough order, passes a weaver's
street-facing window. The audible rhythm of a smith's hammer pulls curious foot
traffic toward the building from the street. A ceramicist and a leatherworker share
the loading dock on delivery day and exchange referrals. None of these effects occurs
in a Type A-only or Type B-only facility, where every tenant operates in the same
trade category and their customer bases largely overlap. Multi-trade adjacency creates
a destination; single-trade concentration creates a cluster. The commercial difference
matters for tenant retention and occupancy rate.

The Manchester Craft and Design Centre (Manchester, UK) documents this cross-trade
foot-traffic dynamic directly: tenants in unrelated trades report that their retail
traffic increases when adjacent studios in different trades are occupied, because
the building itself becomes a reason to visit rather than a stop for a specific
purchase [CITATION-NEEDED: Manchester Craft and Design Centre tenant survey data or
operator report documenting cross-trade foot-traffic benefit; claim is consistent with
general mixed-retail destination literature but requires facility-specific sourcing].

The combined campus is the highest-complexity and highest-capital Artisan Square
configuration. It is also the configuration most likely to produce the destination
effect that anchors occupancy above 85%.

## 2. The IBC Section 508 Strategy

The regulatory barrier that prevents most facilities from combining forge operations
and food production in a single building is the occupancy classification conflict
under the International Building Code. Forge and metalwork operations classify under
IBC Group F-2 (light manufacturing, low-hazard industrial). Commercial food
production under commissary licensing classifies under IBC Group B (business) or,
at higher throughput, Group F-2 as well. The conflict is not the classification
itself — it is the incompatibility of combustion byproducts and food safety
environments.

IBC Section 508 provides the resolution. Under the Mixed Use Protected approach
(§508.4 in IBC 2021), different occupancy groups may occupy the same building if
separated by fire-resistance-rated construction meeting the requirements for the
more restrictive occupancy classification [CITATION-NEEDED: IBC 2021 §508.4 Mixed
Use Protected occupancy separation requirements; confirm 2-hour assembly requirement
for F-2/B separation in the edition adopted by the target jurisdiction]. For the
Artisan Square combined campus, this means a 2-hour fire-rated separation assembly
between the Type A and Type B wings.

In construction terms, a 2-hour fire-rated assembly is achievable via concrete
masonry unit (CMU) wall (8-inch minimum, fully grouted) — the most common solution
in light-industrial adaptive reuse — or fire-rated gypsum board assembly (Type X,
per UL design listings) typical in purpose-built construction. The specific assembly
depends on jurisdiction, wall type (load-bearing vs. non-load-bearing), and the
local authority having jurisdiction (AHJ). A licensed structural engineer must
specify the assembly before permit submission.

Practical consequences of the §508 strategy: each wing requires its own exit access
path and its own fire suppression zone (NFPA 13), with the Type A zone sized for
Class D metal fire risk [CITATION-NEEDED: NFPA 13 requirements for suppression
zoning across occupancy separations; Class D applicability to metalwork studios].
Each wing requires independently controlled ventilation (§3). The single building
envelope reduces construction cost per square foot versus two separate structures:
shared foundation, roof, exterior envelope, and utility mains are cheaper to build
once. The 2-hour wall is the price of that consolidation.

Jurisdictional adoption of IBC 2021 varies; some AHJs interpret mixed-use
protections more conservatively than the base code. The permitting playbook Artisan
Square corporate builds from Location 1 must document which interpretations were
encountered and how the operator resolved them.

## 3. The Mechanical Spine

The mechanical spine is the zone between the two wings housing the loading dock,
materials storage cages, management office, shared bathrooms, and the electrical
and ventilation infrastructure serving both wings
[specs/2026-04-19-artisan-square-design.md §4.2].

**Electrical backbone.** A single 1,000A 3-phase main service feeds the building.
Sub-panels distribute to each wing: 200A 3-phase per Type A studio, 60A 240V
single-phase per Type B studio, each with individual metering. Tenants pay their
own consumption; the operator pays common-area load only
[specs/2026-04-19-artisan-square-design.md §4.5].

Over-building the main service at construction is cheap; upgrading after occupancy
is not. Large commercial service upgrades (above 400A) require utility coordination
— transformer work, new service entrance conductors, metering section — with lead
times typically 6 to 18 months in suburban markets [CITATION-NEEDED: utility service
upgrade lead times for large commercial electrical services; cite utility industry or
construction industry data]. Main conduit runs sized correctly during construction
cost a fraction of that disruption.

**Ventilation spine.** Two entirely separate exhaust stacks — one industrial-grade
for Type A, one food-grade for Type B — run from studio connections to roofline.
Forge and metalwork exhaust contains combustion byproducts and metal particulates
that cannot share ductwork with food-production spaces under any commissary
standards interpretation. A single makeup air unit serves both wings through
dampered separate supply zones, allowing Type B food spaces to maintain positive
pressure (blocking particulate migration) while Type A spaces manage their own
exhaust-supply balance [CITATION-NEEDED: ASHRAE or equivalent guidance on makeup
air unit design for mixed-occupancy buildings with separated exhaust systems;
damper zoning practice for positive-pressure food production spaces].

**Commissary scope boundary.** The commissary umbrella license covers Type B only.
No space with metal grinding, torch use, forge operation, or kiln firing falls under
the commissary in any circumstances. The 2-hour fire-rated wall is the physical
correlate of this boundary: commissary jurisdiction ends where the industrial
occupancy permit begins.

## 4. Cross-Traffic Dynamics and the Anchor Training Studio

Multi-trade adjacency is a customer-acquisition mechanism, not a social program.
A customer who visits a baking tenant to buy bread does not need to know the weaver
exists before arriving — the building's layout creates the discovery. A customer who
discovers handwoven goods on a third visit has become a customer of a second tenant
at zero marketing cost to either. The destination effect increases dwell time and
return visit frequency across all tenants [CITATION-NEEDED: retail destination
literature on cross-tenant foot-traffic in mixed-trade configurations; Manchester
Craft and Design Centre operator data preferred if available].

This is also a retention mechanism. A smith considering a standalone space at lower
per-square-foot rent loses the referral network and cross-traffic. For tenants still
scaling, the network value typically exceeds the rent savings.

**The anchor training studio** is one studio at 150-200 sq ft, rented at 30-40%
below market rate for pre-commercial residency. This is an occupancy-pipeline
mechanism: the anchor studio produces the next cohort of tenants to replace
graduates [specs/2026-04-19-artisan-square-design.md §7.3]. Civic partners —
economic development agencies, vocational programs, community colleges — are
natural funders because the training output aligns with their mandated metrics.
The operator does not depend on civic funding but treats it as an accelerant
that converts the below-market slot into a fully-underwritten pipeline asset.

The anchor studio lease includes a right-of-first-offer clause: when a full-price
vacancy opens in the matching wing, the resident has first right before external
advertisement. A resident who converts to a full-price studio brings demonstrated
production capability and an established customer base — lower qualification risk
and no vacancy gap.

## 5. Functional Requirements Specific to Combined Campus

Requirements a combined campus must meet that Type A-only or Type B-only facilities
do not face:

**Fire-rated occupancy separation.** A 2-hour fire-rated assembly must continuously
separate the Type A and Type B wings from foundation to roofline. This is a
non-negotiable IBC §508.4 requirement. [CITATION-NEEDED: IBC 2021 §508.4 continuity
requirements for fire separation assemblies in mixed-occupancy buildings]

**Independent exhaust systems.** Type A and Type B stacks must not share duct
sections, plenums, or discharge points. They may share structural chase space in
the spine but are separate systems from studio connection to atmosphere. Forge
exhaust in food-production space is a health code violation independent of building
code requirements.

**Commissary scope exclusion.** The commissary umbrella explicitly excludes all
Type A space. The exclusion must appear in the commissary license application,
health department inspection records, and each Type A tenant lease. A boundary
failure risks the commissary license for all Type B tenants.

**Combined electrical capacity.** Main service = Type A wing demand + Type B wing
demand + 20% headroom. For a 24-studio combined campus (8 Type A at 200A 3-phase +
16 Type B at 60A 240V), the pre-headroom minimum is approximately 800A; the
specified 1,000A service covers the headroom requirement
[specs/2026-04-19-artisan-square-design.md §4.5].

**Zone-level utility metering.** Every studio in both wings must have individual
metering. Electrical consumption variance between a forge studio and a weaving studio
is large enough that unmetered combined-campus operation produces unrecoverable
operator cost.
