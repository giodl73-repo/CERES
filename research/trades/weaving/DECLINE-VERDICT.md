# Weaving — Decline-or-Restructuring Verdict

## 1. The Question

Did weaving as a local trade actually decline, or did it restructure? The distinction
matters for CERES because the answer determines what the equipment-economics hypothesis
can and cannot address.

If weaving principally *declined* — meaning demand for its products vanished — then
redesigning loom equipment cannot restore what was never an equipment problem. A cheaper
floor loom does not revive a trade whose customers switched to machine-woven cloth and
no longer need hand-woven yardage. If weaving principally *restructured* — meaning
viable economic forms migrated to different niches without overall demand loss — then
equipment redesign can, in principle, target the restructured niche and improve its
economics.

Weaving presents the clearest commodity-decline pattern in the CERES corpus. The power
loom (Cartwright 1785; commercially adopted 1820s–1840s in Britain, progressively
diffused globally through the second half of the nineteenth century) obliterated
hand-loom commodity cloth production faster and more completely than any other
industrial displacement event studied here. The question this verdict must answer is
whether anything survived that restructuring, and if so, what mechanism preserved it
and whether that mechanism is relevant to CERES's catalog development.

The answer is: yes, but narrowly. Commodity hand-weaving of cloth declined universally
across all four anchor cultures; the verdict for that segment is unambiguous. A genuine
luxury-specialty niche survived, but the survival mechanism was not equipment economics
— it was product differentiation beyond the reach of standardized factory output, and
in the Tokugawa case, also technological adaptation (Jacquard adoption) that changed
the organizational form. Any weaving catalog that targets commodity cloth production
has misread the evidence. One that targets bespoke, heritage, and artisan-specialty
cloth operates in the domain where restructuring, not decline, is the relevant pattern
— provided it confronts the fiber-sourcing dependency honestly.

## 2. Evidence Per Culture

### 2.1 Medieval Northern Europe

Verdict per canonical vocabulary: **mixed** — `restructuring` for the putting-out and
rural-production tier through the early modern period; `demand-collapse` for guild-town
forms specifically by the sixteenth century; `decline` for commodity broadcloth hand-
weaving after the power-loom era; `niche survival` for tapestry and premium handwoven
cloth into the present.

The medieval northern European record shows three structurally distinct production
forms operating simultaneously, each with a different decline trajectory.

**The guild-master tier** in Flemish broadcloth towns — Ghent, Ypres, Bruges — was
already losing structural position from the late fourteenth century through a
combination of English domestic cloth competition, disrupted wool supply during the
Hundred Years War, and internal political instability [medieval-northern-europe
§Decline or Restructuring; Munro 1999; Nicholas 1992]. The erosion of Flemish guild-
town dominance was not caused by the power loom; it preceded industrialization by
four centuries and was driven by a competitive reorganization of putting-out and rural
production. The guild form's institutional protections — searchers, quality controls,
apprenticeship requirements — could not survive the penetration of unincorporated rural
production [Ogilvie 2019]. This is `demand-collapse` for the guild-town organizational
form itself, not for weaving as a practice.

**The putting-out and rural tier** — geographically dispersed household piece-workers
producing for merchant-clothiers — expanded as the guild tier contracted. English
upland production (Yorkshire, the Cotswolds) grew substantially from the thirteenth
century onward precisely because it operated outside guild jurisdiction at lower labor
costs [Carus-Wilson 1952]. This form restructured the trade into a proto-industrial
configuration that persisted into the early nineteenth century — not as a stable
survival but as an intermediate form that itself became the victim of the power loom.

**The industrialization phase** produced the clearest documented decline sequence in
European weaving history. The spinning jenny (1764), water frame (1769), and spinning
mule (1779) first broke the spinning bottleneck, and the resulting abundance of cheap
machine-spun thread actually *increased* the number of hand looms in operation through
the early nineteenth century — the English handloom weaving workforce peaked around
1800–1820 [Thompson 1963; Bythell 1969]. The power loom then collapsed this expanded
workforce. Handloom weavers' wages fell 60–80% in the 1820s–1840s before the trade was
effectively extinguished as a mass occupation [Bythell 1969; CITATION-NEEDED: handloom
weaver wage-decline data, England 1800–1850 — Bythell 1969 statistical appendix or
Thompson 1963]. The handloom weavers' agony is one of the most thoroughly documented
cases of craft displacement by industrial machinery in the historical record.

**Tapestry and high-end specialty cloth** survived as restructured niches. Tapestry
(Arras, Brussels, Aubusson) and premium handwoven wool cloth (Harris Tweed and
equivalents) retained craft-workshop form because they were bespoke, non-standardizable,
and demanded the weaver's skill as part of the product's value proposition. Their
survival reflects economics of differentiation, not cultural preservation
[medieval-northern-europe §Decline or Restructuring].

The chapter's own verdict label — `mixed` — is confirmed here. Conflating guild-town
collapse, industrial displacement, and luxury-niche survival into either "weaving
declined" or "weaving restructured" would misrepresent a differentiated historical
record.

### 2.2 Song-Era China

Verdict per canonical vocabulary: **mixed** — `demand-collapse` for everyday ramie
and hemp household weaving, first displaced by cotton expansion under Yuan and Ming,
then by Western machine textiles from the 1840s; `regulatory-dissolution` for the
imperial *jianghu* workshop system under the Republic; `restructuring` for luxury
figured silk and kesi as prestige niches surviving to the present.

The Song textile economy was stratified across three structurally distinct production
forms: state imperial workshops (*guansi*), rural household producers, and urban
commercial workshops [song-china §The Weaver's Place]. Their decline trajectories
diverged sharply.

**Everyday ramie and hemp household weaving** — the volume base of Song textile
production — experienced `demand-collapse` in two stages. First, the Yuan-period
expansion of cotton cultivation and the cotton gin (*mian jiaoche*) progressively
displaced ramie and hemp as the everyday textile for non-elite households across most
of China. By mid-Ming, cotton cloth had substantially replaced ramie and hemp
[Bray 1997, ch. 6; Chao 1977; Nishijima 1984]. This was a structural shift within
Chinese domestic textile supply that eliminated the Song-era ramie household weaving
economy before any Western industrial competition appeared. Second, from the 1840s
onward, British and Japanese machine-woven cotton cloth undercut hand-woven cotton
production in Chinese domestic markets, producing a further demand-collapse for
hand-woven everyday cloth comparable to the contemporaneous collapse in Western Europe
[Feuerwerker 1970; song-china §Decline or Restructuring; CITATION-NEEDED:
machine-textile import competition effects on Chinese hand-weaving, 1840s–1890s —
Feuerwerker 1970 or Hamilton and Chang on Chinese cloth markets].

**The imperial workshop system** (*jianghu* artisan registration, imperial *zhizao ju*
under Ming and Qing) was dissolved under the Republic and its successors — a
`regulatory-dissolution` that was institutional rather than market-driven.

**Luxury figured silk and kesi** survived and in some periods intensified as prestige
categories. Suzhou and Hangzhou imperial workshops continued under Ming and Qing
administration [CITATION-NEEDED: Ming-Qing *zhizao ju* continuity with Song imperial
silk workshops; Kuhn 1988 or Nishijima 1984]. Suzhou embroidery and Hangzhou figured
silk continue as recognized artistic-craft forms today. This survival reflects a pattern
common across luxury textile forms globally: machine production competed on cost for
volume goods but could not displace craft production in identifiably hand-made prestige
categories where the production process itself is part of the value proposition
[song-china §Decline or Restructuring].

The CERES-relevant finding from the Song case is bifurcated by fiber and product tier.
The ramie/hemp household-weaving economy has no recoverable market base — it was
eliminated not by the power loom but by cotton's superior economics, centuries before
industrial competition arrived. The luxury silk trajectory is the relevant survival
analogue for CERES, and it survived through product differentiation and prestige
positioning, not through equipment redesign.

### 2.3 Tokugawa Japan

Verdict per canonical vocabulary: **mixed** — `decline` for cotton household weaving,
whose economic role was ended by Meiji industrialization; `restructuring` for Nishijin
luxury silk brocade, which survived through organizational disruption and technological
adaptation; `regulatory-dissolution` for the *shokunin* estate and *nakama* guild
structures, abolished 1870.

Tokugawa Japan presents the bifurcated outcome most clearly of any anchor culture.
Two weaving worlds existed within the same empire, operated on different technologies,
served different markets, and experienced categorically different post-Meiji trajectories.

**Cotton household weaving** was acutely vulnerable to Meiji industrialization.
British machine-spun cotton yarn entered from the 1860s; the Osaka Spinning Company
(1882) and subsequent domestic mills produced machine-spun thread at costs far below
hand-spinning; power-loom weaving of cotton cloth entered through state-sponsored mills
and private investment from the 1880s [Morris-Suzuki 1994; tokugawa-japan §Decline or
Restructuring; CITATION-NEEDED: Meiji cotton textile industrialization timeline —
Morris-Suzuki 1994 specific pages]. Cotton household weaving's comparative advantage
was entirely labor cost, in a context where rural women had no alternative market access
for their labor. Machine-spun yarn was cheaper and more uniform; machine-woven plain
cloth undercut household-woven cloth on price in the commodity grade. By the early
twentieth century, rural household cotton weaving as a significant source of household
income had effectively ended in lowland commercial-market areas. This is `decline` in
the canonical sense: the form did not restructure into a viable specialty niche; it
simply ceased to be economical [tokugawa-japan §Decline or Restructuring].

**Nishijin silk brocade** experienced severe disruption but not extinction. The immediate
Meiji shocks were multiple: abolition of the *nakama* guild structure (1870) exposed
Nishijin to unrestricted competition; the movement of the imperial court from Kyoto to
Tokyo (1869) disrupted the patronage base; imported Lyonnais silk brocade and machine-
produced silk fabrics created product competition at the luxury tier [tokugawa-japan
§Decline or Restructuring; CITATION-NEEDED: Nishijin crisis of the early Meiji period —
specialist Nishijin history or Hauser]. The key adaptive move was the 1872 Lyon mission
and the subsequent adoption of the Jacquard mechanism, which replaced draw-boy pattern
selection with punched-card automation and reduced minimum crew for patterned work to a
single operator [Morris-Suzuki 1994; CITATION-NEEDED: Jacquard adoption timeline in
Nishijin — Morris-Suzuki 1994 on Meiji technology transfer; specialist Nishijin history
for specific delegation date and adoption rate]. Nishijin survived as a recognized luxury
specialty district, continues to operate today, and commands prices that reflect both
genuine craft skill and the symbolic capital of historical continuity.

The CERES-relevant distinction between the two Tokugawa cases is that the survival
mechanisms differed: Nishijin survived through market-oriented technological adaptation,
not state regulatory protection. The Jacquard adoption is the critical variable —
without it, the labor economics of draw-loom silk brocade would have been fatal.
This is `restructuring` of a surviving niche, not preservation of the pre-industrial
form intact.

### 2.4 American Frontier

Verdict per canonical vocabulary: **decline** driven by `demand-collapse` for commodity
cloth; `restructuring` for specialty hand-weaving, with terminal trajectory from
skilled-labor reproduction failure.

The American frontier record is the most complete and rapidly executed decline sequence
in the CERES corpus. It provides a quasi-experimental situation: the Lowell mills were
operational by 1814 and producing factory cloth in volume from the 1820s, meaning that
for the entire effective span of the frontier period documented here, household weaving
was competing against machine-made cloth that was progressively cheaper and more uniform
with each decade [Dublin 1979; american-frontier §1].

**Commodity cloth production** experienced demand-collapse driven by price and quality
simultaneously. Household-produced plain cloth — linsey-woolsey, all-wool flannel,
plain cotton — was not merely undercut on price; it was inferior in quality and
regularity to factory alternatives. The collapse happened earliest for households with
cash income and latest for the most isolated and cash-poor frontier settlements. By the
1880s, railroad-freight-accessible general merchandise stores made factory cloth
available at prices that made household weaving irrational across virtually all
settled regions [Strasser 1982; Tryon 1917]. The women who abandoned household weaving
were not losing a treasured practice; they were escaping a burdensome one [Strasser 1982].
The demand-collapse mechanism was loss of willingness to invest household labor time
in weaving, not loss of technical capacity. Looms, skills, and fiber were present;
the economic rationale was not.

**Specialty professional weaving** — Jacquard-equipped itinerant weavers producing
coverlets and custom rag rugs — survived the mid-nineteenth century by restructuring
away from commodity competition into non-fungible custom goods. Factory production
could not replicate a named weaver's custom pattern coverlet from a specific household's
wool yarn. This niche was genuine and documented [american-frontier §Decline or
Restructuring; Hood 2003]. However, the restructuring had a terminal trajectory: as
the generation of pre-industrial-trained weavers aged out, no economic incentive
existed for new entrants to invest training time in professional hand-loom weaving
when mill employment was available. By 1900, the professional itinerant weaving trade
had effectively disappeared from the frontier commercial landscape [CITATION-NEEDED:
census documentation of professional hand-loom weavers, American rural records,
1870–1900 — demographic study using IPUMS or published census trade-classification
data].

The American frontier case is the clearest partial falsification of the equipment-
economics hypothesis for weaving specifically. The demand for commodity cloth hand-
weaving was eliminated — permanently — by industrial production. No equipment redesign
can restore demand for hand-woven plain yardage when factory cloth is available.

## 3. Cross-Cultural Pattern

Four consistent patterns emerge from the evidence across all four anchor cultures.

**Commodity cloth hand-weaving declined universally.** In every anchor culture that
underwent industrialization, the hand production of plain commodity cloth — everyday
yardage in linen, cotton, ramie, hemp, or basic wool — was displaced by machine
production. The mechanism varied: the power loom in northern Europe and Japan; earlier
cotton-expansion displacement of ramie in China; railroad-accessible factory cloth on
the American frontier. The pattern is perfectly consistent. No anchor culture produced
a documented case of commodity-grade hand-weaving successfully competing against
industrial alternatives on price. This is the strongest single finding in the weaving
cross-cultural record and the clearest finding in the CERES corpus to date. Plan H
catalog entries targeting commodity cloth production face an adverse demand trajectory
documented in all four cultures with no counter-evidence.

**Luxury and specialty hand-weaving survived through product differentiation, not
equipment economics.** Tapestry in northern Europe; kesi and figured silk in China;
Nishijin brocade in Japan; Jacquard coverlets on the American frontier — in each case,
the surviving form was non-standardizable, non-fungible, and priced for a market
segment that valued the hand-made character and specific identity of the product.
The survival mechanism was not that hand-looms became cheaper to operate or more
productive; it was that the product occupied a space factory production could not
address equivalently. Equipment redesign is largely irrelevant to this survival
pathway. CERES can improve the economics at the margin by reducing capital barriers
to entry, but the core competitive protection of luxury hand-weaving is differentiation,
not equipment efficiency.

**Nishijin is the one case of industrial technology adoption preserving craft identity.**
The 1872 Lyon mission and Jacquard adoption represent the clearest historical case in
this study of an artisan-trade community adopting industrial technology to preserve its
market position in a reorganized form. The draw-boy was eliminated; the craft survived.
This is a `restructuring` pathway that involved genuine technological change — not
traditional craft practice preserved intact. The lesson for CERES is that
technology-adoption restructuring is a historically documented survival mechanism
for luxury weaving, but it transforms the labor economics and organizational form as
much as it preserves them.

**The fiber-sourcing dependency is universal and unsolved.** No historical weaving form
operated with autonomous fiber supply. Medieval European broadcloth depended on English
staple wool exported through the Calais Staple [Power 1941; Lloyd 1977]. Song luxury
silk required sericulture as a non-separable upstream dependency [Kuhn 1988; Bray 1997].
Nishijin depended on Chinese raw silk through the Nagasaki licensed trade and later
on domestic sericulture hundreds of kilometers distant [Morris-Suzuki 1994]. The
American frontier depended on purchased factory-spun yarn by the 1830s–1840s, having
abandoned home spinning [Tryon 1917; Strasser 1982]. This dependency never resolved
across four cultures and four centuries; it merely shifted form. Any modern CERES
weaving catalog entry that implies fiber autonomy is historically unsupported. The
practical implication for Plan H is direct: even restorable weaving niches require
purchased industrial yarn unless spinning is also restored as a companion trade —
and if spinning is not restored, the weaver is downstream of the same industrial supply
chain that the power loom represents.

**The demand-collapse mechanism for commodity weaving was speed and quality, not merely
price.** The historical record is consistent that factory cloth was not only cheaper
but more uniform and in several respects functionally superior to household-produced
commodity cloth. Strasser's analysis of the American case is explicit: women who could
afford factory cloth adopted it without regret and experienced the transition as
liberation from burdensome labor [Strasser 1982]. The medieval handloom weavers'
60–80% wage collapse documents an analogous competitive dynamic [Bythell 1969].
The implication is that the commodity weaving decline was not a close-run price
competition that better equipment might have reversed; the industrial product dominated
on multiple dimensions simultaneously.

## 4. Verdict

**Overall verdict: mixed.** Weaving did not experience a single, uniform trajectory.
It experienced real `decline` and `demand-collapse` for commodity cloth production
across all four anchor cultures; `regulatory-dissolution` for the guild-town and
imperial-workshop institutional forms; genuine `restructuring` for luxury and specialty
niches that have persisted to the present; and, in the Nishijin case, a `restructuring`
pathway through industrial technology adoption that transformed the trade substantially
while preserving its market position.

These are not ambiguous signals that average out to something in between; they are
documented, distinct trajectories operating on different product segments at different
times across four cultures. The commodity tier went one way; the luxury tier went
another; and the two trajectories have operated independently since the 1840s.

**Confidence: high.** The evidence for decline of commodity hand-weaving is the
strongest and most consistent sub-claim in the weaving verdict, and arguably the
strongest single finding in the CERES research corpus to date. It is documented
quantitatively (Bythell 1969 on handloom wage collapse; Tryon 1917 on household
weaving decline), qualitatively (Strasser 1982 on women's response to factory cloth),
and cross-culturally (all four anchor cultures, no counter-cases). The evidence for
luxury-niche restructuring is well-documented in three of four cultures (northern
Europe, Song China, Tokugawa Japan); the American frontier specialty niche is
documented but with a terminal trajectory that makes it a weaker analog. The fiber-
sourcing falsifier is consistent across all four cultures without exception.

**Confidence is high for commodity decline; moderate for the specific economics
of the restructured luxury niche.** What is well-established is that the luxury niche
exists and has survived. What is less well-established is its current economics —
revenue structure, customer acquisition, capacity to support full-time practitioners —
and how much of its survival reflects genuine economic viability versus cultural-
heritage positioning and subsidized craft institutions. Plan H catalog entries for
luxury hand-weaving should acknowledge that the historical evidence establishes
survival of the niche but does not supply detailed modern economic documentation.

**Lowest-confidence sub-claim:** The extent to which the Nishijin Jacquard adoption
pathway is transferable to other cultural contexts. Nishijin survived partly because
it had a deeply embedded craft identity, an existing luxury consumer base in the
kimono market, and a geographic concentration that supported collective adaptation.
Whether a modern weaving operation could replicate the Nishijin restructuring pathway
without those pre-existing conditions is not answerable from the historical record.
Plan H catalog entries that cite Nishijin as a model should specify which aspects of
the model they are adopting and on what demand evidence.

**On the spec §2 falsifiers:**

- *Demand collapse* holds strongly and universally for commodity cloth hand-weaving.
  This is the clearest partial falsification of the equipment-economics hypothesis
  in the weaving domain: no loom redesign recovers a market that has permanently
  migrated to factory production. This falsifier is load-bearing for Plan H. The
  working hypothesis is not falsified for the luxury-specialty niche, where demand
  persists and the competitive constraint is differentiation, not fundamental demand
  loss — but even there, the binding constraint is not equipment efficiency; it is
  product identity and fiber supply.

- *Regulatory capture* is relevant for the guild-town dissolution (northern Europe)
  and the *shokunin* estate abolition and *nakama* dissolution (Meiji Japan), and
  for the *jianghu* imperial registration system (China). For modern hand-weaving,
  regulatory constraints are not the primary binding constraint identified in this
  verdict.

- *Labor-regime dependency* is documented with unusual clarity for weaving. Medieval
  European broadcloth depended on guild-suppressed journeyman wages and putting-out
  piece rates [Ogilvie 2019; medieval-northern-europe §Labor Regime]. Song silk
  depended on household tax-labor, hereditary artisan registration, and child draw-boy
  labor embedded structurally in the production process [Kuhn 1988; Bray 1997;
  song-china §Labor Regime]. Tokugawa Nishijin depended on indenture apprenticeship
  and child draw-boy operation [Leupp 1992; tokugawa-japan §Labor Regime]. American
  frontier household weaving depended on uncompensated household women's labor
  [Strasser 1982; Ulrich 1990]. Across all four cultures, the cost structure of
  historical weaving incorporated labor forms — unpaid household labor, child labor,
  indenture labor, piece-rate putting-out below market wages — that cannot be
  reproduced under modern labor law. This falsifier constrains cost estimates but
  not functional achievability: a modern loom can operate with market-wage adult labor;
  the cost structure must be adjusted upward accordingly.

- *Supply-chain disappearance* is not an absolute falsifier but is a more binding
  constraint for weaving than for smithing. Bar steel is industrially available;
  artisan-quality weaving fiber (heritage-breed wool, hand-reeled silk, heritage-
  variety linen) requires supply chains that either do not exist at scale or require
  companion trade restoration. The practical implication is that a CERES weaving
  catalog entry sourcing industrial commodity yarn is not falsified on supply-chain
  grounds, but one claiming heritage-fiber authenticity requires a documented
  fiber-supply pathway.

- *Capital formation* is a relevant but moderate constraint for weaving relative to
  smithing. A functional four-shaft floor loom can be acquired for $500–$3,000 for
  an entry-level instrument; a professional Jacquard-equipped loom or a high-quality
  production loom ranges from $5,000–$30,000. These are substantially lower barriers
  than forge capitalization, which reduces the capital constraint's weight in the
  falsifier analysis for weaving specifically. The constraint is real but not
  structurally binding at the scale at which weaving restructuring is plausible.

**The working hypothesis is not falsified for weaving overall, but is category-
constrained.** The equipment-economics hypothesis is most relevant to the restructured
luxury and specialty niches — bespoke cloth, tapestry, custom fiber goods, therapeutic
and educational weaving — where demand persists and the competitive constraint is
economic, not fundamental demand loss. For commodity cloth categories, the hypothesis
is falsified by `demand-collapse`: industrial alternatives dominate on price, quality,
and consistency, and no loom redesign changes this competitive position. CERES's
weaving catalog must reflect this constraint.

Per spec §11.2, a verdict that constrains rather than falsifies the working hypothesis
is a scientific finding, not a null result. The hypothesis survives for the
luxury-specialty domain; its scope is defined.

## 5. Implications for CERES

**Categories plausibly restorable via equipment redesign or improved economics:**

- *Luxury and specialty hand-woven textiles* — tapestry, bespoke cloth, artisan fashion,
  figured silk, complex pattern weaving for ceremonial and high-end consumer markets.
  This segment has structured market demand and is, by design, not competing with
  factory production. The historical evidence across northern Europe, China, and Japan
  shows this as the most durable segment. Modern loom redesign targeting lower capital
  cost, easier pattern setup, and better ergonomics directly addresses the economic
  constraints on new entrants to this segment.

- *Custom and heritage cloth* — handwoven cloth using natural dyes, heritage-breed
  fibers (Harris Tweed model; equivalent American heritage-wool traditions), or
  fiber types not available in industrial supply. This segment commands premium pricing
  because the product identity is inseparable from the production method. The
  supply-chain falsifier applies with specific force here: heritage fiber supply
  must be explicitly addressed in any catalog entry targeting this niche.

- *Therapeutic and educational weaving* — occupational therapy programs, school
  weaving programs, workshop and continuing-education contexts. These are viable
  under the civic lens (spec §2) even if market-lens economics are marginal. Equipment
  redesign targeting accessibility, adjustability, and lower cost-of-entry expands
  the addressable population for this use category. **Civic viability condition:**
  this category's viability depends on institutional demand (therapy programs,
  schools, cooperatives) committing the workshop hours and participant population —
  it is not an assumed demand condition. Plan H entries targeting this segment must
  specify the institutional partner and demand threshold.

- *Repair and alteration services* — darning, patching, reweaving damaged cloth,
  custom tapestry repair. This segment is inherently local and judgment-intensive
  in ways that resist industrial centralization. The historical record does not
  document this as a major economic form, but the demand structure (damaged textiles
  requiring skilled-eye repair) supports a small niche in markets where textile
  quality is high enough to warrant repair. Lower-capital, portable loom designs
  expand the addressable operator population.

**Categories probably not restorable via equipment redesign:**

- *Commodity cloth production* — plain-weave or simple-pattern cloth in cotton,
  linen, or wool produced for general household or apparel use. Industrial alternatives
  dominate on price, quality, and consistency. The historical record shows
  demand-collapse for this segment in all four anchor cultures; there is no documented
  restructuring pathway. No loom redesign changes this competitive position.

- *Everyday utility cloth at volume* — the analog of Song ramie household weaving or
  American linsey-woolsey. The fiber economics, labor economics, and product quality
  comparison all favor industrial production by margins that equipment redesign
  cannot close.

**Falsifier check — does this falsify the working hypothesis for weaving?**

No, for the luxury-specialty domain. Yes, for commodity cloth. Per spec §2 pivot
criteria, a finding that the binding constraint is `demand-collapse` for a specific
sub-segment produces a per-segment pivot, not a trade-wide rejection. Commodity cloth
sub-segments are effectively falsified for equipment redesign. Luxury and specialty
sub-segments are not; for those, the equipment-economics question remains open and is
the correct question for Plan H catalog entries to address.

**The fiber-sourcing falsifier and its implications for Plan H:**

The cross-cultural fiber-dependency finding has a direct implication for catalog
development that does not arise with equal force in smithing: even restorable weaving
niches require purchased industrial yarn unless spinning is also restored as a companion
trade. A Plan H weaving catalog entry that sources industrial commodity yarn is not
historically pure but is operationally defensible — it has a functioning supply chain.
An entry that claims heritage-fiber sourcing without documenting that supply chain is
making an unsupported assumption. An entry that claims full fiber-to-cloth autonomy
(growing fiber, processing, spinning, and weaving) has undertaken to catalog not one
trade but a compound of three or four trades whose interactions require coordinated
analysis. This is the correct framing for the fiber-sourcing dependency: it is not a
reason to abandon weaving catalog development; it is a scope constraint that each
entry must address explicitly.

**Recommendation to Plan H catalog authors:**

Every weaving catalog entry should explicitly state (a) its target product category
(luxury/specialty, heritage/custom, therapeutic/educational, or commodity — with
commodity flagged as non-viable); (b) its fiber supply pathway and its robustness;
and (c) whether it assumes companion-trade spinning or sourced industrial yarn.
Entries targeting luxury or specialty categories are operating within the historically
supported domain. Entries targeting commodity cloth should carry an explicit
`## Known risks` note acknowledging the demand-collapse documented in all four anchor
cultures and stating what specific market condition the entry depends on to overcome
it. Reviewers should use this verdict document to evaluate whether an entry's targeted
product category and fiber supply assumptions are realistic; if an entry implicitly
assumes commodity competitiveness or fiber autonomy without justification, those are
substantive flaws requiring correction before the entry can advance.
