# Baking — Decline-or-Restructuring Verdict

## 1. The Question

Did baking as a local trade actually decline, or did it restructure? The distinction
matters for CERES because the answer determines what the equipment-economics hypothesis
can and cannot address.

If baking principally *declined* — meaning demand for its products vanished — then
redesigning oven equipment cannot restore what was never an equipment problem. A better
wood-fired dome oven does not revive household bread production when commercial bread
from industrial bakeries is both cheaper and more consistent. If baking principally
*restructured* — meaning viable economic forms migrated to different niches without
overall demand loss — then equipment redesign can, in principle, target the restructured
niche and improve its economics.

Baking presents the most complex decline-or-restructuring pattern of any of the three
anchor trades in this research corpus. Three analytically distinct product tiers — commodity
bread, artisan bread, and confection/specialty baking — followed divergent trajectories
across the four anchor cultures. These trajectories do not average into a single answer.
Moreover, the evidence introduces a falsifier for the equipment-economics hypothesis that
is distinct from any raised by the smithing record: the binding constraint on baking
economics may not be the oven at all, but the upstream flour supply chain. This
mill-dependency falsifier has direct implications for how CERES's Plan G (baking catalog)
must be structured and what assumptions it can legitimately make.

## 2. Evidence Per Culture

### 2.1 Medieval Northern Europe

Verdict per canonical vocabulary: **mixed** — `restructuring` for commercial guild
baking under an evolving regulatory framework; `decline` for English artisan bakers
following the displacement of guild and assize structures; `demand-collapse` for
manorial and household baking tied to seigneurial banalité organization.

The medieval northern European record shows a highly regulated commercial baking trade
operating alongside household production, with both segments following separate
trajectories as the industrial period arrived.

Professional urban bakers in English and Flemish towns operated under guild ordinances
and the Assize of Bread — a crown-level price and weight regulation that structured
baker margins throughout the medieval period [medieval-northern-europe §Baker's Place;
Davis 2004]. The Assize's economic function was to maintain baker margins through grain
price volatility by adjusting permitted bread prices; in practice, the system also
capped what bakers could earn, making labor cost management — through household, apprentice,
and journeyman labor — the primary adjustment variable [medieval-northern-europe
§Labor Regime]. This regulatory framework persisted until formal repeal in 1836
[CITATION-NEEDED: Assize of Bread repeal, 6 & 7 Will. IV c. 37; medieval-northern-europe
§Decline or Restructuring], by which point enforcement had already become irregular.

The structural break for the commercial artisan baker was not guild dissolution but
industrial milling. The introduction of roller-milling technology in the 1870s–1900s
produced finely graded, stable white flour at industrial volume and consistency, which
simultaneously removed the local mill-dependency constraint and enabled factory bakeries
to operate at scales and with consistency that single-oven artisan shops could not match
[medieval-northern-europe §Decline or Restructuring; Kaplan 2006]. In England, factory
bread production consolidated rapidly from the 1880s onward [CITATION-NEEDED: English
factory bread industry emergence and market share, 1880s–1920s]; the professional artisan
baker's economic role was substantially eroded by price competition.

The divergence between England and France is the most significant cross-national finding
from this culture. French *boulangeries* survived into the twentieth century through a
combination of consumer demand for *pain de tradition* and regulatory protections that
constrained factory substitution [Kaplan 2006; medieval-northern-europe §Decline or
Restructuring]. Germany maintained analogous artisan-bakery density [CITATION-NEEDED:
German artisan bakery density, 20th-century comparison with England]. This divergence
means that the "decline" verdict for English artisan baking cannot be generalized to
continental Europe without institutional qualification: the English outcome was not the
only possible industrial-period trajectory.

Manorial and household baking — organized around seigneurial communal ovens (*fours
banaux*) in France and analogous manorial-oven obligations in England — was eliminated
as an institutional form when the *banalité* system was abolished by the French Revolution
(1789) and manorial oven obligations decayed in England [medieval-northern-europe
§Decline or Restructuring]. The institutional form dissolved; household baking itself
continued but progressively lost economic necessity as commercial bread became accessible.

**The mill as binding constraint.** The medieval northern European record is explicit
that professional bakers did not mill their own grain and were upstream-dependent on
a small number of mill operators whose capacity, reliability, and pricing structured
baking operations [medieval-northern-europe §Supply Chain; Langdon 2004]. This
dependency was not resolved by industrial milling; it was transformed — local mill
dependency was replaced by industrial flour supply dependency. The character of the
upstream binding constraint changed; the existence of the constraint did not.

### 2.2 Song-Era China

Verdict per canonical vocabulary: **continuous** — no meaningful structural collapse
or guild-dissolution event comparable to the European case; `upstream-supply-restructuring`
in the industrial period when treaty-port flour mills entered the Chinese market.

The Song commercial grain-food trade was structurally unlike the European baker's trade
from the outset. It was organized not around guild-regulated craftsmen with chartered
market rights but around commercial street vendors operating under merchant licensing —
a dense, competitive urban food economy with low capital barriers to entry, no
territorial monopoly, and no formal apprenticeship hierarchy [song-china §Vendor's Place].
This structural difference is not incidental; it is the reason the Song trade did not
experience the institutional erosion that produced the European guild decline narrative.
There was no equivalent institution to dissolve.

Song cities, led by Kaifeng and Hangzhou, supported a wide range of grain-food vendors
producing steamed wheat breads (*mantou*, *baozi*), baked flatbreads (*shaobing*),
noodles, and rice-based preparations, documented in contemporary memoirs including Meng
Yuanlao's *Dongjing Menghua Lu* [song-china §Vendor's Place; song-china §Products].
The primary preparation method was steaming, not oven-baking — the dominant equipment
was a tiered bamboo or clay steamer (*zheng*) with a lower capital threshold than any
masonry oven, which contributed to the competitive density of the market [song-china
§Equipment].

The Mongol conquest (1279) disrupted the Song political economy but did not destroy the
commercial food-vendor trade; Yuan and Ming commercial cities maintained grain-food vendor
markets substantially continuous with Song practice [song-china §Continuity and
Non-Decline]. The structural break comparable to European industrial baking arrived only
in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries through Shanghai and the treaty ports,
where foreign flour mills undercut traditional stone-milled flour on price
[CITATION-NEEDED: industrial flour milling in late Qing/Republican China; song-china
§Industrial Milling as Structural Break]. Crucially, this break affected upstream supply,
not vendor operations: cheaper flour lowered input costs and potentially expanded the
market rather than displacing the preparation trade. The labor-and-skill content of
steaming and flatbread production was not displaced by changes in flour sourcing.

**The mill-dependency falsifier from the Song record.** Song commercial food vendors were
not grain-supply autonomous. Both steamed-bread vendors and shaobing vendors depended on
commercial milled flour as the primary input [song-china §Supply Chain]. The minimum
viable supply chain for any Song-style grain-food operation explicitly included commercial
grain milling as a prerequisite; no historical precedent exists for a small-scale urban
food operation that also integrated grain milling. This is not a Song-specific anomaly;
it is a documented structural feature of commercial grain-food production in the most
continuous and commercially evolved instance in this corpus.

For CERES purposes, the Song case confirms baseline commercial viability of the grain-food
trade across more than a millennium and documents that the equilibrium form was a
competitive vendor market, not a protected craft-shop economy. At small settlement scale,
the density-dependent Song urban vendor model does not transfer directly; the relevant
precedent for a CERES-context settlement is the household or semi-commercial form.

### 2.3 Tokugawa Japan

Verdict per canonical vocabulary: **mixed** — `restructuring` for the elite *goyo
kashi-ya* tier and commercial mid-market wagashi operations, which differentiated into
premium-cultural positioning as industrial confectionery captured the mass-market tier;
`decline` for domestic household wagashi production (progressively displaced by
purchased commercial product); `new trade entry` for Western-style bread baking
(*pan-ya*), which was not a transformation of the wagashi trade but a parallel structural
addition.

The Tokugawa baking trade was not a bread-baking trade at all in the European sense.
Japan's food staple was rice; the primary grain-based specialty-food production trade
was **wagashi** — traditional confections including steamed buns (*manjū*), pounded
rice cake (*mochi*), bean-paste jellies (*yokan*), and hand-formed seasonal confections
(*nerikiri*) [tokugawa-japan §Period and Geography]. This structural difference from the
European case must be stated at the outset: the Japanese analog of baking's economic
niche was occupied by a confectionery trade, not a bread trade.

The trade divided between elite court and domain confectioners (*goyo kashi-ya*) who
supplied ceremonial and gift-economy demand at premium prices, and commercial urban
confectioners serving the townspeople (*chonin*) market in Edo, Osaka, and castle towns
[tokugawa-japan §Confectioner's Place]. The tea ceremony (*chado*) sustained elite demand
for high-quality wagashi as ritual accompaniments and was the mechanism by which elite
patrons paid premium prices; gift-economy exchange (*zotozai*) created recurring
institutional demand insulated from normal consumer-market competition [tokugawa-japan
§Gift-Giving Economy]. Neither mechanism required or responded to oven-equipment design;
the binding constraint on the premium tier was cultural identity and patron relationships,
not production economics.

The Meiji Restoration (1868) introduced Western food culture and *pan-ya* bread bakeries,
which penetrated Japanese urban markets initially from Yokohama and Kobe, sustained in
part by Meiji Army bread-ration procurement [CITATION-NEEDED: Meiji military bread
procurement; tokugawa-japan §Meiji Opening; Cwiertka 2006]. Western-style baking was a
new trade entering Japan, not a transformation of wagashi; the two trades developed in
parallel and remain structurally distinct. Meiji industrialization also lowered sugar
costs dramatically through colonial Taiwan sugar production after 1895, which paradoxically
expanded the wagashi market rather than contracting it: high-sugar wagashi previously
limited by sugar costs became accessible to a broader consumer base [tokugawa-japan
§Sugar Democratization].

Industrial confectionery (Morinaga, founded 1899; Glico, founded 1921) subsequently
captured the mass confection market, pushing surviving wagashi commercial operations
to differentiate into premium-cultural positioning. Named Kyoto and regional shops
leveraged historical lineages, tea ceremony associations, and seasonal product repertoires
as market assets [tokugawa-japan §Wagashi as Premium Confection]. This is `restructuring`
via premium segmentation, not economic survival on commodity-production terms.

The Tokugawa wagashi case is the clearest documented instance in this corpus of specialty
baking surviving via non-price mechanisms: cultural identity, named lineage, seasonal
uniqueness, and institutional patron relationships. The mechanism that preserved wagashi
— consumer willingness to pay for cultural goods — requires no equipment redesign to
sustain; it requires the cultural asset and a consumer market that values it.

**Sugar as the binding constraint.** The Tokugawa record introduces a second instance
of an upstream input, not the oven, as the binding constraint on baking economics. Sugar
was expensive, imported, and at times officially controlled throughout the Tokugawa period
[tokugawa-japan §Sugar: The Binding Constraint]. The structural lesson parallels the
mill-dependency lesson from the bread-baking cultures: in both cases, the oven or steamer
was not the bottleneck in production economics; an upstream input was.

### 2.4 American Frontier

Verdict per canonical vocabulary: **decline** (household baking), driven by
`demand-collapse` combined with `labor-reallocation`; **partial restructuring then
decline** (small commercial baking); **restructuring** into a specialty niche for
artisan bread in the contemporary period.

The American frontier baking record documents the cleanest demand-collapse sequence
in the corpus for household baking. Three stages are documented.

**Stage 1 (1840s–1870s): Rationalization, not displacement.** The adoption of commercial
saleratus and baking powder regularized quick-bread (biscuit) production and reduced the
skill barrier for daily bread-equivalent production, reducing dependence on maintained
sourdough starters [american-frontier §Decline or Restructuring, Stage 1]. This is a
restructuring of household practice, not a decline.

**Stage 2 (1868–1890s): Commercial yeast regularizes leavened bread.** The introduction
of Fleischmann's compressed yeast in 1868 [CITATION-NEEDED: Fleischmann's commercial
introduction date; american-frontier §Decline or Restructuring, Stage 2; Levenstein 1988]
removed the primary skill and management requirement of frontier household baking
(starter maintenance) and made yeast-bread baking more accessible to both household and
commercial bakers.

**Stage 3 (1880s–1920s): Commercial bread displaces household baking.** Factory bread
was more consistent and cheaper than household-baked bread once industrial milling and
rail distribution reached scale. Women's labor — the primary input for household baking —
had increasing alternative employment value as frontier urbanization expanded paid-work
opportunities [american-frontier §Decline or Restructuring, Stage 3; Strasser 1982;
Cowan 1983]. The displacement was `demand-collapse` in the canonical sense: the demand
for household-baked bread did not migrate to a different form of household bread production;
it migrated to commercial purchase. The commercial product was experienced by most
households as superior in consistency and lower in total cost (including women's labor
time), not as a degraded substitute [american-frontier §Decline or Restructuring].

**Stage 4 (1890s–1920s): Industrial scale displaces small commercial bakeries.** Small
frontier-town commercial bakeries were subsequently displaced by large urban bakeries
using mechanical mixers, continuous-process ovens, and rail distribution [american-frontier
§Decline or Restructuring, Stage 4]. A small specialty bakery niche survived as
`restructuring` into premium price-point retail, but this is not a continuation of the
frontier commercial baker's economic position.

A structurally important feature of the American frontier record is that household baking
was *already downstream of industrial production from the start* — commercial flour milling
was already industrial in character before the trans-Appalachian frontier was settled;
Oliver Evans's automated mill design had been widely adopted in the middle Atlantic states
by the early 1800s [american-frontier §Period and Geography; CITATION-NEEDED: Oliver Evans
mill adoption chronology; Kuhlmann 1929]. The frontier household baker was not a
self-sufficient craft operator but a last-mile processing unit on an industrial supply chain.
This directly engages the mill-dependency falsifier: the American case confirms that
even in a household baking context that appeared self-sufficient, the binding constraint
was the upstream flour supply chain, not the oven.

## 3. Cross-Cultural Pattern

Four consistent patterns emerge from the evidence across all four anchor cultures.

**Commodity bread baking declined universally in market economies.** Household
bread production — the dominant mode in medieval Europe and the American frontier —
was displaced in every culture that developed industrial flour milling and commercial
bread distribution. The mechanism was consistent: factory or industrial-scale bread
production combined with the rising opportunity cost of women's household labor time
made commercial bread economically superior for most households regardless of national
context. This is the strongest, most uniform finding from the cross-cultural record.
Plan G catalog entries targeting commodity bread production at household scale face
an adverse demand trajectory documented in every relevant culture.

**Artisan bread declined then revived — the key non-linear trajectory in this corpus.**
The artisan bread case (sourdough, heritage grains, premium bread) is uniquely
non-monotone: demand collapsed from artisan bakeries under industrial competition in
the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, then partially revived in developed
economies from the late twentieth century onward as consumer preference for premium,
craft-identified food products grew [Kaplan 2006; medieval-northern-europe §Decline
or Restructuring]. French *boulangeries* never experienced the full English-style
collapse, sustained by regulatory protection and consumer preference for *pain de tradition*.
Contemporary sourdough and heritage-grain bread markets in developed economies represent
genuine demand that industrial bread does not satisfy — a restructuring back toward
craft form after a period of displacement. This makes artisan bread the most
CERES-relevant trajectory in the corpus: it is the category where equipment economics
can plausibly make a real difference to whether a small-scale bakery is viable.

**Specialty and confection baking survived universally via premium segmentation.**
Wagashi in Japan (tokugawa-japan §Wagashi as Premium Confection), *pain de tradition*
and premium pastry in France and Germany (medieval-northern-europe §Decline or
Restructuring), and contemporary artisan confectionery in all four cultural traditions
all demonstrate `restructuring` via the same mechanism: premium price-point segmentation
leveraging hand-production, cultural identity, and product uniqueness to resist
industrial substitution. The mechanism requires no equipment redesign; it requires
a consumer market willing to pay premium prices for cultural goods. Where that market
exists, specialty baking is structurally durable. Where it does not exist — in lower-
income consumer contexts or in the absence of food-culture premium markets — the
mechanism cannot operate.

**The mill-dependency falsifier applies to all four cultures without exception.**
No historical baking form in this corpus was autonomous from an upstream grain-milling
supply chain. Medieval European professional bakers were upstream-dependent on seigneurial
or commercial mills [medieval-northern-europe §Supply Chain; Langdon 2004]. Song
commercial food vendors depended on commercial urban milling as a prerequisite input
[song-china §Commercial Milling]. Tokugawa wagashi confectioners required commercial
rice and wheat-flour supply, though flour milling was not the binding constraint
for rice-based confections (sugar was). American frontier household bakers were
downstream of industrial commercial milling from the beginning of the period
[american-frontier §Supply Chain; Kuhlmann 1929]. The consistency is total: no
small-scale commercial baking form operated without a milling supply chain. This
is a structural finding, not a peripheral detail. A CERES catalog entry that models
baking economics as beginning with flour — taking commercial flour as a given input —
is historically supported. An entry that models baking as beginning with grain and
assumes the baker mills their own flour has no historical precedent at commercial scale.

**The labor-regime falsifier engages baking more acutely than smithing.** Household
baking in particular rested on women's unpaid labor as the primary production input
in medieval Europe, Tokugawa Japan's domestic confectionery sector, and the American
frontier. This labor was not costed, not compensated at market wages, and not
recorded in household accounts [medieval-northern-europe §Labor Regime; american-frontier
§Labor Regime; Strasser 1982; Hanawalt 1986; Bennett 1996]. Commercial baking
additionally relied on indenture-basis apprentice labor (European guilds; Tokugawa
*detchi-boko*) and wage-capped journeyman labor. None of these cost structures are
reproducible under modern labor law. For baking more than any other trade, historical
per-unit economics are structurally incomparable to modern equivalents; the labor
adjustment required to produce modern cost estimates is large.

## 4. Verdict

**Overall verdict: mixed.** Baking did not experience a single, uniform trajectory.
It experienced genuine `demand-collapse` in household bread production in industrialized
cultures, genuine `restructuring` in artisan bread (first decline then revival),
genuine `restructuring` via premium segmentation in specialty and confectionery tiers,
and `continuous` commercial viability for the Song-type street-vendor form throughout
the period. These are not ambiguous signals that average out to something in between;
they are documented, distinct trajectories operating on different product tiers in
different institutional contexts.

**Confidence: moderate.** The evidence for decline of household commodity baking is
strong and consistent across all four cultures — this is the highest-confidence sub-claim.
The evidence for the artisan bread revival trajectory is solid for France (Kaplan 2006)
and is plausibly general to developed-economy premium food markets, but the comparative
data for Germany, the United States, and other markets are less fully sourced in this
corpus [CITATION-NEEDED: German artisan bakery density, 20th-century comparison;
american-frontier §Decline or Restructuring]. The wagashi restructuring trajectory is
well-supported from a narrative standpoint but lacks economic quantification; the claims
about premium market positioning rest on qualitative evidence rather than price or
market-share data [tokugawa-japan §Wagashi as Premium Confection; CITATION-NEEDED:
wagashi premium market data, post-Meiji].

**Lowest-confidence sub-claim:** The artisan bread revival as a self-sustaining economic
trajectory distinct from premium-trend volatility is the lowest-confidence claim. The
evidence establishes that demand exists and that named artisan bakeries survive; it does
not establish that small-scale artisan bakeries achieve consistent profitability under
modern labor costs or that the revival-trend demand is structurally durable rather than
fashion-contingent. Plan G entries targeting artisan bread should treat the demand
recovery as real but document their assumptions about the premium market's size and
durability in the local context, not assume national trends apply uniformly.

**On the mill-dependency falsifier:** The evidence across all four cultures directly
and unambiguously engages this falsifier. The mill — or, equivalently, the commercial
flour supply chain — is the binding upstream constraint for any small-scale baking
operation. The equipment-economics hypothesis for baking addresses the oven. But the
oven is not the binding constraint identified in the historical record: the mill is.

This partial falsification does not eliminate the equipment-economics hypothesis for
baking; it reframes it. The question is not "does a better oven make baking more viable?"
but rather "given that commercial flour is available as an input, does oven economics
make artisan bread baking or specialty baking viable?" That is a more limited question
with a more tractable answer. For developed-economy contexts where commercial flour supply
is reliable, the oven-economics question is real and relevant. For contexts where commercial
flour supply is uncertain or unavailable, the binding constraint is the milling supply chain,
and oven redesign addresses the wrong problem.

**On the spec §2 falsifiers:**

- *Demand collapse* holds strongly for household commodity bread baking in all cultures
  that underwent industrialization. It does not hold for artisan bread or specialty
  confection tiers, where demand persists or has revived. Equipment redesign targeting
  the commodity-household-baking tier is addressing a segment where demand has collapsed
  and will not recover from equipment improvements. Equipment redesign targeting artisan
  bread or specialty confection is addressing a segment with genuine, documented demand.

- *Regulatory capture* is relevant to the historical guild and assize structures in
  medieval Europe (dissolved) and to the Tokugawa *shokunin* estate (dissolved at Meiji).
  For modern artisan baking, regulatory constraints are lighter — food safety permitting
  and commercial kitchen requirements are the primary regulatory layer — and are not the
  primary binding constraint identified in this verdict.

- *Labor-regime dependency* is documented in all four cultures and is especially acute
  for baking. Historical per-unit baking economics incorporated unpaid household labor
  as the dominant cost component; the modern equivalent must replace this with market-wage
  labor, substantially increasing per-unit costs [REQUIREMENTS.md stub; medieval-northern-europe
  §Labor Regime; american-frontier §Labor Regime]. This falsifier constrains cost
  estimates, not functional achievability. Modern bakeries exist and operate profitably
  at market wages; the cost structure must be adjusted upward accordingly.

- *Supply-chain disappearance* is the key falsifier for baking, operating differently
  than in smithing. Commercial flour is industrially available worldwide; the flour
  supply chain has not disappeared. Unlike the forge (where commercial steel is the
  analogous input), the baking supply chain is fully intact at the commercial-flour level.
  The falsifier operates only in the specific case of a catalog entry that assumes the
  baker mills their own grain: that assumption is historically unsupported and must be
  flagged as an unverified design premise. Any entry assuming vertical integration from
  grain to loaf without commercial flour supply is making a claim with no historical
  precedent.

- *Capital formation* — a fifth constraint — is relevant to artisan bakery start-up
  specifically. A modern wood-fired or gas-fired dome oven of adequate thermal mass runs
  to several thousand dollars for equipment and installation; a commercial kitchen buildout
  including equipment runs substantially higher. Historical equivalent capital was
  accumulated through guild admission, household savings, or patron investment under
  different debt-service structures. A Plan G entry for an artisan bakery should address
  the capitalization pathway, not assume it.

**The working hypothesis is not falsified for baking overall, but is tier-constrained
and reframed by the mill-dependency finding.** The hypothesis is most relevant to artisan
bread and specialty confection tiers, where demand persists, the competitive constraint
is economic, and equipment redesign can plausibly affect margin. For commodity household
baking, the hypothesis does not apply because demand has migrated permanently to commercial
production. The mill-dependency finding reframes the equipment-economics question: oven
design matters within the constraint that commercial flour supply is available; addressing
oven economics without addressing flour supply is an incomplete analysis.

Per spec §11.2, a verdict that constrains rather than falsifies the working hypothesis
is a scientific finding, not a null result. The hypothesis survives; its scope is defined.

## 5. Implications for CERES

**Categories plausibly restorable or viable via equipment redesign (given commercial
flour availability):**

- *Artisan bread production* — sourdough, heritage-grain, and premium loaf bread for
  local urban or semi-urban markets with food-culture premium demand. This segment has
  documented demand recovery in developed economies and is location-bound in ways that
  resist full centralization: freshness, local character, and the ability to serve
  specific neighborhood demand are real competitive advantages. Oven design that lowers
  capital cost, reduces fuel consumption, and extends batch-baking efficiency directly
  addresses the economics of this segment. The historical precedent in France — the
  *boulangerie* form that sustained itself through the industrial period — is the
  most relevant model.

- *Specialty and confection baking* — wagashi-type products, European pastry, heritage
  regional confections. This segment commands premium pricing because it cannot be
  adequately replaced by standardized industrial products; its economic viability rests
  on cultural identity and willingness to pay rather than on price competition. Equipment
  redesign is less critical here than for bread baking, but lower-cost, smaller-footprint
  oven and steaming equipment expands the addressable population of practitioners. The
  Tokugawa wagashi and French patisserie cases are the historical analogs.

- *Community or civic bakery contexts* — shared-oven arrangements serving a defined
  geographic community, potentially modeled on the communal oven form documented in
  medieval Europe and on contemporary community-oven revival projects. These are viable
  under the civic lens (spec §2) even if market-lens economics are marginal. **Civic
  viability condition:** this category's viability depends on a community institution
  committing the facility and organizational structure; an entry that describes civic
  viability without specifying the demand source and governance structure is making an
  unsupported assumption. Plan G entries for this segment must specify organizational
  prerequisites.

- *Small-scale commercial baking at artisan premium* — the contemporary analog of the
  French *boulangerie*, a market-facing small business producing premium bread and pastry
  at local scale. The Song commercial vendor form — highly competitive, low capital barrier,
  serving dense urban demand — is a less applicable model for CERES-scale contexts; the
  French artisan model is the closer analog.

**Categories probably not restorable via equipment redesign:**

- *Commodity household bread baking as primary subsistence production.* The demand
  trajectory is a permanent demand-collapse: industrial bread is cheaper, more consistent,
  and lower in effective household cost (including labor) than household-baked bread at
  comparable scale. No equipment redesign reverses this. Catalog entries targeting
  household bread-making as subsistence economics should treat this as a recreation or
  food-culture activity, not a subsistence-economics claim.

- *Commodity commercial bread production competing with industrial bakers on price.*
  The capital economics and scale requirements of industrial bread production are not
  addressable by the small-scale oven designs within CERES's scope. This segment requires
  industrial-scale equipment, automated mixing and forming, and distribution infrastructure.
  Historical small commercial bakeries competed on freshness, local preference, and
  premium character; price competition with factory bread is not a viable strategy in
  any of the four anchor cultures.

**The mill-dependency requirement for Plan G:**

Every Plan G baking catalog entry must explicitly state its flour sourcing assumption.
An entry that begins with commercial flour as a given input — the historically supported
assumption — may proceed to analyze oven economics and market viability on that basis.
An entry that proposes to integrate milling with baking, or that assumes flour will be
locally sourced from non-commercial milling, is proposing a more capital-intensive and
operationally complex operation than any historical precedent supports at small scale,
and must acknowledge that the binding constraint is the milling supply chain, not the
oven. Reviewers should use this verdict to check whether any Plan G entry's flour-sourcing
assumption is stated explicitly and is consistent with the local context; an entry that
implicitly assumes self-sufficient grain-to-loaf operation without addressing milling
is making an unsupported design premise.

**Falsifier check — does this falsify the working hypothesis for baking?**

Partially, and on specific sub-claims. Equipment redesign cannot restore commodity
household baking: the demand has migrated permanently. Equipment redesign cannot make
small-scale commodity bread price-competitive with industrial production. For these
sub-segments, the hypothesis is effectively falsified. For artisan bread and specialty
confection, the hypothesis is not falsified: the equipment-economics question is real
and relevant. The mill-dependency finding does not falsify the hypothesis but reframes
its scope — oven economics matters within the constraint that flour supply is available.
Per spec §2 pivot criteria, this produces per-segment pivots, not a trade-wide rejection.

**Recommendation to Plan G catalog authors:**

Every baking catalog entry should explicitly state (1) its target product tier
(commodity, artisan, specialty/confection); (2) its flour-sourcing assumption (commercial
flour as input, or mill-integrated operation); and (3) its market-demand basis (premium
food-culture demand, civic/institutional, subsistence). Entries targeting artisan bread
or specialty confection at premium price points are operating within the historically
supported domain where the equipment-economics hypothesis applies. Entries targeting
commodity bread production should carry an explicit `## Known risks` note acknowledging
the adverse demand trajectory and stating what market condition the entry depends on.
Entries proposing mill-integrated baking operations must address milling as the primary
capital and operational challenge, not the oven.

Full bibliography is deferred to `research/trades/baking/SOURCES.md` (Task 8).

**Inherited — verified in prior chapters:**

1. Kaplan, Steven L. 1984. *Provisioning Paris: Merchants and Millers in the Grain and
   Flour Trade during the Eighteenth Century*. Cornell University Press. [French grain
   and bread supply chain, guild regulation of bakers.]
2. Kaplan, Steven L. 2006. *Good Bread Is Back: A Contemporary History of French Bread,
   the Way It Is Made, and the People Who Make It*. Duke University Press. [French
   artisan baking survival, factory bread, regulatory protection, artisan bread revival —
   the primary source for the artisan-bread-revival trajectory.]
3. Dyer, Christopher. 2002. *Making a Living in the Middle Ages: The People of Britain,
   850–1520*. Yale University Press. [Medieval English economy, peasant diet, grain types.]
4. Carlin, Martha. 1998. "'Fast Food' and Urban Living Standards in Medieval England."
   In *Food and Eating in Medieval Europe*, ed. Carlin and Rosenthal. Hambledon Press.
   [Medieval English professional food production, bakers, urban food trades.]
5. Jordan, William Chester. 1996. *The Great Famine: Northern Europe in the Early
   Fourteenth Century*. Princeton University Press. [Grain market vulnerability,
   supply-chain stress.]
6. Bennett, Judith M. 1996. *Ale, Beer, and Brewsters in England: Women's Work in a
   Changing World, 1300–1600*. Oxford University Press. [Women's labor in ale and bread,
   barm-as-leavening.]
7. Hanawalt, Barbara A. 1986. *The Ties That Bound: Peasant Families in Medieval England*.
   Oxford University Press. [Household labor, women's economic roles.]
8. Davis, James. 2004. "Baking for the Common Good: A Reassessment of the Assize of
   Bread in Medieval England." *Economic History Review* 57(3): 465–502. [Assize of
   Bread regulation and economics — principal specialized study.]
9. Langdon, John. 2004. *Mills in the Medieval Economy: England, 1300–1540*. Oxford
   University Press. [Mill ownership, capacity, and dependency structure — load-bearing
   for mill-as-binding-constraint argument.]
10. Ogilvie, Sheilagh. 2019. *The European Guilds: An Economic Analysis*. Princeton
    University Press. [Guild economic functions, apprenticeship.]
11. Epstein, S.R. 1998. "Craft guilds, apprenticeship, and technological change in
    preindustrial Europe." *Journal of Economic History* 58(3): 684–713. [Guild economics,
    institutional erosion.]
12. Anderson, E.N. 1988. *The Food of China*. Yale University Press. [Commercial food
    markets, Song urban food economy.]
13. Bray, Francesca. 1994. *Science and Civilisation in China*, vol. VI, pt. II:
    Agriculture. Cambridge University Press. [Rice and wheat agricultural zones,
    grain processing.]
14. Huang, H.T. 2000. *Science and Civilisation in China*, vol. VI, pt. V: Fermentations
    and Food Science. Cambridge University Press. [Grain-food fermentation, shaobing,
    steaming equipment — primary technical reference for Chinese grain-food preparation.]
15. Elvin, Mark. 1973. *The Pattern of the Chinese Past*. Stanford University Press.
    [Song economic history, granary system.]
16. von Glahn, Richard. 2016. *The Economic History of China: From Antiquity to the
    Nineteenth Century*. Cambridge University Press. [Comprehensive Song economic history;
    verification source for economic claims.]
17. Rath, Eric C. 2010. *Food and Fantasy in Early Modern Japan*. University of California
    Press. [Tokugawa food culture, wagashi social function, tea ceremony and confectionery,
    elite patronage — primary English-language source for Tokugawa baking.]
18. Cwiertka, Katarzyna J. 2006. *Modern Japanese Cuisine: Food, Power and National
    Identity*. Reaktion Books. [Meiji-period food transitions, sugar trade, Western bakery
    entry, industrial confectionery.]
19. Hanley, Susan B. 1997. *Everyday Things in Premodern Japan*. University of California
    Press. [Material standards, household consumption patterns.]
20. Leupp, Gary P. 1992. *Servants, Shophands, and Laborers in the Cities of Tokugawa
    Japan*. Princeton University Press. [*Detchi-boko* apprenticeship, *shokunin* estate.]
21. Strasser, Susan. 1982. *Never Done: A History of American Housework*. Pantheon.
    [Primary reference for household labor division, baking as women's work, time economics
    of household baking — multiple load-bearing claims in the American frontier section.]
22. Cowan, Ruth Schwartz. 1983. *More Work for Mother: The Ironies of Household Technology
    from the Open Hearth to the Microwave*. Basic Books. [Cast-iron stove adoption,
    domestic technology and labor.]
23. Levenstein, Harvey. 1988. *Revolution at the Table: The Transformation of the American
    Diet*. Oxford University Press. [Commercial yeast adoption, commercial bread displacement.]
24. Cronon, William. 1991. *Nature's Metropolis: Chicago and the Great West*. Norton.
    [Grain processing, railroad distribution, Minneapolis flour milling — load-bearing for
    industrial-flour-supply argument in the American frontier section.]
